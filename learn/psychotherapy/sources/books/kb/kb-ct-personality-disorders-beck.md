# KB: Cognitive Therapy of Personality Disorders
> Aaron T. Beck, Arthur Freeman, Denise D. Davis & Associates | 2nd Edition, 2004 (Guilford Press)

## Core Framework

### How CT-PD Differs from Standard CBT
1. **Duration**: Much longer treatment required -- personality schemas are "structuralized" into normal cognitive organization, unlike the more plastic beliefs of Axis I disorders
2. **Target**: Focus shifts from automatic thoughts to underlying schemas (core beliefs), conditional assumptions, and compensatory strategies
3. **Relationship**: Therapeutic relationship requires far more attention; therapist becomes role model, friendly advisor
4. **Affect**: Cognitive change requires a certain level of affective experience -- experiential techniques (imagery, reliving childhood events) are essential, not optional
5. **Tripartite approach**: Must address cognitive, behavioral, AND affective domains simultaneously; no single approach suffices
6. **Resistance as data**: Noncollaboration is "grist for the mill" and reveals operative schemas
7. **Schema operations**: Three levels of schema change -- restructuring (tearing down and rebuilding), modification (renovation), and reinterpretation (understanding in more functional terms)

### Beck's Schema Concept
- **Schemas** are stable structures that select and synthesize incoming data; they assign meaning to events
- Properties: breadth (narrow to broad), flexibility/rigidity, density (prominence), valence (degree of activation from latent to hypervalent)
- In PDs, maladaptive schemas operate continuously in everyday information processing (unlike Axis I where they activate only during episodes)
- Five schema types: cognitive (interpretation), affective (feelings), motivational (wishes), instrumental (action preparation), control (self-monitoring/inhibition)
- **Hypervalent schemas** have low activation threshold, are prepotent (supersede more adaptive schemas), and actively inhibit competing schemas
- Personality = relatively stable organization of systems and modes; modes = coordinated activation of cognitive, affective, motivational, and behavioral schemas

### The Cognitive Shift
- Transition from personality disorder to Axis I involves progressive narrowing and intensifying of dysfunctional beliefs
- PD baseline beliefs ("I might be undesirable") become anxiety beliefs ("I could be undesirable") and then depressive certainties ("I am undesirable")
- Axis I beliefs are more plastic and amenable to change; PD beliefs are more structuralized and resistant

### Evolutionary Framework
- Personality strategies can be traced to evolutionary prototypes (predation, attachment, display, withdrawal)
- These strategies become maladaptive when inflexible, overgeneralized, and imperative
- The genetically determined prototypes interact with environmental influences to produce personality patterns

## Chapter Digest

### Chapter 1: Overview of Cognitive Therapy of Personality Disorders (Freeman & Pretzer)
Outlines the cognitive-behavioral approach to PDs. Heuristic signs of Axis II include: lifelong patterns ("I've always been this way"), persistent noncompliance, therapy hitting a wall, unawareness of effect on others, lip service without follow-through, and ego-syntonic personality problems. Reviews the evidence base: PBQ validates disorder-specific belief sets for avoidant, dependent, OCPD, narcissistic, and paranoid PDs. DBT shows significant reduction in parasuicidality and dropout in BPD. Comorbid PDs decrease Axis I treatment efficacy but those who persist in treatment can improve. Key finding: it is maladaptive avoidant and paranoid *beliefs* (not PD diagnosis per se) that predict poor CBT outcome for depression. (250 words)

### Chapter 2: Theory of Personality Disorders (Beck)
The theoretical backbone. Traces personality from evolutionary strategies through information processing to schema activation and behavior. Presents Table 2.1 (basic beliefs and strategies for 9 PDs) and Table 2.2 (overdeveloped/underdeveloped strategies). Introduces the concept of cognitive profiles: each PD has a characteristic view of self, view of others, core beliefs, conditional beliefs, instrumental beliefs, main threats, strategies, and affects. Elaborates the information processing sequence: stimulus -> cognitive schema (interpretation) -> affective schema (feeling) -> motivational schema (desire) -> action schema (mobilization) -> control schema (inhibition/permission). Thinking styles: histrionics are global/impressionistic ("miss the trees for the forest"), OCPD focuses excessively on details ("miss the forest for the trees"), avoidant uses "cognitive avoidance" to dampen unpleasant thoughts. BPD and schizotypal not included in the main profile table as they lack a single idiosyncratic belief-strategy set. (180 words)

### Chapter 3: Assessment of Personality Disorders (Butler)
Reviews conceptual/methodological issues. Dimensional approaches show better reliability than categorical. Key instruments: PBQ (9 scales, 126 items, good discriminant validity), Schema Questionnaire (SQ/SQ-SF, 15-16 EMSs, relatively stable even when Axis I symptoms improve), SCID-II, MCMI-III. PBQ profiles provide idiographic patient maps superior to categorical diagnosis alone. The PBQ Avoidant and Paranoid scales predict poor CBT outcome for depression. For BPD, 14 empirically derived beliefs discriminate BPD from other PDs. Young's schema modes (vulnerable child, angry child, punitive parent, detached protector, healthy adult) and coping styles (overcompensation, surrender, avoidance) are described. LEAD standard (Longitudinal, Expert, All Data) recommended over single-assessment diagnosis. (140 words)

### Chapter 4: General Principles and Specialized Techniques (Beck & Freeman)
Case conceptualization is central -- formulate early, share with patient, modify as new data emerge. Distinguishes Axis I mode (plastic, responsive to treatment) from Axis II mode (structuralized, resistant). Lists 10 cognitive techniques: guided discovery, idiosyncratic meaning search, distortion labeling, collaborative empiricism, examining others' behavior, scaling, reattribution, deliberate exaggeration, advantages/disadvantages, decatastrophizing. The "downward arrow" technique accesses core schemas by probing progressively deeper meanings until affect is aroused. Three options for schema change: restructuring (urban renewal), modification (renovation), reinterpretation (functional reframing). Schema diaries: adequacy logs, predictive diaries, old vs. new schema analysis. Behavioral techniques: activity scheduling, mastery/pleasure, rehearsal/modeling, relaxation, in vivo exposure, graded task assignment. Imagery and reliving childhood experiences are essential experiential tools for modifying "hot schemas." (160 words)

### Chapter 5: The Cognitive Therapy Relationship with Personality-Disordered Patients (Davis)
Expands the interpersonal domain. The therapist shifts from authority to friendly advisor/role model. Enumerates 22 causes of noncollaboration including: patient skill deficits, therapist skill deficits, cultural mismatch, beliefs of significant others, fear of therapy failure, fear that change harms others, threat to identity/sense of self, harmoniously blended dysfunctional beliefs (therapist and patient share blind spots), poor model socialization, secondary gain, poor timing, managed care pressure, unstated/vague/unrealistic goals, forced treatment, passive expectations, rigidity, poor impulse control, frustration, and lowered self-esteem. Reframes transference/countertransference as emotional reactions within the therapy process. Therapist DTRs are recommended for managing emotional reactions. (130 words)

### Chapter 6: Paranoid Personality Disorder (Pretzer)
Key word: mistrust. Three perpetuating cycles: (1) vigilance bias -- looking for malice finds apparent malice; (2) interpersonal provocation -- guarded/hostile behavior elicits hostility from others, "confirming" beliefs; (3) moderate self-efficacy -- just confident enough to maintain vigilance rather than abandon it or relax. Treatment: build trust slowly via consistent reliability, increase self-efficacy first (reduces need for vigilance), use collaborative empiricism to test paranoid beliefs. The "continuum technique" is especially useful: rating trustworthiness on 0-100 scale rather than all-or-nothing. Assertiveness training reduces provocation of others. Empathy training late in therapy. PPD clients tend toward premature termination; frame booster sessions as "preventive maintenance." (120 words)

### Chapter 7: Schizoid and Schizotypal Personality Disorders (Morrison & Renton)
**Schizoid**: Key word: isolation. Core beliefs: "I am different/a loner/worthless," "People are cruel/unfulfilling." Unlike avoidant PD, schizoid does not desire relationships. Treatment challenges include engaging an inherently disengaged person; advantages/disadvantages analysis of therapy itself may take multiple sessions. Normalize experiences on a continuum.
**Schizotypal**: Features paranoia, unusual perceptual experiences, magical thinking. Joe case: bullied at school, developed beliefs "others will attack me," "I am different and worthless." Treatment combined verbal reattribution with behavioral experiments (dropping safety behaviors). 30 sessions. Beliefs about being different were accepted; beliefs about worthlessness targeted with schema change methods. Stigma addressed via normalization with prevalence data on hallucinatory and paranoid experiences in the general population. (130 words)

### Chapter 8: Antisocial Personality Disorder (Freeman & Davis)
Not "untreatable" -- the myth stems from (1) psychoanalytic superego requirements, (2) involuntary treatment, (3) viewing ASPD as monolithic genetic entity. Conceptualization: linear/personal (not interpersonal) worldview; cannot hold another's perspective simultaneously with own. Core beliefs: "I need to look out for myself," "Others are patsies or exploitative." Treatment frames problems as choices with risk-benefit analysis. The "choice review" exercise lists situation, choices, advantages, disadvantages, and effectiveness ratings. Cognitive hierarchy of moral development: Level 1 (pure self-interest), Level 2 (consideration of others for long-range self-interest), Level 3 (genuine consideration of others/societal rules). Teaching introspection may itself be a reasonable treatment goal. Depressed antisocial patients respond to treatment; nondepressed do not (Woody et al., 1985). (140 words)

### Chapter 9: Borderline Personality Disorder (Arntz)
Most comprehensive chapter. BPD prevalence: 1-2.5% general population; costs comparable to schizophrenia; ~10% suicide rate. Conceptualization integrates Beck (dichotomous thinking, poor self-concept, identity problems) with Young's schema modes: (1) abused/abandoned child -- desperate longing for care but fearing intimacy; (2) angry/impulsive child -- tantrum-like rage or impulsive gratification; (3) punitive parent -- internalized self-condemnation, "you are bad and deserve punishment"; (4) detached protector -- emotional shutdown, the most common mode. Treatment hierarchy: life-threatening issues > therapeutic relationship > self-damaging behaviors > schema work/trauma processing. Limited reparenting approach: therapist actively breaks through detachment, provides between-session phone contact, soothes, sets personal (not institutional) limits. Empathic confrontation has 3 elements: empathy + confrontation with consequences + explicit functional alternative. Key techniques: daily diary of emotions/thoughts/behaviors, tackling dichotomous thinking (VAS scales), imagery rescripting for trauma. (170 words)

### Chapter 10: Histrionic Personality Disorder (Fleming)
Key word: expressiveness. Core belief: "I am inadequate and unable to handle life on my own" + "It is necessary to be loved by everyone." Cognitive style: global, impressionistic, guided by feelings rather than logic. Histrionics "miss the trees for the forest." Treatment challenges: conflicting thought styles between global patient and systematic therapist; patient views therapist as all-powerful rescuer. Avoid being seduced into "savior" role. Reinforce competence and attention to specifics. Use dramatic methods: externalization of voices, vivid imagery, dramatic behavioral experiments. Allow time for dramatic storytelling within session structure. Homework must be made compelling -- use patient's own dramatic language. Distinguish from bipolar spectrum, narcissistic PD (histrionics will act subservient for attention; narcissists sacrifice attention for superiority), and BPD (less self-destructive). (140 words)

### Chapter 11: Narcissistic Personality Disorder (Davis)
Key word: self-aggrandizement. Core belief: inferiority/unimportance (activated by ego threat; otherwise masked by compensatory superiority belief). Conditional assumptions: evidence of superiority (achievements prove worth), relationships as tools, power/entitlement, image preservation, meritorious contribution. "Narcissistic insult" = any event threatening self-image -> anger, defensiveness, depression. Research: narcissism linked to high (not low) self-esteem, hostile aggression triggered specifically by ego threats (Baumeister). Treatment: do not directly challenge grandiosity early; use cost-benefit analysis of current strategies; test beliefs about needing to be special for happiness; explore childhood origins of specialness = worth equation; build alternative sources of self-esteem (sharing, belonging, contributing). Table 11.3: menu of alternative beliefs for building non-narcissistic self-esteem. 40 sessions typical. (140 words)

### Chapter 12: Dependent Personality Disorder (Fleming)
Key word: attachment/helplessness. Two core assumptions: (1) "I am inherently inadequate and helpless," (2) "The solution is to find someone strong to take care of me." Deceptively easy to engage initially (cooperative, appreciative) but frustrating later (passive clinging, resistance to autonomy). DPD associated with anxious attachment (Bowlby). Treatment paradox: increasing competence triggers fear of abandonment. Use continuum of independence (not dichotomous dependent vs. independent). Key techniques: graded autonomy tasks, assertiveness training, challenging dichotomous thinking, behavioral experiments to test "if I'm competent I'll be abandoned," imperfect homework assignments (deliberate imperfection to counter all-or-nothing thinking). Fade sessions gradually -- the spacing itself becomes a behavioral experiment. Group therapy helps dilute therapist dependency. Include significant others if dependency is being reinforced. (130 words)

### Chapter 13: Avoidant Personality Disorder (Padesky & J. Beck)
Key word: avoidance (behavioral, cognitive, emotional). Core beliefs: "I am socially inept and undesirable," "Others will reject me if they know the real me," "I can't tolerate unpleasant feelings." Three types of avoidance: social (withdrawing from situations), cognitive (suppressing/distracting from upsetting thoughts), emotional (damping dysphoric feelings, substance use). Treatment must address all three. Differentiating from social phobia: APD involves pervasive avoidance across all social situations + self-deprecation + belief in intolerability of distress. From dependent PD: both see self as inadequate but avoidant sees others as critical/rejecting while dependent sees others as nurturant. Techniques: graduated exposure, building tolerance for negative affect in session, schema change methods for core beliefs, positive data logs. Address cognitive avoidance first -- patients "shut down" when approaching painful material. (130 words)

### Chapter 14: Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (Simon)
Key words: control and should. Core belief: "If I don't perform perfectly, I am personally unacceptable/deficient." Underlying: deep concern about being helpless/overwhelmed; overemphasis on systems compensates for perceived defectiveness. Thinking style: "misses the forest for the trees" -- excessive detail focus with minimized subjective experience. Treatment is more businesslike than usual; pushing emotional closeness early is counterproductive. OCPD patients may produce perfectionistic homework or use techniques in a ruminative, vacillating manner -- both are samples of problematic behavior to address. Key techniques: behavioral experiments (comparing productivity on procrastinated vs. non-procrastinated tasks), double-standard technique, advantages/disadvantages of specific beliefs, predictive diaries. Typical cognitive distortions: dichotomous thinking, magnification, overgeneralization, "should" statements. 15 sessions over 6 months in the index case. (130 words)

### Chapter 15: Passive-Aggressive Personality Disorder (Fusco)
Key word: resistance. Core conflict: desire for approval/support from authorities vs. desire to maintain autonomy. Core beliefs: "Being controlled is intolerable," "I have to do things my own way," "Others are intrusive/demanding." Millon's negativistic personality: resentful expression, contrary interpersonal style, skeptical cognition, discontented self-image, irritable mood. Treatment: avoid power struggles; allow patient control over agenda; use Socratic questioning and cost-benefit analysis to find middle ground between dependence and oppositionalism. Assertiveness training converts covert anger to overt, functional expression. Self-monitoring of affective shifts and automatic thoughts. Communication training: "I" statements, pausing, appropriate eye contact. Core beliefs about control reactivate easily; relapse prevention list of trigger situations essential. (130 words)

### Chapter 16: Synthesis and Prospects for the Future (Pretzer)
Ten clinical guidelines: (1) individualized conceptualization over standard formulations; (2) collaborative, clearly identified shared goals; (3) focus extensively on therapeutic relationship; (4) begin with low-self-disclosure interventions when appropriate; (5) promote increase in self-efficacy; (6) do not rely solely on verbal interventions -- use behavioral experiments; (7) work to identify and address client's aversive emotions as they arise; (8) anticipate noncollaboration and address it proactively; (9) attend to cognitive as well as behavioral aspects of problems; (10) maintain a long-range perspective -- personality change is gradual. Future directions: need for controlled outcome studies, dimensional vs. categorical classification, mechanism studies. (100 words)

### Cognitive Profiles by Personality Disorder (Table 2.3 -- Expanded)

#### Avoidant PD
| Domain | Content |
|---|---|
| **View of self** | Vulnerable to depreciation, rejection; socially inept; incompetent |
| **View of others** | Critical, demeaning, superior |
| **Core beliefs** | "I am no good... worthless... unlovable." "I can't tolerate unpleasant feelings." |
| **Conditional beliefs** | "If people got close to me, they'd discover the 'real me' and reject me -- intolerable." "If I undertake something new and don't succeed, it would be devastating." |
| **Instrumental beliefs** | "Stay clear of risky involvement." "Avoid unpleasant situations at all costs." |
| **Main threats** | Being discovered as a "fraud," being put down, demeaned, rejected |
| **Main strategy** | Avoid evaluative situations; avoid unpleasant feelings or thoughts |
| **Main affect** | Dysphoria (anxiety + sadness); anxiety about social/work evaluation |
| **Overdeveloped** | Social vulnerability, Avoidance, Inhibition |
| **Underdeveloped** | Self-assertion, Gregariousness |

#### Dependent PD
| Domain | Content |
|---|---|
| **View of self** | Needy, weak, helpless, incompetent |
| **View of others** | (Idealized) Nurturant, supportive, competent |
| **Core beliefs** | "I am completely helpless." "I am all alone." |
| **Conditional beliefs** | "I can function only if I have access to somebody competent." "If I am abandoned, I will die." "If I am not loved, I will always be unhappy." |
| **Instrumental beliefs** | "Don't offend the caretaker." "Stay close." "Cultivate as intimate a relationship as possible." "Be subservient to bind him/her." |
| **Main threats** | Rejection or abandonment |
| **Main strategy** | Cultivate dependent relationships; subordinate to a "strong" figure; placate |
| **Main affect** | Anxiety (over disruption of dependent relationship); depression if figure removed; euphoria when dependent wishes granted |
| **Overdeveloped** | Help seeking, Clinging |
| **Underdeveloped** | Self-sufficiency, Mobility |

|---|---|

| **Main affect** | Unexpressed anger (rebellion against authority's rules), alternating with anxiety about reprisals |
| **Overdeveloped** | Autonomy, Resistance, Passivity, Sabotage |

#### Obsessive-Compulsive PD
| Domain | Content |
|---|---|
| **View of self** | Responsible, accountable, fastidious, competent (covert: inept, helpless) |
| **View of others** | Irresponsible, casual, incompetent, self-indulgent |

| **Main affect** | Regret, disappointment, anger (at self and others); anxiety about substandard performance; depression after serious "failure" |
| **Overdeveloped** | Control, Responsibility, Systematization |
| **Underdeveloped** | Spontaneity, Playfulness |

#### Borderline PD
| Domain | Content |
|---|---|
| **View of self** | Vulnerable (to rejection, betrayal, domination); deprived; powerless; out of control; defective; unlovable; bad |

#### Schizotypal PD
| Domain | Content |
|---|---|
| **View of self** | Unreal, detached, loner; vulnerable, socially conspicuous; supernaturally sensitive |
| **View of others** | Untrustworthy, malevolent |
| **Core beliefs** | Idiographic, odd, superstitious; magical thinking (clairvoyance, telepathy, "sixth sense"); "It's better to be isolated from others." |
| **Main strategy** | Watch for and neutralize malevolent attention; stay to self; be vigilant for supernatural forces |

## Disorder-Specific Formulations

### Avoidant PD
The avoidant person wants closeness but fears evaluation. Three avoidance types operate: social (withdrawing from situations), cognitive (suppressing upsetting thoughts), and emotional (damping dysphoria, sometimes with substances). The developmental pathway typically involves early experiences of criticism, ridicule, or rejection that produce core beliefs of being socially inept and fundamentally defective. The conditional formula "If people know the real me, they will reject me" makes every social encounter a test of acceptability. Low tolerance for dysphoria prevents developing methods to overcome shyness. Treatment addresses all three avoidance types: graduated exposure for social avoidance, building affect tolerance for emotional avoidance, and teaching patients to stay with uncomfortable thoughts for cognitive avoidance. The APD patient may "shut down" in session -- going blank, changing topics, standing up -- when approaching core material. This is cognitive avoidance in vivo and must be addressed directly but gently. Schema change methods (positive data logs, historical tests of belief) target the core belief "I am undesirable." Differentiation from social phobia: APD is pervasive across all social contexts with self-deprecation and belief in intolerability of distress. From dependent PD: both see self as inadequate, but avoidant sees others as critical/rejecting while dependent sees others as nurturant. (200 words)

### Dependent PD
Two key assumptions: "I am inherently inadequate" and "I must find someone strong to care for me." The dependent person sacrifices autonomy in exchange for caretaking. This adaptation carries costs: no opportunity to learn independent skills, must perpetually please the caretaker, and feels devastated if the relationship ends. Developmental origins often include overprotective parenting or family environments low in expressiveness and high in control. The treatment paradox: increased competence triggers abandonment fear. Deceptively easy engagement (cooperative, appreciative, flattering) gives way to frustrating passive resistance to autonomy. Therapist must resist being seduced into the rescuer role. Key strategy: use a continuum of independence rather than dichotomous thinking (totally dependent vs. totally independent). Graded autonomy tasks, deliberately imperfect homework assignments, and behavioral experiments testing "if I'm competent, I'll be abandoned." Fade sessions gradually as a built-in behavioral experiment. The termination process is itself therapeutic -- gradually increasing spacing proves the patient can function independently. Group therapy helps dilute therapist dependency. (170 words)

### OCPD
"Control" and "should" define this disorder. The OCPD patient makes a virtue of means to such an extent that means become ends. Core schema: "If I don't always function perfectly, I am personally unacceptable." Deep fear of being overwhelmed/helpless drives overemphasis on systems as compensation. Thinking style: "misses the forest for the trees" -- excessive detail focus with minimized subjective experience. Subclinical OCPD traits are rewarded by society (conscientiousness, perseverance, reliability), making the disorder harder to identify and challenge. The therapist-patient relationship is more businesslike than usual; pushing emotional closeness early is counterproductive. OCPD patients may produce perfectionistic homework or use techniques in a ruminative, vacillating manner -- both are samples of problematic behavior to address. Key techniques: behavioral experiments (comparing productivity on procrastinated vs. non-procrastinated tasks), double-standard technique (would you judge a friend this way?), advantages/disadvantages of specific beliefs, predictive diaries comparing predicted catastrophes with actual outcomes. Typical cognitive distortions: dichotomous thinking, magnification, overgeneralization, "should" statements. 15 sessions over 6 months in the index case. (180 words)

### Paranoid PD
Three self-perpetuating cycles maintain paranoia: (1) selective vigilance finds apparent evidence of malice, confirming beliefs; (2) guarded/hostile interpersonal behavior provokes actual hostility from others; (3) moderate self-efficacy maintains the belief that vigilance is necessary and just barely sufficient. Trust-building is the primary therapeutic task -- established through consistent, reliable behavior rather than persuasion. Begin by increasing self-efficacy through standard anxiety management and problem-solving; this reduces the perceived need for vigilance. Use collaborative empiricism, particularly the continuum technique (rating trustworthiness on 0-100 rather than all-or-nothing). As trust develops, address paranoid beliefs more directly through behavioral experiments testing predictions about others' intentions. Improve interpersonal skills to reduce provocation of others. Develop empathy for others' perspectives late in therapy. PPD clients prefer self-reliance and may want to terminate prematurely; frame booster sessions as "preventive maintenance" reflecting good judgment, not weakness. (160 words)

### Narcissistic PD
Core belief of inferiority is masked by compensatory beliefs of superiority. "Narcissistic insult" (any threat to self-image) triggers anger, defensiveness, or depression. Five conditional assumption domains: evidence of superiority (achievements prove worth), relationships as tools, power/entitlement, image preservation, and meritorious contribution. Research shows narcissism is linked to moderate-to-high self-esteem, not low self-esteem; hostility is triggered specifically by ego threats. Treatment must not directly challenge grandiosity early. Instead, use collaborative curiosity about the costs of current strategies, cost-benefit analysis of needing to be special, testing beliefs through behavioral experiments (e.g., doing something ordinary and rating enjoyment). Explore childhood origins of "specialness = worth" equation. Build underdeveloped strategies: sharing, group identification, contributing without self-advancement as motive. A menu of alternative beliefs (Table 11.3) provides options the patient has never considered. Typical treatment: 40 sessions over 1.5 years. Maintain follow-up contact to support functional efforts and note regression toward self-aggrandizing strategies. (170 words)

### Histrionic PD
Core assumption: "I am inadequate and unable to handle life on my own" drives desperate need for attention/approval. The histrionic takes a "pragmatic approach" to getting caretaking -- actively seeking attention rather than passively hoping (cf. dependent PD). Cognitive style is global and impressionistic -- guided by feelings rather than logic. Treatment challenge: the systematic, detail-oriented approach of CT directly conflicts with the histrionic style; this conflict IS the treatment. Reinforce competence and attention to specifics. Use dramatic methods that leverage the patient's style: externalization of voices, vivid imagery modification, dramatic behavioral experiments. Allow structured time for dramatic storytelling in sessions. Written homework will be viewed as boring -- use patient's own dramatic language in assignments. Biggest risk: early dropout due to boredom. Set genuinely compelling goals (not just what the patient thinks therapy "should" address). Avoid being seduced into the rescuer role; set consistent limits on fee manipulation and special requests. (160 words)

### Antisocial PD
Not untreatable but requires modified expectations. Core: personal (not interpersonal) worldview; cannot simultaneously hold another's perspective. Treatment frames everything as choices with consequences. The "choice review" exercise (listing situation, choices, advantages, disadvantages, effectiveness ratings) respects the patient's need for autonomy while introducing consideration of consequences. The cognitive hierarchy of moral development provides a treatment roadmap: Level 1 (pure self-interest), Level 2 (consideration of others for long-range self-interest -- the realistic target), Level 3 (genuine social consideration). Teaching introspection/self-monitoring may itself be a valid treatment goal. Depressed antisocial patients respond to treatment; nondepressed do not. Structure, contingency management, and environmental supports are essential. Therapist must remain alert to manipulation (e.g., offers of "favors") and address these as data about the patient's typical strategies. The therapist cannot predict how destructive the patient would have been without treatment, but incremental improvements in prosocial behavior have real-world value. (160 words)

### Borderline PD
The most complex disorder in the book. Conceptualization integrates Beck (dichotomous thinking, impaired self-concept, identity diffusion, weak reality testing under stress) with Young's schema mode model. Four pathological modes cycle: the abused/abandoned child (desperate, longing for care but fearing it), the angry/impulsive child (tantrum-like rage or impulsive gratification), the punitive parent (internalized self-condemnation: "you are bad and deserve punishment"), and the detached protector (emotional shutdown, the most common mode). The healthy adult mode is the treatment target. Treatment hierarchy: life-threatening issues > therapeutic relationship > self-damaging behaviors > schema work/trauma processing. Limited reparenting: therapist actively breaks through detachment, provides between-session availability, soothes, sets personal (not institutional) limits, and tolerates high levels of negative emotion. Empathic confrontation has three elements: empathy + confrontation with consequences + explicit functional alternative. Key techniques: daily diary linking emotions/thoughts/behaviors to modes, tackling dichotomous thinking with visual analogue scales, imagery rescripting for trauma, limit-setting with graduated consequences (Table 9.3). Dropout prevention requires active pursuit of absent patients. 1-year minimum for stabilization; longer treatment needed for core schema change. (200 words)

### Schizoid PD
The embodiment of autonomy. Unlike avoidant PD, the schizoid person genuinely does not desire close relationships (vs. avoidant who desires but fears). Developmental pathway: peer rejection/bullying + being seen as "different" by family -> beliefs about self as different, others as cruel, relationships as unrewarding. Treatment challenge: therapy is inherently interpersonal, contradicting the patient's fundamental orientation. May require 5+ sessions simply discussing advantages/disadvantages of attending therapy before work can begin. A schizoid-specific "blueprint" for coping serves as the relapse prevention document. Differentiate from avoidant PD (desire for relationships), autism spectrum (stereotyped behaviors), and prodromal psychosis. (100 words)

### Schizotypal PD
Features paranoia, unusual perceptual experiences, and magical thinking alongside Cluster A social withdrawal. Often emerges from childhood bullying and rejection. Treatment targets paranoid beliefs and social anxiety using standard cognitive techniques adapted for this population: examining evidence, generating alternatives, behavioral experiments (dropping safety behaviors). Magical thinking may respond to strategies developed for obsessional patients (testing thought-action fusion beliefs). Normalize experiences using prevalence data on hallucinatory/paranoid experiences in the general population. Address stigma of the diagnosis directly. Not all schizotypal features need to be targeted -- if unusual experiences are not distressing and serve a function, they can be accepted. 30 sessions typical. (100 words)

### Passive-Aggressive PD
The "active ambivalent": wants approval and support from authority but cannot tolerate control. Millon's negativistic personality: resentful expression, contrary interpersonal style, skeptical cognition, discontented self-image, irritable mood. Treatment must navigate between the Scylla of too much structure (triggers control resistance) and the Charybdis of too little structure (enables passivity). Allow the patient maximum control over agenda setting and goal selection. When the patient defies their own chosen goals, identify this as empirical evidence of the ambivalence pattern. Use cost-benefit analysis to find middle ground between dependence and oppositionalism. Assertiveness training converts covert anger to overt, functional expression. Self-monitoring of affective shifts and their associated automatic thoughts. Written rules for scheduling, billing, and time frames prevent passive-aggressive acting-out around therapy logistics. (130 words)

## Beck vs Young: Schema Concepts

### Overlap
- Both use "schema" as fundamental unit of personality organization
- Both see schemas as stable, self-perpetuating, and developed from early experience
- Both emphasize identifying and modifying core beliefs/schemas as essential to PD treatment
- Both recognize that schemas are activated by relevant situations and bias information processing
- Both developed self-report measures (PBQ vs. SQ) to assess personality-related cognitive content
- Both acknowledge the role of compensatory strategies in maintaining schemas

### Divergence
| Feature | Beck's Schema Concept | Young's EMS Model |
|---|---|---|
| **Terminology** | "Schemas" = basic cognitive structures | "Early Maladaptive Schemas" (EMSs) = broad pervasive themes |
| **Mapping** | Disorder-specific (each PD has a characteristic belief set) | Trans-diagnostic (EMSs cut across DSM categories) |
| **Number** | 9 PD-specific belief sets (PBQ) | 18 EMSs organized under 5 domains |
| **Modes** | Beck (1990s workshops): "regression into intense emotional states" in BPD | Young elaborated into formal mode model: child modes, maladaptive coping modes, parent modes, healthy adult |
| **Coping styles** | Compensatory strategies = overdeveloped strategies | Three coping styles: overcompensation, surrender, avoidance |
| **Assessment** | PBQ maps directly onto Axis II disorders | SQ/YSQ measures dimensions independent of DSM nosology |
| **BPD** | Not given a single belief profile; endorses many belief sets | Central focus: mode flipping explains BPD instability |
| **Relationship** | Collaborative empiricism; therapist as "auxiliary reality tester" | Limited reparenting; therapist more explicitly meets unmet childhood needs |
| **Stability** | Schemas become more extreme during Axis I episodes (cognitive shift) | EMSs relatively stable even when Axis I symptoms improve (SQ-SF data) |
| **Emphasis** | Information processing, cognitive distortions, evolutionary strategies | Developmental needs, emotional deprivation, attachment patterns |

### Clinical Integration
Beck and Young's models are complementary rather than contradictory. Beck provides disorder-specific cognitive profiles and a rich repertoire of cognitive techniques; Young provides a developmental/attachment-based framework and the mode model that is especially useful for BPD. The BPD chapter in this volume (Ch. 9, Arntz) explicitly integrates both approaches. The PBQ and SQ can be used together for complementary assessment -- PBQ maps dimensional personality disorder beliefs while SQ reveals underlying developmental schemas.

## Exam-Ready Extracts

> **EXAM PEARL 1:** Beck's Table 2.1 -- Each PD has a basic belief/attitude and corresponding strategy: Dependent ("I am helpless" -> Attachment), Avoidant ("I may get hurt" -> Avoidance), Paranoid ("People are dangerous" -> Wariness), Narcissistic ("I am special" -> Self-aggrandizement), OCPD ("I must not err" -> Perfectionism), Antisocial ("Others are to be taken" -> Attack), Histrionic ("I need to impress" -> Dramatics), Schizoid ("I need plenty of space" -> Isolation), Passive-Aggressive ("I could be controlled" -> Resistance). BPD and Schizotypal are excluded as they lack a single belief-strategy set.

> **EXAM PEARL 2:** Three schema change options exist on a continuum: Schema RESTRUCTURING (tear down and rebuild -- rare, ideal), Schema MODIFICATION (smaller changes -- most common realistic goal), Schema REINTERPRETATION (understand and reframe in more functional ways -- most conservative).

> **EXAM PEARL 3:** The Personality Belief Questionnaire (PBQ) has 9 scales with 14 items each (126 items total). Five scales validated against SCID-II diagnoses: Avoidant, Dependent, OCPD, Narcissistic, and Paranoid PDs all scored highest on their corresponding PBQ scale. The BPD scale was empirically derived: 14 beliefs discriminating BPD from other PDs.

> **EXAM PEARL 4:** Axis I vs. Axis II differences: Axis I beliefs are more PLASTIC and responsive to treatment (or even spontaneous remission); Axis II beliefs are STRUCTURALIZED into normal cognitive organization and require much longer, more intensive treatment. The PD mode is the "normal" mode -- denser, more heavily represented, less amenable to change.

> **EXAM PEARL 5:** Young's four BPD schema modes: (1) Abused/Abandoned Child -- desperate, longing for care but fearing it; (2) Angry/Impulsive Child -- tantrum-like rage or impulsive gratification; (3) Punitive Parent -- self-condemnation, "you are bad and deserve punishment"; (4) Detached Protector -- emotional shutdown, most common presentation. The Healthy Adult mode is the treatment target.

> **EXAM PEARL 6:** BPD treatment hierarchy (Beck/Arntz): (1) Life-threatening issues, (2) Therapeutic relationship, (3) Self-damaging behaviors, (4) Schema work and trauma processing. This hierarchy applies both within-session (agenda) and across therapy phases.

> **EXAM PEARL 7:** Thinking styles distinguish personality disorders: Histrionics are GLOBAL and IMPRESSIONISTIC ("miss the trees for the forest"), OCPDs are DETAIL-FOCUSED ("miss the forest for the trees"), Avoidants use COGNITIVE AVOIDANCE (dampening unpleasant thoughts before they form).

> **EXAM PEARL 8:** Cognitive therapy is effective for antisocial patients ONLY when they are depressed at pretreatment (Woody et al., 1985). Nondepressed antisocial patients show little response. This finding supports the role of distress as a motivator for change in ASPD.

> **EXAM PEARL 9:** The "downward arrow" technique (Beck, 1985) probes progressively deeper meanings until core schemas are accessed. The arousal of strong affect signals that a core schema has been reached and that dysfunctional thinking is NOW more accessible to modification.

> **EXAM PEARL 10:** Narcissistic PD has a DUAL belief structure: the manifest/compensatory belief is superiority ("I am special"), but the core belief is INFERIORITY/UNIMPORTANCE, activated only under ego threat. Research (Baumeister) shows narcissism is linked to high self-esteem + hostile aggression when threatened, NOT to low self-esteem.

> **EXAM PEARL 11:** Avoidant PD vs. Dependent PD vs. Schizoid PD -- all have impaired social functioning but for different reasons: Avoidant DESIRES closeness but FEARS rejection; Dependent DESIRES closeness and SEEKS attachment figures idealized as competent; Schizoid DOES NOT DESIRE closeness and prefers isolation. Avoidant sees others as critical; Dependent sees others as nurturant; Schizoid sees others as intrusive.

> **EXAM PEARL 12:** The 22 causes of noncollaboration (Ch. 5) include: patient skill deficits, therapist skill deficits, cultural mismatch, interfering beliefs of significant others, fear of therapy failure, fear that change harms others, threat to identity, harmoniously blended dysfunctional beliefs (therapist and patient share blind spots), poor model socialization, secondary gain, poor timing, managed care reactance, unstated/vague/unrealistic goals, lack of motivation, passive/magical expectations, rigidity, poor impulse control, frustration with lack of progress, and lowered self-esteem from patient status.

> **EXAM PEARL 13:** Empathic confrontation (Arntz, for BPD) has THREE elements: (1) empathic expression that therapist understands why the dysfunctional strategy was chosen, (2) confrontation with the negative effects of the strategy, (3) explicit formulation of a functional alternative with request to follow through.

> **EXAM PEARL 14:** Beck's cognitive profiles (Table 2.3) include VIEW OF SELF, VIEW OF OTHERS, MAIN BELIEFS, and MAIN STRATEGY for each PD. The overdeveloped/underdeveloped strategies (Table 2.2) pair each PD's hypertrophied pattern with its atrophied counterpart. These two tables together are the single most testable content in the book.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 1:** When therapy stalls or noncompliance persists unexpectedly, always consider an undiagnosed personality disorder. The six heuristic signs: "I've always been this way," persistent noncompliance, sudden therapy stoppage, unawareness of effect on others, lip service without change, and personality patterns seen as ego-syntonic.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 2:** For paranoid patients: build trust through CONSISTENT, RELIABLE BEHAVIOR rather than persuasion. Begin by increasing self-efficacy (reduces need for vigilance). Use the continuum technique (0-100 trustworthiness ratings) instead of all-or-nothing trust assessments. Address paranoid beliefs directly only after trust is established.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 3:** For dependent patients: the treatment itself models the problem. Resist the rescue role. Use the continuum of independence (not dichotomous). Fade sessions gradually as a built-in behavioral experiment. Deliberately imperfect homework combats all-or-nothing thinking. Remember: increased competence TRIGGERS abandonment fear -- pace accordingly.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 4:** For borderline patients: validate emotions FIRST before addressing cognitions. The initial therapeutic response to distress should be calm, accepting, soothing presence. Premature practical suggestions fuel the punitive parent mode ("so I did it wrong"). Address dichotomous thinking with visual analogue scales. Use the treatment hierarchy to prioritize within and across sessions.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 5:** For narcissistic patients: do NOT directly challenge grandiosity early. Use collaborative curiosity about the COSTS of current strategies. The "narcissistic insult" is any ego threat -- recognize it as a schema activation and use it as an opportunity to access the core belief of inferiority.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 6:** For OCPD patients: the therapeutic relationship should be MORE BUSINESSLIKE than usual, not less. Do not push emotional closeness early. Perfectionistic homework is a SAMPLE of the problem, not evidence of engagement. Behavioral experiments (comparing procrastinated vs. non-procrastinated task performance) are more convincing than cognitive disputation alone.

> **MNEMONIC 1:** **SPACES** -- Beck's six schema properties: **S**cope (breadth), **P**lasticity (flexibility/rigidity), **A**ctivation level (valence), **C**entrality (density/prominence), **E**nergy (latent to hypervalent), **S**tability (trait vs. state)

> **MNEMONIC 2:** **DAMP HANS** -- Personality disorders with specific cognitive profiles in Table 2.3: **D**ependent, **A**voidant, **M**ixed (Passive-Aggressive), **P**aranoid, **H**istrionic, **A**ntisocial, **N**arcissistic, **S**chizoid. (BPD and Schizotypal excluded from the main profile because they lack a single belief-strategy set.)

> **MNEMONIC 3:** The BPD Treatment Hierarchy = **LTSS**: **L**ife-threatening issues > **T**herapeutic relationship > **S**elf-damaging behaviors > **S**chema work/trauma processing. Always return to higher levels if lower-level work reactivates crises.

> **MNEMONIC 4:** Young's BPD Schema Modes = **AAPD-H**: **A**bused/Abandoned Child, **A**ngry/Impulsive Child, **P**unitive Parent, **D**etached Protector, **H**ealthy Adult. The detached protector is the most common presentation; the healthy adult is the treatment target.
