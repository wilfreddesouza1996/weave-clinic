# KB: Schema Therapy in Practice
> Arnoud Arntz & Gitta Jacob | 2013 | Wiley-Blackwell | 280 pages
> Translated from German original: *Schematherapie in der Praxis* (Beltz, 2011)

## Core Framework

This book is **not** a restatement of Young et al. (2003). It is a **practical clinical manual centred on the schema mode approach** — the post-Young evolution of ST developed primarily by Arntz's Maastricht group and Bernstein's forensic work. Key departures from Young:

1. **Mode-first, not schema-first.** The 18 EMSs are reviewed (Ch. 1) but treated as background. The entire clinical apparatus — case conceptualisation, treatment planning, and intervention — operates through the **schema mode model** (18 modes across 4 categories). Modes are states; schemas are traits. Modes are easier to detect in session and more directly targetable.

2. **Expanded mode inventory.** Young (2003) defined 10 modes. Arntz/Bernstein expanded to **18 modes** including angry protector, avoidant protector, detached self-soother, attention-seeker, perfectionistic overcontroller, paranoid overcontroller, conning/manipulative, and predator. All assessed via the Schema Mode Inventory (SMI; Lobbestael et al., 2010).

3. **Disorder-specific mode models.** Prototypical mode constellations for BPD, NPD, HPD, AvPD, DPD, OCPD, paranoid PD, forensic populations, and chronic Axis-I (eating disorders, OCD, chronic depression, bipolar). Young only specified BPD and NPD.

4. **Crisis support finding.** Nadort et al. (2009) showed no added benefit of out-of-hours crisis phone support in BPD — this standard was removed from the protocol. Email contact offered instead.

5. **Imagery rescripting as structured protocol, not exposure.** Arntz explicitly differentiates IR from prolonged exposure: the patient need only reach the point of emotional contact with the traumatic memory, not relive the full trauma. Arntz et al. (2007) showed IR superior to imaginal exposure on anger, guilt, and shame reduction in PTSD.

6. **Transdiagnostic orientation.** The book presents ST as a generic method, not disorder-specific. Mode models for Axis-I disorders (OCD, eating disorders, chronic depression) are proposed by fitting symptoms into personality-level mode frameworks.

7. **Medication caveat.** Giesen-Bloo et al. (2006) found post hoc that psychotropic medication (particularly sedatives) significantly reduced ST effectiveness in BPD. Sedation blocks emotional processing required for IR.

## Chapter Digest

### Part I: Case Conceptualisation

#### Chapter 1 — Basics (pp. 5-40)
Reviews 18 EMSs across 5 schema domains with case examples for each. Introduces three coping styles (surrender, avoidance, overcompensation) and the schema mode model (child modes, dysfunctional parent modes, coping modes, healthy modes). Establishes the needs-based framework: schemas develop from unmet childhood needs. Five groups of basic needs map to five schema domains. Covers the FAQ on schema vs. mode differentiation, positive schemas, and hierarchical relations between schemas. The key clinical principle: modes are states, schemas are traits; modes are more directly accessible in session.

#### Chapter 2 — The Mode Concept (pp. 41-90)
The centrepiece chapter. Presents 18 schema modes in detail across four categories: (a) dysfunctional child modes (lonely, abandoned/abused, humiliated/inferior, dependent, angry, obstinate, enraged, impulsive, undisciplined), (b) dysfunctional parent modes (punitive, demanding — with subtypes for achievement-focused demanding and guilt-inducing punitive), (c) dysfunctional coping modes (compliant surrender; avoidant — detached protector, avoidant protector, angry protector, detached self-soother; overcompensatory — self-aggrandizer, attention-seeker, perfectionistic overcontroller, paranoid overcontroller, bully & attack, conning/manipulative, predator), (d) healthy modes (happy child, healthy adult). Provides disorder-specific mode models for BPD, NPD, HPD, AvPD, DPD, OCPD, paranoid PD, and forensic patients. Discusses OCD, eating disorders, chronic depression, and bipolar within mode framework. Extended section on differentiating anger-related modes (angry child vs. angry protector vs. bully & attack) via countertransference. Discusses sexual problems mapped to modes.

#### Chapter 3 — Communicating the Mode Concept to the Patient (pp. 91-99)
Order of mode introduction: (1) vulnerable child modes first (validates suffering), (2) dysfunctional parent modes, (3) coping modes (often confrontational), (4) healthy adult mode. Patients name their own modes. Disagreement is acceptable — agree to revisit. Loyalty issues with parent modes addressed (introjects, not real parents). Overcompensation modes hardest for patients to accept; use video feedback if needed.

### Part II: Treatment

#### Chapter 4 — Treatment Overview (pp. 103-116)
Treatment goals by mode type: vulnerable child = validate/comfort/process; angry child = ventilate/redirect; impulsive child = validate needs + set limits; parent modes = question/limit/fight; coping modes = empathic confrontation + reduce; healthy modes = strengthen. Three intervention channels: cognitive (reframing, psychoeducation, pro/con lists), emotion-focused (imagery rescripting, chair work), behavioral (pattern breaking, skills training, exposure). Therapeutic relationship: limited reparenting + empathic confrontation + limit-setting. Figures 4.1-4.5 provide visual maps of intervention by mode.

#### Chapter 5 — Overcoming Coping Modes (pp. 117-140)
Core strategy: empathic confrontation. Sequence: identify/label mode, explore biographical background, discuss pros and cons (always start with pros to validate), then work toward reduction. Two-chair technique for exploring coping modes — the mode is "interviewed" about its function. Detailed protocols for detached protector ("Wall" dialogue), narcissistic self-aggrandizer ("Super Philip" dialogue with vulnerable child chair added). Variation with Playmobil figures for severely dissociative BPD patients. Direct bypassing of coping modes in later therapy (skip to child/parent mode behind it). Behavioral: increase social activity for avoidant modes; social skills training for overcompensation; DBT skills as temporary bridge for self-injury/dissociation. Key principle: confrontation of coping mode is not rude — it is necessary because coping modes destroy relationships.

#### Chapter 6 — Treating Vulnerable Child Modes (pp. 141-191)
The longest and most clinically dense chapter. Sections on: limited reparenting (including extra reparenting — transitional objects, audio recordings, email contact; crisis phone support removed per Nadort 2009); cognitive techniques (psychoeducation on children's rights, schema flashcards); emotion-focused techniques (imagery rescripting — full protocol); behavioral techniques (end abusive relationships, express needs, positive activities for happy child).

**Imagery rescripting protocol (Table 6.3):** (1) Relaxation/safe place (optional), (2) Access current stressful situation in imagery, (3) Affect bridge to childhood memory, (4) Explore childhood scene — focus on child's feelings and needs, (5) Introduce helping figure to rescript, (6) Deepen safety and attachment, (7) Optional: transfer solution back to current situation.

**Three types of helping figures:** (a) therapist (weak healthy adult), (b) third-party helper — real or fantasy (intermediate), (c) patient's own healthy adult (strong). Sequence: therapist first, then third party, then patient's own healthy adult over course of therapy. Joint rescripting as intermediate step.

**Rescripting process varies by parent mode type:** Aggressive/abusive parent modes = dramatic intervention (police, fighting, even killing perpetrator); guilt-inducing depressive parent modes = explain mother's depression, relieve child of responsibility, arrange professional help for mother. Aggression in IR: Arntz et al. (2007) found IR with revenge/aggressive rescripting led to greater anger control than exposure alone in PTSD. No evidence of disinhibition.

Limits of reparenting: differentiate emotional dependency (acceptable temporarily) from functional dependency (must be addressed). BPD + dependent traits = special dilemma requiring gradual shift from reparenting to autonomy-building.

#### Chapter 7 — Treating Angry and Impulsive Child Modes (pp. 193-215)
Two distinct categories: angry/enraged (hot affect, needs not met) vs. impulsive/undisciplined (hedonistic needs, lack of discipline). Different biographical roots: angry = punished for expressing needs; undisciplined = spoiled or overburdened without guidance. Treatment for angry child: validate, ventilate, anticipate punitive parent activation, limit destructive expression, encourage adequate expression. Treatment for undisciplined child: validate needs, confront spoiled/impulsive character, model discipline, set limits. Obstinacy = blocked autonomy; often requires addressing the mode directly before any other work. Chair dialogue between undisciplined child and healthy adult with therapist taking the dysfunctional chair to provoke healthy response.

#### Chapter 8 — Treating Dysfunctional Parent Modes (pp. 217-243)
Central goal: reduce/eliminate parent modes, replace with healthy values. Chair dialogue as primary technique. **Chair dialogue protocol:** Two-chair (parent mode vs. healthy adult), three-chair (add vulnerable child or angry child). Therapist takes active role — models healthy adult, fights punitive parent, even throws chair out of room. Patient sits on punitive parent chair only briefly (or not at all in severe cases — report from distance). Therapist gradually transfers healthy adult role to patient over therapy.

**Adapting chair dialogues to parent mode type:** Achievement-focused demanding parent = discussion/negotiation; guilt-inducing parent = address responsibility attribution and right to set limits; punitive parent = firm fighting, removal from room. Recording messages to counter punitive parent mode between sessions. Advanced techniques: unfinished business, saying goodbye, couples therapy in modes, dream work.

Dealing with guilt: fighting the parent mode is not fighting real parents — it is fighting damaging introjects. Understanding parents' motives comes later; premature understanding suppresses anger and reinforces introjects. Especially in Cluster-C: anger first, understanding later.

#### Chapter 9 — Strengthening the Healthy Adult Mode (pp. 245-254)
Healthy adult development is both indirect (reducing dysfunctional modes) and direct. Strategies: relate all interventions to mode model (keeps healthy adult reflective), discuss emotion-focused interventions collaboratively, include patient stepwise as healthy adult in IR and chair work, record sessions, address therapeutic relationship problems openly. Termination: 2-3 years for severe PD (start with 2x/week), shorter for less severe. Taper frequency gradually, offer booster sessions, remain marginally available post-termination (e.g., bi-annual email exchange).

## Clinical Techniques Catalogue

### 1. Imagery Rescripting — Detailed Protocol

**Steps (Table 6.3):**
1. **Relaxation.** Patient closes eyes, brief breathing focus. Safe-place image optional (often impractical for severe BPD — skip if patient cannot find one).
2. **Current stressful situation.** Patient imagines current problem from field perspective. Focus on feelings, not narrative details. Direct questions if needed: "Do you feel sad, anxious, ashamed?"
3. **Affect bridge.** "Wipe out the current scene but keep the feeling. Let a childhood memory come up." If nothing comes: ask about childhood photographs, then "fill the photo with life."
4. **Explore childhood scene.** Brief description. Key questions: "Who's there? What's happening? How do you feel? What do you need?" NOT exposure — stop at emotional contact point. Patient need not relive full trauma.
5. **Introduce helping figure.** Three types depending on healthy adult strength: (a) therapist enters scene, (b) third-party helper (real or fictional), (c) patient as own healthy adult. Sequence across therapy: a then b then c. Joint rescripting as intermediate step.
6. **Rescript.** Any creative solution is permitted. Limits of reality do not apply. The helping figure MUST win. Aggressive perpetrator = dramatic means (police, weapons, killing if needed). Guilt-inducing parent = explain illness, relieve child of responsibility, arrange professional help. Monitor child's feelings throughout. "Rewind the tape" if solution doesn't work.
7. **Deepen safety and attachment.** Nature, play, safe family scenes. Focus on sensory details. May take child to therapist's (fantasized) home/family for severe cases.
8. **Optional transfer.** Re-enter current situation with new emotional resources. Skip for severely disordered patients.

**Key rules:**
- Therapist should NOT ask permission to enter scene — just step in
- Patient's coping-mode solutions (e.g., fleeing, daydreaming) should be redirected
- Aggressive rescripting allowed: Arntz et al. (2007) showed increased anger control, not disinhibition
- If patient dislikes extreme action (e.g., killing) — rewind and try alternative
- Sedative medications may block effectiveness (Giesen-Bloo et al., 2006)
- Record exercises for patient to listen at home

### 2. Chair Work Protocols

**Two-chair dialogue (coping mode exploration):**
- One chair = coping mode, one chair = external perspective / healthy adult
- Patient takes coping mode's seat, therapist interviews the mode: "Why does [patient] need you? When did you start? What do you accomplish?"
- Add empty chair for vulnerable child — ask coping mode how child feels
- Goal: validate function, then surface interpersonal costs

**Three-chair dialogue (parent mode reduction):**
- Chairs: punitive/demanding parent, vulnerable child, healthy adult
- Start with vulnerable child or healthy adult (NOT punitive parent)
- Patient sits on punitive parent chair only briefly — 2-3 sentences maximum
- Therapist models healthy adult: argues with parent mode, protects child, may throw parent chair out of room
- Patient gradually takes over healthy adult role across sessions

**Chair dialogue adaptation by parent mode type:**
- **Achievement-demanding:** Discussion/negotiation; patient often resolves it; therapist may provoke from demanding chair to strengthen patient's healthy adult
- **Guilt-inducing:** Focus on responsibility attribution; patient's right to set limits; address parentification
- **Punitive (severe):** Firm confrontation; cut mode short; deprecate it; put chair out of room; record anti-punitive message for patient's phone

**Surrogate materials:**
- Playmobil figures for patients too scared for chairs (especially initial BPD work)
- Glove puppets, toy blocks, tokens
- Patient reports from usual chair without sitting on mode chairs
- Progress to real chairs over time — chairs produce stronger emotional activation

### 3. Schema Flashcard

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| Current feeling | Emotion + trigger |
| Mode identification | Which mode + biographical background |
| Typical coping reaction | Avoidance / overcompensation / surrender + specific behaviors |
| Reality testing | Negative cognition vs. healthy cognition + proofs |
| Alternative behaviors | Dysfunctional urge vs. healthy alternative |

### 4. Empathic Confrontation

Formula: validate biographical origin + name the mode + describe interpersonal impact + invite reflection. "I understand that [mode] was very important for you as a child because [biography]. However, right now I notice that [interpersonal effect]. I wonder whether [mode] might be getting in the way of [goal]?"

### 5. Pro-and-Con Lists for Coping Modes

Always start with pros (validates the mode, reduces defensiveness). Collect all pros before approaching cons. Return to pros/cons repeatedly throughout therapy — one discussion is never sufficient.

### 6. Limited Reparenting in Practice

- Warmth, validation, emotional support for vulnerable child mode
- Transitional objects (teddy bears, postcards with personal messages)
- Audio recordings of therapist's messages to child mode
- Email contact as low-threshold connection
- Session recordings for homework
- Physical contact: cautious, culture-appropriate (handshake, occasional hug); never erotic; therapist's own boundaries respected
- In IR: offer everything you would offer your own children (hugging, protecting, alternative family); this is restricted to imagery, not reality

### 7. Behavioral Pattern Breaking

- Social skills training (with video feedback)
- Homework assignments linked to mode model
- Exposure with response prevention (for OCD symptoms as coping mode)
- DBT skills as temporary bridge for self-injury/dissociation (not as primary intervention)
- Positive activity scheduling (link to happy child mode)
- Self-reinforcement for discipline (reward compliance, withhold pleasant activities for non-compliance)
- Operant conditioning for lack of discipline (e.g., next appointment only after homework completed)

## BPD-Specific Protocol

Arntz's primary research area. The BPD mode model (Young et al., 2003; manualized in Arntz & van Genderen, 2009) is the most empirically validated:

**Four core BPD modes:**
1. **Abandoned/abused child** — intense abandonment fear, threat, helplessness
2. **Angry/impulsive child** — rage at unfair treatment + impulsive need fulfillment
3. **Punitive parent** — extreme self-hatred, self-devaluation (the signature BPD mode)
4. **Detached protector** — emotional shutdown, substance abuse, medication misuse, binge eating, dissociation, fantasy worlds

**Treatment phases:**
- **Phase 1 (up to 1 year):** Work primarily with coping modes (detached protector). Build therapeutic relationship. Empathic confrontation. Introduce imagery work gradually (open eyes, time limits, positive imagery only at first).
- **Phase 2:** Imagery rescripting for childhood trauma. Therapist as primary helping figure. Chair dialogues against punitive parent mode.
- **Phase 3:** Transfer healthy adult role to patient. Behavioral pattern-breaking. Establish healthy relationships outside therapy.

**Duration:** 2-3 years recommended, starting at 2x/week sessions.

**Key BPD-specific techniques:**
- All sessions recorded; patient listens as homework
- Ventilate anger before accessing vulnerable child mode
- Imagery rescripting may need to include taking child to therapist's (fantasized) family
- Punitive parent chair thrown out of room (literally)
- Distinguish emotional dependency (OK temporarily) from functional dependency (address actively)
- Safe-place imagery often fails for BPD — skip it; make the IR exercise itself the safe place
- Medication (especially sedatives) reduces effectiveness — minimize
- BPD + dependent traits: differentiate and address dependency only when it blocks progress, not at start

**Evidence base:**
- Giesen-Bloo et al. (2006): Outpatient ST superior to TFP for BPD (RCT, Archives of General Psychiatry)
- Farrell et al. (2009): Group ST effective for BPD outpatients (RCT)
- Nadort et al. (2009): Crisis support outside office hours does not improve outcomes
- Arntz et al. (2007): IR superior to imaginal exposure for PTSD on anger, guilt, shame

## Exam-Ready Extracts

> **EXAM PEARL 1:** Schema therapy distinguishes 18 schema modes across 4 categories: dysfunctional child modes, dysfunctional parent modes (punitive + demanding), dysfunctional coping modes (surrender, avoidance, overcompensation), and healthy modes (healthy adult + happy child). Young (2003) originally defined only 10.

> **EXAM PEARL 2:** The three coping styles — surrender, avoidance, and overcompensation — map directly onto coping mode categories. Surrender = compliant surrender mode. Avoidance = detached protector, avoidant protector, angry protector, detached self-soother. Overcompensation = self-aggrandizer, attention-seeker, overcontroller, bully & attack, conning/manipulative, predator.

> **EXAM PEARL 3:** Imagery rescripting is NOT exposure therapy. The patient contacts the traumatic emotion but does NOT relive the full trauma. Rescripting begins as soon as emotional contact is made. Arntz et al. (2007) showed IR was superior to imaginal exposure on guilt, shame, and anger control in PTSD.

> **EXAM PEARL 4:** The BPD mode model has four core modes: abandoned/abused child, angry/impulsive child, punitive parent, and detached protector. The detached protector is the primary coping mode and the first target in treatment (often for up to 1 year before imagery work begins).

> **EXAM PEARL 5:** Limited reparenting is the therapeutic relationship model in ST. Nadort et al. (2009) demonstrated that crisis phone support outside office hours adds no benefit for BPD patients. This was removed from the protocol.

> **EXAM PEARL 6:** In chair dialogues, the patient should use second person ("You are wrong") when addressing the punitive parent mode, to maintain distance. The punitive parent is named differently from the patient (e.g., "the inquisitor," "the punitive father"). The patient should NOT sit on the punitive parent chair for more than 2-3 sentences in severe cases.

> **EXAM PEARL 7:** Three types of helping figures in imagery rescripting correspond to healthy adult mode strength: (1) therapist enters scene (weak HA), (2) third-party helper — real or fictional (intermediate), (3) patient as own healthy adult (strong). The sequence across therapy should progress from therapist to third party to patient's own healthy adult.

> **EXAM PEARL 8:** Differentiating anger-related modes: angry child = impulsive, childlike, triggered by unmet needs, patient wants relationship repair; angry protector = secondary emotion creating distance, wall-like quality; bully & attack = controlled intimidation, therapist feels threatened/dominated. Use countertransference to distinguish.

> **EXAM PEARL 9:** Giesen-Bloo et al. (2006) found that concurrent psychotropic medication (especially sedatives/benzodiazepines) significantly reduced ST effectiveness in BPD, likely by blocking the emotional intensity required for imagery rescripting and other experiential interventions.

> **EXAM PEARL 10:** The "affect bridge" connects current emotional distress to childhood memory: "Wipe out the current scene but keep the feeling. Let a childhood memory come up." If the childhood image is pleasant, it likely represents a coping mode (e.g., self-soothing, self-sacrifice) — explore further by disrupting the scene.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 1:** When the punitive parent chair is thrown out of the room during chair work, NEVER bring it back in, even if the patient feels lonely. Instead, comfort the vulnerable child mode and help patient identify real supportive relationships. Loneliness = vulnerable child mode, not a reason to restore the punitive parent.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 2:** If a patient refuses the therapist entering the imagery rescripting scene, do NOT ask permission — just step in. Patients who seem to reject help often experience relief once the therapist is present. If rejection persists, explore whether it is the punitive parent mode (loyalty/fear) rather than the vulnerable child mode that is refusing.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 3:** Emotional dependency on the therapist is acceptable in the early-to-mid phase of BPD treatment. Functional dependency (needing the therapist for every practical step) must be confronted. Use the distinction between emotional and functional dependency when navigating this dilemma.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 4:** Therapists' own punitive parent modes (e.g., "I must never hurt my patient," "My patient must always feel good in session") block necessary confrontation. If you consistently avoid confronting coping modes, reflect on whether your own guilt-inducing parent mode — often rooted in being a parentified child — is interfering.

> **MNEMONIC 1: CAVE** — Coping modes Are Validated Early (always start with pros before cons when discussing coping modes).

> **MNEMONIC 2: TTP** — Three Types of helping Persons in IR: Therapist, Third-party, Patient's own healthy adult (in order of increasing healthy adult strength).

> **MNEMONIC 3: FIGHT** — For punitive parent chair work: Feel the child's pain, Identify the parent mode source, Get therapist on healthy adult chair, Hit back at punitive messages (firmly), Throw the chair out of the room.

> **MNEMONIC 4: SAFE** — Schema mode Assessment requires Four Elements: (1) Self-report questionnaires (SMI), (2) Affect observation in session, (3) Feedback from patient about interaction patterns, (4) Exploration of biographical origins.
