# KB: Psychiatric Interviewing -- The Art of Understanding
> Shawn Christopher Shea, M.D. | 2nd Edition (1998) | W.B. Saunders Company

## Core Framework

Shea's text is an integrative, technique-focused guide to the initial psychiatric interview. It is not tied to any single school of psychotherapy. Instead, it synthesizes analytic, interpersonal, self-psychological, cognitive-behavioral, and existential perspectives into a unified, practical approach to the first clinical encounter.

**Central thesis:** The initial interview is a creative act governed by two complementary principles -- (1) powerful engagement of the patient, and (2) thorough, valid data gathering in a limited time. These are not in tension; when done sensitively, thorough data gathering *is* effective engagement.

**What makes this text unique:**
- **Facilics** -- Shea's original system for studying and understanding how clinicians structure interviews and manage time. Provides a visual schematic language for supervision.
- **The CASE Approach** (Chronological Assessment of Suicide Events) -- A structured, four-region method for eliciting suicidal ideation that is the most operationalized suicide interview protocol in the literature.
- **Validity techniques** -- A catalogue of specific questioning strategies (behavioral incident, shame attenuation, gentle assumption, symptom amplification, denial of the specific, normalization, exaggeration, induction to bragging) designed to maximize the accuracy of sensitive disclosures.
- **Process over content** -- The text teaches clinicians to attend to the *how* of interviewing (blending, gates, regions, expansions) rather than just the *what*.

**Organization:**
- Part I: Fundamentals of Interviewing (Chapters 1-4)
- Part II: The Interview and Psychopathology (Chapters 5-7)
- Part III: Advanced Techniques of Interviewing (Chapters 8-10)
- Appendices: Annotated 60-minute intake, write-up strategies, oral boards tips

## Chapter Digest

### Chapter 1: Interviewing -- The Principles Behind the Art

Defines the interview as a verbal and nonverbal dialogue between two participants whose behaviors affect each other's communication patterns. Seven goals of the assessment interview are enumerated: (1) establish therapeutic alliance, (2) collect valid database, (3) develop compassionate understanding, (4) make tentative diagnosis, (5) formulate treatment plan, (6) decrease patient anxiety, (7) instill hope and ensure return.

Key concepts: **Engagement** (ongoing development of safety and respect) vs **blending** (behavioral/emotional clues that engagement is proceeding). Blending is assessed via three methods: subjective (clinician's felt sense), objective (DOU, RTL, interruption patterns), and patient self-report. "Unipolar blending" occurs with histrionic/hypomanic patients -- superficially good but one-sided.

**Empathy cycle** (Barrett-Lennard): Five phases -- patient expresses feeling, clinician recognizes it, clinician conveys recognition, patient receives it, patient provides feedback. Breakdowns can occur at each phase. Rogers' distinction between empathy and identification is stressed. Empathic statements can paradoxically disengage guarded patients.

**Validity and reliability:** Introduces the behavioral incident (Pascal/Jenkins) as the foundational validity technique. Type A errors = verbal content errors (cannon questions, negative questions, omitting behavioral incidents). Type B errors = nonverbal biasing. "Reliably invalid" questioning is a persistent clinician habit.

**Understanding the patient:** Three frameworks -- interpersonal perspective (Sullivan/Whitehorn), parataxic distortion, and phenomenological perspective. Core pains driving patient behavior: isolation, self-loathing, fear of the unknown, fear of loss of internal control, fear of loss of external control, loss of meaning, fear of physical pain.

### Chapter 2: Dynamic Structure of the Interview

The interview has five phases: (1) Introduction, (2) Opening, (3) Body, (4) Closing, (5) Termination. The opening phase (5-7 minutes) is the cornerstone of engagement -- 30-90% of clinician verbalizations should be open-ended.

**Scouting period** (Introduction + Opening, ~6-9 minutes): Clinician assesses four areas using the acronym **PACE** -- Patient's perspectives and conscious agenda, Assessment of mental status, Clinician's perspective and unconscious agenda, Evaluation of the interview itself.

**Three problematic interview types:** shut-down, wandering, and rehearsed. Each requires specific interventions -- shut-down requires series of open-ended questions and gentle commands; wandering requires focusing statements; rehearsed requires behavioral incidents and affective engagement.

**Facilics** -- the study of interview structure. Three core concepts:
- **Regions** (content regions: 10 standard areas including HPI, diagnostic, social history, family history, lethality, etc.; process regions: free facilitation, resistance, psychodynamic)
- **Expansions** (stilted vs blended -- blended expansions feel conversational and maintain engagement)
- **Gates** (transitions between regions): spontaneous, natural, referred, implied, phantom. Natural gates use cue statements from the patient. Referred gates refer back to earlier patient statements -- powerful for entering sensitive regions like the cognitive mental status. Manufactured gates set up a natural gate deliberately.

**Time management:** Begin structuring after the scouting period (~8 minutes in). By end of second 15 minutes, most content regions should be touched. The "dead quarter" (second 15 minutes) is where clinicians most often lose structure.

**Closing phase:** Requires adequate time (5-10 minutes). Includes psychoeducation, treatment planning, medication introduction (if applicable), asking for patient feedback, and ensuring the patient will return.

### Chapter 3: Nonverbal Behavior -- The Interview as Mime

Communication is ~10% words and 90% "hidden cultural grammar" (Edward T. Hall). Covers kinesics (body movement), proxemics (interpersonal space), and paralanguage (how words are spoken -- tone, pitch, rate, volume).

Key concepts: displacement activities (self-touching, fidgeting) as anxiety markers; the RZ (reaction zone) -- interpersonal distance that varies by culture and psychopathology; gaze behavior and its relationship to engagement and deception; the clinician's own body language as a powerful tool or impediment.

Practical applications: note-taking should be minimized during sensitive topics (especially suicide inquiry); clinician should maintain comfortable, open posture; matching or mirroring patient's nonverbal behavior can enhance blending; sudden changes in patient's nonverbal behavior (gaze aversion, displacement activities) signal important material.

### Chapter 4: Assessment Perspectives -- Pathways to Effective Treatment Planning

Introduces the DSM-IV multiaxial system as an organizing framework for the database. Emphasizes that the DSM is a tool, not a straitjacket. Five axes explained with case application.

**Framework for meaning:** Every patient operates from a unique framework of values, beliefs, and meaning structures that must be understood for effective treatment planning. Crisis intervention is fundamentally about understanding the patient's perspective on their crisis.

**Family systems perspective:** The patient exists within a dyadic, triadic, and broader system context. Patterns of interaction, roles, boundaries, and communication styles within the family system inform both the presenting problem and the treatment plan.

**Core pains** (revisited and expanded): The fundamental human pains that drive psychopathology and resistance -- isolation, self-loathing, fear of the unknown, fear of loss of internal control, fear of loss of external control, loss of meaning, physical pain. Understanding which core pain is active in a patient at any moment provides the key to engagement and to understanding resistance.

### Chapter 5: Interviewing Techniques in Depression and Other Mood Disorders

Section 1: Detailed approach to differential diagnosis of depression by DSM-IV criteria using blended expansions. Demonstrates how to explore neurovegetative symptoms (sleep, appetite, energy, concentration, libido, psychomotor changes) through natural conversational flow rather than checklist questioning. Covers bipolar disorder screening -- using spontaneous gates from depression exploration to probe for manic episodes.

Section 2: Phenomenology of depression and its ramifications for treatment planning. Discusses the lived experience of depression, the impact on the patient's framework for meaning, and the importance of understanding the patient's explanatory model.

### Chapter 6: Interviewing Techniques While Exploring Psychosis

Section 1: Techniques for exploring hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder, and other psychotic phenomena. Key principle: enter the patient's psychotic world with genuine curiosity rather than challenging it. Use phenomenological exploration to understand the quality, content, and emotional impact of psychotic experiences.

Critical psychotic processes requiring evaluation for lethality: (1) command hallucinations (must assess loudness, frequency, duration, content, degree of hostility, degree patient feels compelled to obey), (2) alien control, (3) religious preoccupation ("Abraham syndrome").

Section 2: Phenomenological understanding of psychotic experience. Schizophrenia "rapes the soul" -- the core pains of losing internal control and loss of meaning are paramount. Patients with schizophrenia most often attempt suicide not during active psychosis but during devastating demoralization.

### Chapter 7: Personality Disorders -- Reflections of the Social History

The social history is the primary vehicle for uncovering personality pathology. Detailed exploration of interpersonal patterns, occupational history, legal history, substance use, sexual history, and behavioral patterns reveals characterological dysfunction more reliably than direct questioning about personality traits.

**Validity techniques** are introduced in full detail here (see dedicated section below). The chapter demonstrates strategic use of shame attenuation, gentle assumption, symptom amplification, denial of the specific, normalization, exaggeration, and induction to bragging in the context of exploring antisocial behavior, substance abuse, sexual history, domestic violence, and other sensitive areas.

**Empathic approaches to difficult histories:** The clinician creates a safe environment for disclosure through unconditional positive regard, strategic normalization, and careful attention to the patient's shame responses. When shame is detected, it should be addressed directly -- ignoring it risks losing the patient from treatment entirely.

### Chapter 8: Exploring Suicidal and Homicidal Ideation

The most extensively revised chapter in the 2nd edition. Two-stage framework: (1) setting the platform for suicide inquiry, (2) eliciting suicidal ideation using the CASE Approach.

**Risk factors** reviewed through two case presentations (Mr. Jamison and a second case). Statistical indicators: sex (men 3:1 completed), age (bimodal in men -- elderly and late adolescent peaks), psychotic process, substance abuse, recent discharge, absence of spouse, lack of social supports, recent losses, previous attempts, family history, anxiety/agitation, hopelessness.

**The Triad of Lethality:** (1) a serious suicide attempt, (2) a high degree of current suicidal intent, (3) a lack of deterrents (supports, reasons for living).

**CASE Approach** detailed in full (see dedicated section below).

**Homicidal ideation:** Similar principles apply. The Tarasoff duty to warn/protect is discussed. Assessment includes exploring the specificity of the threat, availability of means, history of violence, substance use, and psychotic processes that might fuel violence.

### Chapter 9: The Mental Status and Vantage Points -- Bridges to Psychotherapy

Introduces the concept of "vantage points" -- different conceptual lenses the clinician can adopt during the interview. Two types:

**Attentional vantage points:** Where the clinician directs attention -- the patient's verbal content, the patient's nonverbal behavior, the clinician's own internal reactions, or the interviewing process itself. "Self-remembering" (Gurdjieff) -- the practice of periodically pulling back from the interview to observe both the patient and oneself.

**Conceptual vantage points:** Theoretical frameworks applied to understand the patient -- psychodynamic (drives, defenses), interpersonal (Sullivan), behavioral, existential, and structural (Kernberg). The structural interview (Kernberg) uses clarification, gentle confrontation, and interpretation to trigger anxiety and observe the resulting defense patterns -- neurotic defenses (rationalization, intellectualization) vs borderline defenses (splitting, projection, devaluation) vs psychotic defenses (thought disorganization, regression).

**Intuitive vantage point:** Intuition is not a gift but "the spontaneous appearance of past knowledge whose acquisition the student has forgotten." Fostered by fascination, openness, and playfulness. Can be suppressed by over-analytical interviewing. The triad of intuition: fascination + openness + playfulness. "The surest sign that one is thinking too much or trying too hard is the feeling that the interview is strained."

### Chapter 10: The Art of Moving with Resistance

Resistance is not a demon but an "odd ally" offering a pathway to understanding. The chapter provides a language for analyzing and transforming resistance.

**Observable resistance:** Verbal, nonverbal, or mixed. Must be detected through blending assessment and self-remembering.

**Seed resistances:** The underlying core pains (fear of the unknown, loss of control, etc.) that generate observable resistances. If observable resistances keep recurring, address the seed resistance.

**Direction of response:** Clinician can move "with" or "against" resistance. Generally, moving with the resistance resolves it more effectively. Strongly opposing resistance provokes the patient to "dig in."

**Three approaches for transforming resistance:** (1) attend to content, (2) attend to process (metacommunication), (3) attend to the therapeutic relationship. Specific techniques: providing information to reduce fear of the unknown, giving control back to the patient, addressing seeds directly, normalizing the patient's experience, well-timed humor.

**Common resistances addressed:** the hostile patient, the tearful patient, the seductive patient, the silent patient, the "I want a different therapist" patient, the patient who asks personal questions, and the involuntary patient.

## Interviewing Techniques

### Engagement and Rapport

**Opening techniques:**
- Warm, unhurried greeting with appropriate handshake
- Ask patient how they prefer to be addressed (first name vs last name)
- Describe what to expect: "Perhaps it would be of value to describe what we'll be doing today"
- Address referral source transparently: "Your wife had called earlier... were you aware she had called ahead?"
- Give initial control to patient: "Perhaps we could start with your telling me a little about how you see things at this point"

**Empathic statements:**
- Complex: "Sounds like you've been going through a lot"
- Simple: "I can see that this is really painful for you"
- Reflection: "What has it been like for you to share such complicated material today?"
- Reinforcing: "You've done an excellent job of sharing difficult material. It's really helping me to understand what you've been experiencing"
- Caution: Empathic statements may disengage guarded patients -- use behavioral incidents instead

**Facilitation:**
- "Uh-huh," "Go on," "I see," head nods
- Generally effective but can backfire with hostile patients
- Avoid during sensitive disclosures -- silence and attentive presence are more powerful

**Verbal/nonverbal integration:**
- Minimize note-taking during sensitive topics
- Maintain open body posture
- Attend to displacement activities as anxiety markers
- Match speaking pace and energy level to patient
- The clinician's tone of voice during sensitive questions should be identical to tone during routine questions

### Structuring the Interview

**Phases:** Introduction (1-2 min) -> Opening (5-7 min) -> Body (30-35 min) -> Closing (5-10 min) -> Termination (1-2 min)

**PACE assessment (scouting period):**
- **P**atient's perspectives and conscious agenda
- **A**ssessment of mental status
- **C**linician's perspective and unconscious agenda
- **E**valuation of the interview itself

**10 Content regions:**
1. History of the present illness
2. Diagnostic explorations (DSM-IV criteria)
3. Patient's perspective
4. Formal mental status examination
5. Social history
6. Family history
7. Suicidal/homicidal potential
8. Past psychiatric history
9. Developmental and psychogenetic history
10. Medical history

**3 Process regions:**
1. Free facilitation regions (nondirective listening)
2. Resistance regions (actively decreasing specific resistance)
3. Psychodynamic regions (exploring how/why patient responds)

**Five types of gates:**
1. **Spontaneous gate:** Patient pivots to new topic; clinician follows
2. **Natural gate:** Clinician uses patient's cue statement to transition to new region -- "Have you ever used anything like a nightcap to sort of knock yourself out?" (from insomnia to drug/alcohol)
3. **Referred gate:** Clinician refers back to earlier patient statement to enter new region -- "Earlier you had mentioned problems with concentration..."
4. **Implied gate:** Transition is implied by the clinician's question without explicit reference
5. **Phantom gate:** Abrupt, non-sequitur transition -- generally disengaging and to be avoided

**Manufactured gate:** Clinician deliberately steers conversation to create a natural gate into a sensitive region (e.g., guiding from depression to alcohol to violence/homicide).

**Time management principles:**
1. Before interview: determine which content regions are needed
2. During interview: monitor progress every 5-10 minutes
3. Avoid overuse of facilitation regions in the body
4. Begin structuring after scouting period (second 15 minutes)
5. Inadequately structured second quarter forces rigid catch-up later
6. Once in a content region, expand it fully before moving on
7. Exception: follow pivot points into highly charged material (suicidal ideation, incest, trauma)

### Validity Techniques

**Behavioral Incident** (Pascal/Jenkins): The foundational technique. Ask for specific historical details rather than patient opinions. Two styles: (1) chronological unfolding ("What happened next?") and (2) specific concrete data ("Did you load the gun?"). Creates a "verbal videotape" of events. Especially critical in suicide assessment.

**Shame Attenuation:** Phrase questions so that affirmative answers stroke the ego rather than admit failure. Example: "Some people have problems holding their liquor, and others are pretty good at it. How about you, are you pretty good at holding your liquor?" The phrasing makes the problematic answer (heavy drinking) the non-shameful one. Must not condone behavior -- suspend judgment while allowing patient's perspective.

**Gentle Assumption:** Assume the suspected behavior is occurring and frame the question accordingly. "How frequently do you find yourself masturbating?" is more valid than "Do you masturbate?" Can be softened: "if at all." Contraindicated with easily led patients and children (risk of false memories). Powerful for suicide assessment: "What other ways have you thought of killing yourself?"

**Symptom Amplification:** Set the upper limit of a quantity so high that even when the patient minimizes, significant pathology is still revealed. "How many physical fights have you been in, 20, 30, 40?" Patient says "Oh no, maybe 10" -- clinician still knows there is a problem. Caveat: do not set absurdly high numbers that destroy credibility.

**Denial of the Specific:** Ask about specific items rather than generic categories, forcing the patient to deny each specific act. "Have you used coke?" "How about speed?" "Quaaludes or Valium?" "Marijuana?" Jogs memory, makes deception harder, and frequently uncovers material denied in response to generic questions.

**Normalization:** Frame the question so the patient realizes others have had similar experiences. "Frequently when people are feeling very upset, they have thoughts of killing themselves. Have you had any thoughts of wanting to kill yourself?" Reduces stigma. Structurally distinct from shame attenuation: normalization references what *other people* do; shame attenuation references the *patient's difficult circumstances*.

**Self-Normalization:** Variant where the clinician references their own hypothetical reactions. "I know that if I had learned that I had AIDS it would scare me a lot. I'm wondering if you are having some of those types of feelings?"

**Exaggeration** (Othmer & Othmer): When a patient displays inordinate shame over a minor disclosure, the clinician humorously exaggerates the behavior to absurd proportions, helping the patient gain perspective. Best used sparingly and with good timing. Technically an engagement technique more than a validity technique.

**Induction to Bragging** (Othmer & Othmer): Begin with a complimentary statement, then inquire about problematic behavior. "Well, you are obviously very big and clearly work out regularly, I bet you don't take any shit from anybody? How many fights have you actually been in?" Distinct from shame attenuation by the presence of a literal compliment.

## CASE Approach for Suicide Assessment

### Overview

The **Chronological Assessment of Suicide Events** (CASE) Approach is a structured, four-region interview strategy for eliciting suicidal ideation and determining the extent of suicidal planning and action. It is NOT a risk factor assessment tool and NOT a clinical decision-making tool. It is a **data-gathering method** -- how to get the information from which a clinical decision can be made.

**Core principle:** Organize the sprawling set of pertinent questions into four chronological regions, each with a well-defined body of information. Explore each region thoroughly before moving to the next.

**Four regions explored in order:**
1. Presenting suicidal event (if one exists)
2. Recent suicidal ideation and events (past 6-8 weeks)
3. Past suicidal events
4. Immediate suicidal ideation and future plans

**Key validity techniques used throughout:** behavioral incident, gentle assumption, symptom amplification, denial of the specific.

### Prerequisite: Setting the Platform (Stage 1)

Before asking about suicide, the clinician must create an atmosphere where the patient feels maximally safe while intensively involved in the painful emotions pushing toward suicide. Three primary **gates** into suicidal ideation:
1. **Psychotic process** (exploring delusions, hallucinations)
2. **Depression and hopelessness** (exploring depressive affect)
3. **Crisis, anger, or confusion** (exploring acute stressors)

The clinician leads the patient toward emotional involvement in one of these areas before asking about suicide. Do not "pop the question" -- lead up to it. If one gate fails, try a different one. If a patient spontaneously hints at suicide, follow it immediately.

**General principles:**
- Always use explicit words: "kill yourself," "commit suicide," "take your life"
- This metacommunication says: "It is okay to discuss thoughts of suicide with me"
- Do not accept the first "no" -- resistance issues may be at work
- Avoid negative questions: "You're not having thoughts of hurting yourself, are you?"
- No note-taking during suicide inquiry -- watch for nonverbal cues
- Myth debunked: asking about suicide does NOT give patients the idea

**Why patients resist discussing suicide:**
- Feels like a sign of weakness (shame)
- Feels immoral or sinful
- Feels like a taboo subject
- Fear clinician will think they are "crazy"
- Fear of being "locked up"
- Genuinely wants to die and does not want interference

### Step 1: Presenting Events Region

If the patient presents with a suicide attempt or gesture, create a **verbal videotape** of the event using behavioral incidents. Ask the patient to "walk you through" what happened, step by step.

**Information to gather:**
1. What method was used?
2. How serious was the action? (pills: which ones, how many; cutting: where, how deep)
3. What was the degree of intent to die? (tell anyone? isolated location? suicide note? will? said goodbye?)
4. How does the patient feel about surviving? ("What are some of your thoughts about the fact that you are still alive now?")
5. How well planned vs impulsive?
6. Role of alcohol/drugs?
7. Interpersonal factors? (failure, "people would be better off without me," revenge)
8. Specific stressors?
9. Degree of hopelessness at the time?
10. Why did the attempt fail? (How was patient found?)

**Strategy:** Most of this information surfaces naturally through behavioral incident technique -- ask "What happened next?" repeatedly through the event narrative.

### Step 2: Recent Events Region (Past 6-8 Weeks)

Explores the types and extent of suicidal thoughts and actions over the preceding weeks. Three sub-tasks:
1. Find out what plans have been contemplated
2. Determine how far the patient acted on these plans
3. Determine how much time is spent on suicidal thoughts daily

**Strategy 1 (Explore each method as uncovered):**
- Use **gentle assumption** to elicit the next method: "What other ways have you been thinking about?"
- For each method, use a series of **behavioral incidents** to determine extent of action: "Do you have a gun?" -> "Did you ever get it out with intention?" -> "Did you load it?" -> "Did you put it to your body?" -> "Did you take the safety off?" -> "What stopped you?"
- After gentle assumptions yield negatives, use **denial of the specific**: "Have you thought about cutting yourself?" "Hanging?" "Jumping?" "Carbon monoxide?"
- Each positive denial of the specific is followed by behavioral incidents
- Conclude: "Over the past 6-8 weeks, how much time daily do you think about killing yourself?"

**Strategy 2 (Generate full list first, then explore):**
- Use gentle assumptions + denial of the specific to generate complete list of methods first
- Then return to each method and explore with behavioral incidents
- Same concluding frequency question

**Key notation for supervision diagrams:**
- GA = Gentle assumption
- BI = Behavioral incident
- DS = Denial of the specific
- -- = Denies ideation

**Side benefit:** While describing actions via behavioral incidents, patients spontaneously discuss advantages and disadvantages of killing themselves, revealing which "way they are leaning."

**Maltsberger's views of death by suicide:**
1. Gateway to dreamless sleep (nothingness)
2. Reunion with lost person/thing
3. Escape from a persecutory enemy
4. Destruction of an internalized enemy
5. Passage to a better world
6. Revenge on someone through abandonment/self-destruction

### Step 3: Past Events Region

Time-limited exploration. Gather only information that could change the safety decision:
1. What was the most serious past attempt? (Is current method the same? Is the patient viewing current stressors the same way as during past attempt?)
2. Approximate number of past gestures/attempts?
3. When was the most recent attempt?

Do not spend excessive time here, especially with patients with extensive histories (e.g., borderline structure). Focus on decision-altering data.

### Step 4: Immediate Events Region

The single most powerful region. Focuses on: Where is the patient RIGHT NOW concerning suicidal intent?

**Questions to ask:**
- "Right now, are you having any thoughts about wanting to kill yourself?"
- "What would you do later tonight or tomorrow if you began to have suicidal thoughts again?"
- Explore current hopelessness vs. hope
- Evaluate support structure and design specific interventions
- Brainstorm concrete safety plans

**Safety contracting:**
- Provides NO guarantee of safety
- Main value is as an **assessment tool** -- watch the patient's face, body, and voice during the contracting process for signs of deceit or ambivalence
- More useful in established therapeutic relationships than first encounters
- If hesitancy is noted: "It looks like this is sort of hard for you to agree to, what's going on in your mind?"
- Avoid with borderline/passive-aggressive patients prone to manipulation around contracting
- Document quality of the contract: "Patient was able to make a sound safety contract showing good eye contact, genuine affect, and unhesitant tone of voice"

## Resistance and Difficult Patients

### Language of Resistance

**Observable resistance:** The visible/audible manifestation -- verbal ("I'm not talking to you"), nonverbal (arms crossed, gaze aversion), or mixed. Must be detected through blending assessment.

**Seed resistance:** The underlying core pain driving the observable resistance. Options include: fear of the unknown, fear of loss of external control, fear of loss of internal control, fear of rejection/judgment, isolation, self-loathing, loss of meaning, physical pain.

**Principle:** If observable resistances keep recurring despite intervention, address the seed resistance directly.

**Direction of response:** With (accepting, accommodating) vs Against (opposing, limit-setting). Generally, moving with resistance is more effective. Strongly opposing resistance invites a fight. However, some situations (especially with borderline pathology in ongoing therapy) require limit-setting.

### Strategies for Specific Situations

**Shut-down patient:** Use series of 5-6 consecutive open-ended questions or gentle commands. Do not revert to closed-ended or swing questions. Avoid sharpness of tone. Be patient -- after several open-ended questions, most patients begin to yield.

**Wandering patient:** Use focusing statements to redirect. Avoid reinforcing tangentiality with empathic statements or follow-up questions on tangential material. Natural gates can redirect. Behavioral incidents force reflection and disrupt rehearsed narratives.

**Hostile patient:** Address the hostility directly with curiosity, not defensiveness. "What specifically about this situation is upsetting you?" Explore seed resistances. Provide information. Give control. Do not match hostility.

**Patient who wants a different therapist:** Explore the concern without defensiveness. "What do you think an older clinician would be able to do?" Often the seed is fear of being misunderstood. Address the seed.

**Involuntary patient:** Acknowledge the coercion explicitly. Give whatever control is possible. "If something is too difficult to talk about, I want you to tell me. You don't have to talk about something that is too painful at present."

**Patient asking personal questions:** Consider the context carefully. With paranoid patients, personal disclosures can be twisted -- generally avoid. With most patients, brief appropriate disclosures can enhance engagement. The question is not "Should I answer?" but "What purpose would my answer serve?"

**Tearful patient:** Allow the tears. Do not rush to comfort or distract. Silence and attentive presence communicate safety. When the patient is ready, gently explore: "What were you feeling just now?"

## Motivational Interviewing Elements

Shea's text predates the formal MI literature but contains substantial overlap with MI principles:

- **Unconditional positive regard** (Rogers) is emphasized throughout as foundational to engagement
- **Normalization** functions similarly to MI's normalizing of ambivalence
- **Shame attenuation** parallels MI's strategy of reducing discrepancy gently
- The emphasis on patient perspective and agenda (PACE) aligns with MI's autonomy support
- The concept of "moving with resistance" directly parallels MI's "rolling with resistance"
- Behavioral incidents parallel MI's use of open questions to elicit change talk through specific examples
- The closing phase emphasis on patient education and collaborative treatment planning parallels MI's information-giving with permission

However, Shea does not discuss change talk, sustain talk, or the formal OARS framework.

## Exam-Ready Extracts

> **EXAM PEARL 1:** The CASE Approach explores suicidal ideation in four chronological regions: (1) Presenting events, (2) Recent events (6-8 weeks), (3) Past events, (4) Immediate events. The order is deliberate -- trust builds through regions 1-3, and the most critical assessment (immediate intent) comes last when the patient is most likely to be honest.

> **EXAM PEARL 2:** The behavioral incident is the foundational validity technique. Ask for specific facts, not opinions. "Did you load the gun?" is more valid than "Were you seriously thinking about it?" Clinicians who never use behavioral incidents are "reliably invalid."

> **EXAM PEARL 3:** Type A validity errors are verbal content errors (negative questions, cannon questions, omitting behavioral incidents). Type B validity errors are nonverbal biasing (shaking head while asking, looking away). Both systematically decrease the likelihood of obtaining accurate data.

> **EXAM PEARL 4:** Three methods of assessing blending: (1) Subjective -- clinician's felt sense of the interview quality, (2) Objective -- DOU (duration of utterance), RTL (reaction time latency), interruption patterns, (3) Patient self-report. "Unipolar blending" with histrionic/manic patients is artificially good and misleading.

> **EXAM PEARL 5:** Five types of gates (transitions between regions): spontaneous, natural, referred, implied, phantom. Natural gates and referred gates are the most useful. Phantom gates are abrupt transitions that damage engagement. Manufactured gates are deliberately engineered natural gates.

> **EXAM PEARL 6:** The scouting period (first 6-9 minutes) uses PACE: Patient's perspective, Assessment of mental status, Clinician's perspective of unconscious agenda, Evaluation of interview process.

> **EXAM PEARL 7:** Shame attenuation phrases the question so that the problematic answer is the non-shameful one. It is structurally distinct from normalization (which references what *other people* do) and from induction to bragging (which includes an actual compliment).

> **EXAM PEARL 8:** Safety contracts provide NO guarantee of safety. Their primary value in the initial interview is as an **assessment tool** -- observe the patient's nonverbal response during contracting for signs of ambivalence or deception.

> **EXAM PEARL 9:** Kernberg's structural interviewing uses gentle confrontation (presenting the patient's own contradictions) to observe defense patterns: neurotic defenses (rationalization, intellectualization) vs borderline defenses (splitting, projection, devaluation, entitlement) vs psychotic defenses (thought disorganization, regression under stress).

> **EXAM PEARL 10:** Three dangerous psychotic processes that require specific evaluation for lethality: (1) command hallucinations, (2) alien control, (3) religious preoccupation. The "Abraham syndrome" involves patients who believe God is testing their faith through commands to harm self or others.

> **EXAM PEARL 11:** In the CASE Approach recent events region, after gentle assumptions yield negatives, always follow up with denial of the specific for common methods (cutting, hanging, jumping, carbon monoxide). New methods are frequently uncovered at this stage despite earlier denial.

> **EXAM PEARL 12:** The "dead quarter" (second 15 minutes) is when clinicians most commonly lose interview structure. Failure to structure during this period forces rigid, disengaging catch-up later.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 1:** When a patient's opening speech includes "I'm not nuts" or similar disclaimers, listen for: (a) fear of being misunderstood, (b) a recognized problem being reframed as non-psychological, (c) a need to feel valued and heard, (d) possible stigma about mental health. Address these before proceeding with diagnostic exploration.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 2:** Ask "What are some of your thoughts about the fact that you are still alive now?" after a suicide attempt. The answer directly reveals current intent -- relief suggests decreased risk; disappointment or indifference suggests continued danger.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 3:** When introducing the cognitive mental status, use a referred gate to the patient's own complaints: "Earlier you mentioned problems with concentration. I'd like to ask some questions that can help us sort out the specific problems." This avoids the disengaging "Now I have to ask some silly questions."

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 4:** Exploring the sexual history: maintain identical nonverbal behavior as during the rest of the interview. "Sexual questions asked with a calm matter-of-factness usually result in matter-of-fact responses." Practice in front of a mirror or through role-play.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 5:** The closing phase question "What has this interview been like for you?" serves three functions: (a) provides constructive feedback, (b) metacommunicates respect and caring, (c) may uncover residual resistance or unaddressed concerns that could prevent the patient from returning.

> **MNEMONIC 1:** **PACE** -- Scouting period assessment: Patient's perspectives, Assessment of mental status, Clinician's perspective, Evaluation of interview process.

> **MNEMONIC 2:** **CASE** -- Chronological Assessment of Suicide Events: Presenting events, Recent events (6-8 weeks), Past events, Immediate events.

> **MNEMONIC 3:** **Gates** -- Five transition types (mnemonic: **S**ome **N**ice **R**eferred **I**mplied **P**hantom): Spontaneous (patient-initiated), Natural (clinician uses patient's cue), Referred (clinician refers to earlier statement), Implied (implied by question), Phantom (abrupt -- avoid).

> **MNEMONIC 4:** Validity techniques for sensitive material: **"She Gets Sick, Doesn't Need Exaggeration, Is Bragging"** -- Shame attenuation, Gentle assumption, Symptom amplification, Denial of the specific, Normalization, Exaggeration, Induction to Bragging.
