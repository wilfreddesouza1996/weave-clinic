# KB: Cognitive Behavior Therapy -- Basics and Beyond
> Judith S. Beck | Third Edition | 2021 | The Guilford Press

## Core Framework

### The Cognitive Model
The cognitive model proposes that dysfunctional thinking (which influences mood and behavior) is common to all psychological disturbances. When people learn to evaluate their thinking in a more realistic and adaptive way, they experience a decrease in negative emotion and maladaptive behavior. It is not a situation in and of itself that determines what people feel and do, but rather how they construe that situation.

**The fundamental sequence:**
```
Situation/Event --> Automatic Thoughts --> Reaction (emotional, behavioral, physiological)
```

**The more complex model (with beliefs):**
```
Core Beliefs
    |
Intermediate Beliefs (rules, attitudes, assumptions)
    |
Trigger Situation
    |
Automatic Thoughts
   / \
Emotion  Behavior
```

### Three Levels of Cognition
1. **Automatic thoughts** -- most superficial level; situation-specific words or images that pop into the mind spontaneously
2. **Intermediate beliefs** -- underlying assumptions, rules, and attitudes (e.g., "If I try to initiate relationships, I'll get rejected")
3. **Core beliefs** -- deepest, most fundamental beliefs about self, others, and the world (e.g., "I'm helpless"; "Others will hurt me"; "The world is dangerous")

### Historical Development
Aaron T. Beck, originally trained as a psychoanalyst, developed cognitive therapy in the 1960s-1970s. While investigating the psychoanalytic concept that depression results from hostility turned inward, he found depressed patients' dreams contained themes of defectiveness, deprivation, and loss -- not hostility. He recognized that his clients reported two types of thinking: a free-association stream and a stream of quick, evaluative "automatic" thoughts tied to emotions. When he helped clients identify, evaluate, and respond to these thoughts, they rapidly improved. The first randomized controlled trial (Rush et al., 1977) showed cognitive therapy was as effective as imipramine. Over 2,000 outcome studies now support CBT's efficacy.

### Recovery-Oriented Cognitive Therapy (CT-R)
A recent innovation by Aaron Beck and colleagues that adds emphasis on the cognitive formulation of clients' adaptive beliefs and behavioral strategies. CT-R focuses on clients' aspirations, values, strengths, personal qualities, skills, and resources rather than emphasizing symptoms and psychopathology. It has a more future-oriented time focus compared to traditional CBT's emphasis on problems from the past week.

## Chapter Digest

### Ch 1: Introduction to Cognitive Behavior Therapy (pp. 1-15)
Introduces the two main case examples: Abe (55, European heritage, severe depression, divorced, unemployed) and Maria (37, recurrent severe depression, borderline personality traits). CBT was developed by Aaron Beck in the 1960s-70s, originally named "cognitive therapy." The cognitive model proposes that people's thinking influences their emotions and behavior. Cognitions occur at three levels: automatic thoughts, intermediate beliefs, and core beliefs. CBT has been adapted across diverse populations, settings, and formats. Over 2,000 outcome studies demonstrate efficacy. CT-R adds emphasis on values, aspirations, strengths, and positive conclusions. Four stages of therapist development are outlined: (1) learning basic conceptualization and session structure, (2) integrating conceptualization with technique selection, (3) automatic integration of new data with refined hypotheses, (4) lifelong learning.

### Ch 2: Overview of Treatment (pp. 16-25)
Presents 14 core principles of CBT treatment: (1) treatment plans based on evolving cognitive conceptualization, (2) sound therapeutic relationship required, (3) continual progress monitoring, (4) culturally adapted and individualized, (5) emphasizes the positive, (6) stresses collaboration and active participation, (7) aspirational, values-based, and goal-oriented, (8) initially emphasizes the present, (9) educative, (10) time-sensitive, (11) sessions are structured, (12) guided discovery to respond to dysfunctional cognitions, (13) includes Action Plans (homework), (14) uses a variety of techniques to change thinking, mood, and behavior. CBT integrates techniques from ACT, DBT, mindfulness-based CT, schema therapy, and others within a cognitive framework.

### Ch 3: Cognitive Conceptualization (pp. 26-55)
The cognitive conceptualization is the cornerstone of CBT. It provides the framework for understanding clients, planning treatment, selecting interventions, and overcoming stuck points. Two types of Cognitive Conceptualization Diagrams (CCDs) are introduced: the Strengths-Based CCD (organizing adaptive cognitions, behaviors, and life history) and the Traditional CCD (organizing maladaptive patterns). Core beliefs fall into three categories: helplessness (ineffectiveness), unlovability (inability to gain/maintain love), and worthlessness (immorality/toxicity). Schemas are cognitive structures containing core beliefs. Information processing in depression involves immediately processing negative data while discounting or not noticing positive data. Intermediate beliefs (attitudes, rules, assumptions) link core beliefs to automatic thoughts. Coping strategies are patterns of normal behaviors that become problematic through inflexible overuse.

### Ch 4: The Therapeutic Relationship (pp. 56-70)
Four essential guidelines: (1) treat every client the way you'd want to be treated, (2) be a nice human being and help the client feel safe, (3) remember clients are supposed to pose challenges, (4) keep expectations reasonable. Key counseling skills: empathy, acceptance, validation, accurate understanding, inspiring hope, warmth, interest, positive regard, caring, encouragement, positive reinforcement, compassion, humor. Collaboration is a hallmark of CBT -- collaborative empiricism where therapist and client act as scientists examining evidence. Self-disclosure is used judiciously to strengthen the relationship, normalize difficulties, demonstrate CBT techniques, or model skills. Alliance ruptures are repaired by first asking whether the client is right, then modeling apology if so, or gently examining the cognition if not. Therapists must manage their own negative reactions through self-monitoring, CBT self-application, supervision, and good self-care.

### Ch 5: The Evaluation Session (pp. 71-86)
The evaluation session precedes the first therapy session and takes 1-2 hours. Six parts: (1) start the session -- set expectations and agenda, (2) conduct the psychosocial assessment -- including description of a typical day, (3) relate diagnostic impressions, set broad goals, and general treatment plan, (4) set an easy Action Plan, (5) establish expectations for treatment, (6) summarize and elicit feedback. Assessment areas include chief complaint, symptoms, mental status, diagnosis, medications, relationships, best lifetime functioning, and history. The evaluation also addresses hopelessness and skepticism, involves a trusted person when appropriate, and sets the foundation for the therapeutic alliance.

### Ch 6: The First Therapy Session (pp. 87-116)
The most important objective is to inspire hope. Structure: (1) mood/medication check, (2) set agenda, (3) update and Action Plan review, (4) discuss diagnosis and psychoeducation, (5) identify aspirations, values, and goals, (6) activity scheduling or work on an issue, (7) set new Action Plan, (8) summary, (9) feedback. Key psychoeducation elements: depression as a real illness (pneumonia analogy), depressed thinking as a symptom (black glasses metaphor, horse blinders metaphor), the cognitive model using client's own examples, and the treatment plan (changing thinking, working toward goals, learning skills). Values, aspirations, and goals are identified and an image of achieving aspirations is created to generate positive emotion. The "jack-in-the-box" metaphor is used to set realistic expectations about mood improvement.

### Ch 7: Activity Scheduling (pp. 117-134)
Essential for most depressed clients. Inactivity maintains depression through a vicious cycle: depressed mood leads to negative thoughts, which lead to inactivity, which prevents opportunities for mastery/pleasure/connection, which reinforces depression. Conversely, increased activity leads to positive thoughts and increased hopefulness. Activity Charts track activities hour-by-hour and can rate pleasure, mastery, and overall mood (0-10). Four categories of activities: self-care, connecting with people, managing at home, and recreation/entertainment. When clients resist, the therapist reminds them of their aspirations, makes a chart of "things that make me feel better vs. worse," frames scheduling as an experiment, and makes it a no-lose proposition. Comparing predicted vs. actual ratings of activities demonstrates the inaccuracy of negative predictions.

### Ch 8: Action Plans (pp. 135-159)
Action Plans (homework) are integral, not optional. Research shows clients who complete Action Plans progress significantly better. Types of Action Plan items: (1) reading therapy notes, (2) monitoring automatic thoughts, (3) evaluating and responding to automatic thoughts, (4) behavioral experiments, (5) disengaging from unhelpful thought processes (mindfulness), (6) steps toward goals, (7) mood-lifting activities, (8) credit lists, (9) practicing behavioral skills, (10) bibliotherapy, (11) preparing for the next session. Adherence is increased by: tailoring to the individual, providing rationale, collaborative setting, erring on the easy side, explicit instructions, reminder systems, beginning in session, and imagining completion. Covert rehearsal (imagining doing the Action Plan) identifies obstacles. When clients fail to complete Action Plans, conceptualize whether the obstacle is practical, cognitive, cognitive masked as practical, or therapist-related.

### Ch 9: Treatment Planning (pp. 160-173)
Three phases of therapy: (1) beginning -- build alliance, identify aspirations/values/goals, socialize to CBT, educate about cognitive model, schedule activities, teach automatic thought identification; (2) middle -- strengthen adaptive beliefs, more directly modify dysfunctional beliefs using intellectual and emotional techniques; (3) final -- prepare for termination, increase well-being, build resilience, prevent relapse. A treatment plan is based on diagnostic evaluation, cognitive formulation, conceptualization, client's aspirations and strengths, and obstacles to goals. Individual sessions are planned using an extensive set of questions covering pre-session review, mood check, weekly review, agenda setting, discussing agenda items (defining the issue, devising strategy, choosing techniques, monitoring the process), and post-session reflection.

### Ch 10: Structuring Sessions (pp. 174-191)
Standard session format: **Initial part** -- (1) mood/medication check, (2) set agenda, (3) update and review Action Plan, (4) prioritize agenda. **Middle part** -- (5) work on agenda item 1 (summarize, intervene, set Action Plan), (6) work on items 2-3 if time. **End** -- (7) summarize session, (8) review Action Plan, (9) elicit feedback. Preparing for Therapy Worksheet helps clients come prepared. In the update, start with positives ("When were you at your best this week?") to counteract the depression's negative focus. Get clients' attribution for improved mood to reinforce the cognitive model. Three types of summaries: content summaries (in cognitive model form), client summaries (checking understanding), and session summaries (tying threads together). The Feedback Form provides structured written feedback opportunity.

### Ch 11: Problems in Structuring Sessions (pp. 192-209)
Common problems include: clients attributing improvement to external factors only; too-detailed mood descriptions; mood worsening requiring crisis intervention; clients disliking structure; difficulty with agenda setting (vagueness, going off-topic, hopelessness/overwhelm); unfocused discussion; inefficient pacing; failing to make therapeutic interventions; and difficulty with problem solving. Solutions involve gentle interruption, redirection, forced-choice questions, flexibility with structure while gradually socializing clients, and using self as model ("If I had this problem, what would I do?"). When clients are distressed at session end, shift to lighter topics. Always address negative feedback about therapy.

### Ch 12: Identifying Automatic Thoughts (pp. 210-226)
Automatic thoughts are a stream of thinking coexisting with manifest thought. Characteristics: brief, often barely conscious, seem to spring up spontaneously, accepted uncritically. They can be about external situations or internal experiences (other thoughts, emotions, behaviors, physiological states). They occur before, during, and/or after situations. Clients are usually more aware of resulting emotions than the thoughts themselves. The cardinal question: "What was just going through my mind?" Extended cognitive model: situations include discrete events, thought streams, memories, images, emotions, behaviors, and physiological experiences. Forms of automatic thoughts include words, questions, images, and memories. When clients have difficulty identifying thoughts: use imagery recall, suggest a thought, give a multiple choice, have them imagine a similar situation, focus on bodily sensations, or use role play.

### Ch 13: Emotions (pp. 227-238)
CBT promotes positive emotions and addresses excessive negative emotions. It is important to differentiate thoughts from emotions. Emotions are typically one word (sad, angry, anxious); thoughts are ideas ("I feel like a failure" is a thought, not a feeling). The content of automatic thoughts matches specific emotions: loss/failure themes produce sadness; threat/danger themes produce anxiety; violation of rules/injustice themes produce anger. When the reported emotion doesn't match the thought content, investigate further. Techniques to regulate emotion: problem solving, evaluating thoughts, engaging in mindful activities, exercise, accepting emotion nonjudgmentally, mindfulness, relaxation, self-soothing, focusing on strengths. Some clients have dysfunctional beliefs about negative emotions (e.g., "If I get upset, I'll lose control") that must be addressed.

### Ch 14: Evaluating Automatic Thoughts (pp. 239-259)
Three kinds of thoughts relevant to therapy: (1) inaccurate thoughts leading to distress, (2) accurate but unhelpful thoughts, (3) thoughts that are part of dysfunctional thought processes (rumination, obsession, self-criticism). Socratic questioning involves multiple types of questions: evidence questions ("What's the evidence?"), alternative explanation questions, decatastrophizing questions ("What's the worst/best/most realistic outcome?"), impact questions ("What happens if you keep thinking this?"), distancing questions ("What would you tell a friend?"), and problem-solving questions. After evaluation, assess outcome: re-rate belief and emotion. If evaluation is ineffective, consider: more central thoughts not yet identified, superficial evaluation, core belief involved, intellectual but not emotional change, or unhelpful thought process. Additional techniques: identify cognitive distortions, design behavioral experiments, use self-disclosure, ask client for helpful response. When thoughts are true: problem-solve, investigate invalid conclusions, or work toward acceptance and valued action.

### Ch 15: Responding to Automatic Thoughts (pp. 260-272)
Therapy notes are composed collaboratively -- responses to automatic thoughts that clients read daily. Notes should be written in client's own words, read each morning, and pulled out as needed. The Testing Your Thoughts Worksheet and Thought Record (originally Daily Record of Dysfunctional Thoughts, Beck et al., 1979) help clients evaluate thoughts between sessions. The Thought Record includes columns for: date/time, situation, automatic thought(s), emotion(s), adaptive response, and outcome (re-rating of emotion and belief). Audio-recorded therapy notes are an alternative for clients who can't or don't like to read.

### Ch 16: Integrating Mindfulness into CBT (pp. 273-281)
Mindfulness is maintaining attention on immediate experience while being open, accepting, and curious. It is especially effective for clients engaged in rumination, worry, obsessive thinking, continual self-criticism, or avoidance of internal experiences. Integration into CBT involves: (1) identifying beliefs about the thought process (e.g., "Rumination will help me solve problems"), (2) evaluating those beliefs, (3) engaging in the unhelpful process in session to activate it, (4) doing a 5-minute mindfulness of the breath exercise, (5) re-rating distress, (6) drawing conclusions. The AWARE technique for anxiety: Accept the anxiety, Watch it from a distance, Act constructively with it, Repeat, Expect the best.

### Ch 17: Introduction to Beliefs (pp. 282-301)
Core beliefs are the most central ideas about self, others, and the world. Schemas are cognitive structures containing core beliefs. Modes are clusters of interrelated schemas. The adaptive mode has cognitive schemas of effectiveness/lovability/worth, motivational schemas promoting activity, affective schemas of hope and well-being, behavioral schemas of approach, and normal physiological schemas. The depressive mode has cognitive schemas of helplessness/unlovability/worthlessness, motivational schemas to conserve energy, affective schemas of sadness/hopelessness, behavioral schemas of avoidance/withdrawal, and physiological schemas of fatigue. Negative core beliefs about self fall into three categories: helplessness, unlovability, and worthlessness. Intermediate beliefs are identified using the downward arrow technique. Educating clients about beliefs uses the metaphor of a screen that filters information. Clients are motivated to change beliefs by examining advantages/disadvantages and conducting behavioral experiments.

### Ch 18: Modifying Beliefs (pp. 303-321)
**Strengthening adaptive beliefs:** elicit positive data and draw conclusions, examine advantages of the adaptive belief, point out meaning of positive data, reference other people, use Evidence Chart, induce images of current and historical experiences, act "as if." **Modifying maladaptive beliefs** (usually later in treatment): Socratic questioning, reframing (Reframes Chart), behavioral experiments, stories/movies/metaphors, cognitive continuum (0-100% scale between extremes), using others as reference point, self-disclosure, intellectual-emotional role plays, historical tests, restructuring meaning of early memories. Beliefs change at the intellectual level first; experiential techniques (imagery, role play, metaphors, behavioral experiments) are needed for emotional-level change. The Belief Change Worksheet consolidates evidence for adaptive belief and reframes of negative belief.

### Ch 19: Additional Techniques (pp. 322-339)
Techniques include: making decisions (advantages-disadvantages analysis with weighting), graded task assignments (staircase analogy for breaking down overwhelming goals), exposure (graded hierarchies with predicted vs. actual anxiety ratings, safety behaviors identification, imaginal exposure, daily exposure monitoring), role playing (for social skills, uncovering automatic thoughts, modifying beliefs), the "pie" technique (pie charts for goal-setting time allocation and causality attribution), and self-comparisons (teaching clients to compare present self to worst point rather than to others or pre-depression self).

### Ch 20: Imagery (pp. 340-352)
Imagery affects emotions more than verbal processes. Five positive imagery interventions: focusing on positive memories, rehearsing adaptive coping techniques, distancing (envisioning getting through a rough period), substituting positive images (changing the "channel"), and focusing on positive aspects of upcoming situations. Identifying negative images requires persistent questioning. Modifying negative images: changing the "movie" (client as director), following the image to completion (3 sub-techniques: coping with difficult experiences, jumping to near future, jumping to far future), and reality testing the image. The key insight is that the mind automatically stops spontaneous images at the worst point; continuing the image typically reveals the client can cope.

### Ch 21: Termination and Relapse Prevention (pp. 353-364)
Relapse prevention begins from session 1. Early: teach that recovery follows a non-linear path (graph resembling southern U.S. border -- "visiting Texas"). Throughout: attribute progress to client's own changes in thinking and behavior (not external factors or medication alone), teach lifelong skills, build resilience and well-being. Near termination: taper sessions collaboratively (weekly to biweekly to monthly), address concerns about tapering (advantages-disadvantages with reframes), conduct self-therapy sessions (structured guide provided), prepare for setbacks (identify early warning signs, create action plan for symptom recurrence), address reactions to termination (mixed feelings are normal), schedule booster sessions (3, 6, 12 months).

### Ch 22: Problems in Therapy (pp. 365-376)
Problems are uncovered through client feedback, periodic summaries, objective tracking, and session recording review. Conceptualization of problems considers: beliefs about self ("I'm incompetent/defective"), beliefs about therapist ("She can't help/doesn't care"), beliefs about therapy process ("This won't work"), client skill deficits, and therapist factors. Common problems: clients who want to talk only about problems (not goals), clients who are highly avoidant, clients who don't do Action Plans, therapists who fail to structure sessions, therapists who avoid addressing difficult topics, and therapeutic relationship ruptures. The guiding principle: view problems as opportunities to refine conceptualization. When stuck, consult the Cognitive Therapy Rating Scale for self-evaluation.

## Key Tables

### Cognitive Distortions (Full List)

| Distortion | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **All-or-nothing thinking** | Viewing a situation in only two categories instead of on a continuum | "If I'm not a total success, I'm a failure." |
| **Catastrophizing (fortune-telling)** | Predicting the future negatively without considering more likely outcomes | "I'll be so upset, I won't be able to function at all." |
| **Disqualifying/discounting the positive** | Telling yourself positive experiences, deeds, or qualities don't count | "I did that project well, but that doesn't mean I'm competent; I just got lucky." |
| **Emotional reasoning** | Believing something must be true because you "feel" it strongly, ignoring contrary evidence | "I know I do a lot of things okay at work, but I still feel like I'm a failure." |
| **Labeling** | Putting a fixed, global label on yourself or others | "I'm a loser"; "He's no good." |
| **Magnification/minimization** | Unreasonably magnifying the negative and/or minimizing the positive | "Getting a mediocre evaluation proves how inadequate I am. Getting high marks doesn't mean I'm smart." |
| **Mental filter (selective abstraction)** | Paying undue attention to one negative detail instead of the whole picture | "Because I got one low rating on my evaluation [with several high ratings], it means I'm doing a lousy job." |
| **Mind reading** | Believing you know what others are thinking without evidence | "He's thinking that I don't know the first thing about this project." |
| **Overgeneralization** | Making a sweeping negative conclusion beyond the current situation | "Because I felt uncomfortable at the meeting, I don't have what it takes to make friends." |
| **Personalization** | Believing others behave negatively because of you, without considering other explanations | "The repairman was curt to me because I did something wrong." |
| **"Should" and "must" statements** | Having a precise, fixed idea of how you or others should behave, overestimating how bad it is when expectations aren't met | "It's terrible that I made a mistake. I should always do my best." |
| **Tunnel vision** | Seeing only the negative aspects of a situation | "My son's teacher can't do anything right. He's critical and insensitive and lousy at teaching." |

### Levels of Cognition

| Level | Characteristics | Accessibility | Modifiability | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Automatic thoughts** | Situation-specific, brief, spontaneous, verbal or imagistic | Most accessible; client often unaware until trained | Most easily modified | "I can't do anything right" |
| **Intermediate beliefs** | Rules, attitudes, and conditional assumptions; not usually articulated | Moderate; can be elicited through downward arrow | Moderately modifiable | "If I ask for help, people will see I'm incompetent" |
| **Core beliefs** | Global, absolute, rigid; about self, others, world; operate as "truths" | Least accessible; often unarticulated even to self | Most difficult to modify; require multiple techniques over time | "I'm incompetent" / "I'm unlovable" / "I'm worthless" |

### Negative Core Beliefs About the Self (Three Categories)

| Category | Theme | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Helplessness** | Ineffectiveness in getting things done, self-protection, measuring up | "I'm incompetent, helpless, useless, needy"; "I'm powerless, weak, vulnerable, trapped"; "I'm inferior, a failure, a loser, defective" |
| **Unlovability** | Personal qualities resulting in inability to get/maintain love and intimacy | "I'm unlovable, unlikeable, undesirable, unattractive, boring, unwanted"; "I am different, defective, not good enough"; "I'm bound to be rejected, abandoned, alone" |
| **Worthlessness** | Immorality, toxicity, dangerousness | "I'm immoral, morally bad, worthless, unacceptable"; "I'm dangerous, toxic, crazy, evil"; "I don't deserve to live" |

### Adaptive Core Beliefs About the Self (Three Categories)

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Effective** | "I am reasonably competent, effective, in control, successful, and useful"; "I can reasonably do most things, protect myself, and take care of myself"; "I have strengths and weaknesses" |
| **Lovable** | "I am reasonably lovable, likeable, desirable, attractive, wanted, and cared for"; "I am okay, and my differences don't impair my relationships"; "I am good enough to be loved" |
| **Worthy** | "I am reasonably worthwhile, acceptable, moral, good, and benign" |

## Clinical Techniques Catalogue

### Socratic Questioning
**Purpose:** Guided discovery to help clients evaluate automatic thoughts and beliefs without challenging or disputing them.

**Key question types:**
1. **Evidence questions:** "What's the evidence that this thought is true? What's the evidence on the other side?"
2. **Alternative explanation:** "Is there another way of looking at this? Could there be another explanation?"
3. **Decatastrophizing:** "What's the worst that could happen? The best? The most realistic?"
4. **Impact questions:** "What's the effect of thinking this way? What could be the effect of changing your thinking?"
5. **Distancing:** "What would you tell a friend/family member in this situation?"
6. **Problem-solving:** "What would be good to do now?"
7. **Downward arrow (for beliefs):** "If that thought were true, what would it mean about you?"

### Guided Discovery
The therapist uses questions (rather than lectures or challenges) to help clients examine their thinking and arrive at their own conclusions. The stance is one of collaborative empiricism -- therapist and client together examine the evidence, like scientists testing hypotheses.

### Thought Records / Daily Record of Dysfunctional Thoughts
**Columns:**
1. Date/Time
2. Situation (What was happening? What triggered the thought?)
3. Automatic thought(s) (What went through my mind? Rate belief 0-100%)
4. Emotion(s) (What did I feel? Rate intensity 0-100%)
5. Adaptive response (What's the evidence? Is there another way to look at this?)
6. Outcome (Re-rate belief in automatic thought; re-rate emotion)

**Testing Your Thoughts Worksheet** -- a simpler version with questions guiding evaluation on the reverse side.

### Behavioral Activation / Activity Scheduling
- Schedule activities that provide mastery, pleasure, connection, and self-care
- Use Activity Chart (hourly schedule) to plan and track
- Rate activities on pleasure and mastery (0-10)
- Compare predicted vs. actual ratings to demonstrate inaccuracy of negative predictions
- Categories: self-care, connecting with people, managing at home, recreation
- "Jack-in-the-box" metaphor: improvement may require many "cranks" before mood lifts

### Cognitive Restructuring (Step-by-Step)
1. Identify the situation
2. Identify the automatic thought(s) and/or image(s)
3. Identify the emotion(s) and rate intensity
4. Evaluate the thought using Socratic questions
5. Develop an adaptive response
6. Re-rate belief in original thought and emotion intensity
7. Record the adaptive response in therapy notes for daily review

### Behavioral Experiments
- Collaboratively design experiments to test negative predictions
- Identify the specific prediction, design the test, predict the outcome, carry out the experiment, evaluate the result
- Draw adaptive conclusions about the experience and what it says about the client
- Can be conducted in session or between sessions
- More powerful than verbal techniques alone for changing beliefs at both intellectual and emotional levels

### Graded Exposure
- Create a hierarchy of avoided situations rated by predicted anxiety (0-100)
- Start with situations predicted at approximately 30% anxiety
- Engage in exposure daily if feasible
- Stay in situation until feared outcome doesn't happen
- Monitor and eliminate safety behaviors
- Use Exposure Monitor: date, activity, predicted anxiety, actual anxiety, predictions
- Imaginal exposure for situations too fearful or impractical for in vivo exposure

### Problem Solving
1. Identify and define the problem specifically
2. Generate multiple possible solutions (brainstorming)
3. Evaluate each solution (advantages/disadvantages)
4. Choose and implement a solution
5. Evaluate the outcome
6. If unsuccessful, try another solution

### Relaxation and Mindfulness Techniques
**Formal mindfulness (mindfulness of the breath):** 5-minute practice focusing on breathing sensations; when mind wanders, gently bring attention back without judgment. Record for home practice.

**Informal mindfulness:** Brief engagement of full awareness during daily activities.

**AWARE technique for anxiety:**
- **A**ccept the anxiety
- **W**atch it from a distance (rate 0-10, observe without judgment)
- **A**ct constructively with it (continue normal activity)
- **R**epeat the steps
- **E**xpect the best

### Action Plans / Homework Design
**Principles for effective Action Plans:**
1. Tailor to the individual (abilities, preferences, motivation, functioning level)
2. Provide or elicit the rationale (link to aspirations, values, goals)
3. Set collaboratively
4. Err on the easy side
5. Provide explicit instructions (when, where, how long, with whom)
6. Set up reminder systems
7. Begin in session when possible
8. Ask client to imagine completing the Action Plan
9. Check likelihood of completion (0-100%)
10. If less than 90%, identify and address obstacles
11. Make it a no-lose proposition initially

### Additional Key Techniques
- **Credit lists:** Running daily list of anything done that's even a little difficult
- **Advantages-disadvantages analysis:** Four-quadrant chart for decision-making
- **Cognitive continuum:** 0-100% scale to combat all-or-nothing thinking
- **Pie chart:** For causality attribution and time allocation
- **Self-comparisons:** Compare present to worst point, not to others or pre-illness self
- **Graded task assignments:** Break overwhelming tasks into manageable steps (staircase metaphor)
- **Role playing:** For social skills, uncovering thoughts, and modifying beliefs
- **Coping cards:** Physical or virtual cards with key adaptive responses to read when needed
- **Acting "as if":** Behave as if the adaptive belief is true
- **Intellectual-emotional role play:** Therapist argues the negative belief while client argues the adaptive belief, then switch

## Exam-Ready Extracts

> **EXAM PEARL:** The cognitive model proposes that it is not a situation itself that determines what people feel and do, but rather how they construe that situation. The fundamental sequence is: Situation --> Automatic Thought --> Reaction (emotional, behavioral, physiological).

> **EXAM PEARL:** Negative core beliefs about the self fall into three categories: helplessness (I'm incompetent, powerless, inferior), unlovability (I'm unlovable, undesirable, bound to be rejected), and worthlessness (I'm immoral, dangerous, don't deserve to live).

> **EXAM PEARL:** CBT has 14 principles of treatment. The most commonly tested: (1) based on evolving cognitive conceptualization, (2) requires sound therapeutic relationship, (6) stresses collaboration and active participation, (8) initially emphasizes the present, (11) sessions are structured, (12) uses guided discovery not challenging, (13) includes Action Plans (homework).

> **EXAM PEARL:** The standard CBT session structure: mood check --> set agenda --> update + Action Plan review --> prioritize agenda --> work on agenda items --> summarize --> review new Action Plan --> elicit feedback. This is sometimes abbreviated as the "bridge" from one session to the next.

> **EXAM PEARL:** Three types of automatic thoughts relevant to therapy: (1) inaccurate thoughts leading to distress (evaluate with Socratic questioning), (2) accurate but unhelpful thoughts (problem-solve or work toward acceptance), (3) thoughts that are part of dysfunctional processes like rumination or self-criticism (use mindfulness, evaluate beliefs about the process).

> **EXAM PEARL:** The Cognitive Conceptualization Diagram (CCD) organizes: relevant life history --> core beliefs --> intermediate beliefs (rules, attitudes, assumptions) --> coping strategies --> and three examples of situations with automatic thoughts, their meanings, emotions, and behaviors.

> **EXAM PEARL:** The "downward arrow technique" is used to identify intermediate and core beliefs by asking: "If that thought were true, what would it mean about you?" This question is asked repeatedly until the core belief is reached.

> **EXAM PEARL:** Collaborative empiricism is a hallmark of CBT -- therapist and client act as scientists, jointly examining evidence for and against the client's cognitions. CBT does not "challenge" thoughts; it helps clients assess accuracy and utility through guided discovery.

> **EXAM PEARL:** Schemas are cognitive structures containing core beliefs. In depression, negative schemas become activated and positive schemas become deactivated. This leads to biased information processing where negative data are immediately absorbed while positive data are discounted or unnoticed.

> **EXAM PEARL:** CT-R (Recovery-Oriented Cognitive Therapy) adds emphasis on aspirations, values, strengths, and positive conclusions to traditional CBT. It focuses more on future-oriented goals rather than past problems, cultivates positive emotions, and was originally developed for severe mental illness including schizophrenia.

> **EXAM PEARL:** Beck's original 1977 RCT with A. John Rush showed cognitive therapy was as effective as imipramine for depression, and the follow-up showed CT was more effective than imipramine in preventing relapse. Over 2,000 outcome studies now support CBT.

> **EXAM PEARL:** The diathesis-stress model in CBT: vulnerabilities (rigid values, biased information processing, genetic predisposition) interact with relevant stressors to activate latent negative core beliefs and trigger depression.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** When clients express hopelessness about treatment, use the pneumonia analogy: "If you had bacterial pneumonia, would you try to cure yourself? No, you'd go to a doctor. Depression is every bit as real as pneumonia, and we have effective treatment for it."

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** When depressed clients avoid activities, use the "jack-in-the-box" metaphor: some people crank it once and the clown pops out (feel better quickly); others need to crank many times. But you have to start somewhere.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** For clients who see everything through a negative lens, use the "black glasses" metaphor: depression is like wearing the blackest glasses imaginable, making everything look dark and negative. Treatment helps "scratch off the black paint."

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** The "horse blinders" metaphor: depression is like wearing blinders that restrict vision to only what's immediately ahead, and through the black glasses, it all looks terrible. Treatment opens up the blinders to see the full picture.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** When clients fear termination or setbacks, use the "southern U.S. border" graph: recovery isn't a straight line. Setbacks are normal -- they're just "visiting Texas." You'll continue through Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, then maybe "visit Florida," but you'll keep progressing toward "Maine."

> **MNEMONIC:** **AWARE** technique for anxiety management: **A**ccept the anxiety, **W**atch it, **A**ct constructively, **R**epeat, **E**xpect the best.

> **MNEMONIC:** **Three H's of negative core beliefs:** **H**elplessness (I can't cope), **H**eartless world (Unlovability -- I'm unlovable), **H**opeless character (Worthlessness -- I'm bad).

## Appendices Summary

### Appendix B: Beck Institute Case Write-Up (Abe)
Six-part structure: (1) identifying information and referral, (2) diagnostic data and functioning, (3) Cognitive Conceptualization Diagram, (4) case conceptualization summary (history, precipitants, maintaining factors, values/aspirations, narrative), (5) treatment plan (overall plan, problem list with evidence-based interventions), (6) course of treatment and outcome. Abe had 18 sessions over 8 months (12 weekly, then biweekly, then monthly). PHQ-9 dropped from 18 to 3; GAD-7 from 8 to 2; well-being from 1 to 8.

### Appendix C: AWARE Technique Steps
Accept, Watch, Act constructively, Repeat, Expect the best. Designed for excessive worry (Beck & Emery, 1985); adaptable for angry or depressive rumination.

### Appendix D: Restructuring Meaning of Early Memories
Two techniques: (1) Reenactment and therapist-client role play -- heighten affect with current situation, retrieve related childhood memory, discuss on intellectual level, then role-play with switched roles; (2) Older client-younger client role play -- the client's current self speaks to the younger self to reframe dysfunctional beliefs formed in childhood. Both are advanced techniques used more often with personality disorders, typically in the middle-to-end phase of treatment.
