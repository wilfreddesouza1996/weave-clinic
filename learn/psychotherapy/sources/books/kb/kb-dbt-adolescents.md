## Core Framework

### What DBT Is

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) was developed by Marsha M. Linehan in the late 1980s as the first empirically validated treatment for adults with chronic emotional and behavioral dysregulation, initially targeting suicidal behavior and borderline personality disorder. The treatment is built on a dialectical philosophy: that two opposing ideas can both be true simultaneously, and that holding them together creates a new synthesis. The central dialectic in DBT is the tension between **acceptance** (the client is doing the best they can) and **change** (the client needs to do better).

DBT is a comprehensive treatment package with four standard modes: **individual therapy**, **group skills training**, **between-session telephone coaching**, and a **therapist consultation team**. For adolescents, these modes are modified: skills training is conducted as a multifamily group (parents alongside teens), phone coaching is extended to parents as well, and family therapy sessions are added as needed. Each mode serves a distinct function, and they are intentionally kept separate to prevent crises from derailing skills acquisition.

### The Biosocial Model

DBT explains chronic emotional dysregulation through a **biosocial theory**: the transactional interaction over time between (1) a biological vulnerability to emotional dysregulation and (2) a pervasively invalidating environment.

**Biological vulnerability** consists of three features:
- **High emotional sensitivity** — emotions are triggered easily and by subtle cues
- **High emotional reactivity** — emotional responses are intense once triggered
- **Slow return to baseline** — once dysregulated, the individual remains emotionally elevated for hours rather than minutes

**The invalidating environment** is one that consistently communicates that a person's internal experiences (feelings, thoughts, urges) are wrong, inaccurate, inappropriate, or an overreaction. Three types of invalidation are described:
1. Punishing or dismissing valid emotional expression ("Stop crying, there's nothing to cry about")
2. Intermittently reinforcing extreme emotional displays only (which shapes escalation)
3. Oversimplifying solutions ("Just say no," "Just study harder")

Over time, the transaction between these two factors produces chronic emotional dysregulation, self-invalidation (the person begins to distrust their own inner states), behavioral dysregulation (impulsive or avoidant coping), interpersonal dysregulation, and identity confusion. Importantly, the model is **transactional**: adolescents can also invalidate their environments, and parents can have their own biological vulnerabilities that feed the cycle.

### How Adolescent DBT Differs from Standard Adult DBT

Rathus and Miller adapted Linehan's original manual beginning in the 1990s for urban, multiproblem, suicidal adolescents at Montefiore Medical Center. The key differences are:

1. **Five modules instead of four**: A fifth module — **Walking the Middle Path** — was developed specifically for adolescents and families. It addresses dialectics, validation, and behavior change principles relevant to parent–teen conflict.
2. **Multifamily skills group**: Parents/caregivers attend skills group alongside their adolescent. This reduces the invalidating environment, models skills in vivo, and provides parental coaching.
3. **Three additional dialectical dilemmas** specific to adolescent–family dynamics (excessive leniency vs. authoritarian control; normalizing pathological behaviors vs. pathologizing normative behaviors; forcing autonomy vs. fostering dependence).
4. **Shorter duration per session**: 2 hours (vs. 2.5 hours for adults), given adolescents' attention spans.
5. **Simplified language and visuals** on handouts, adjusted for reading levels, learning disabilities, and ESL backgrounds.
6. **Developmentally appropriate content**: Teaching examples, role plays, and exercises are calibrated to adolescent experience (school, social media, peer relationships, family curfews).
7. **Phone coaching extended to parents**, not just adolescents.
8. **Family sessions and parenting sessions** available on an as-needed basis.
9. **Additional skills** unique to this manual: THINK skills, Parent–Teen Shared Pleasant Activities List, self-validation handout, FOOD and MOOD / sleep hygiene handouts.
10. **Treatment duration**: One full 24-week cycle through all five modules (vs. one year in standard DBT). Many programs repeat for a second cycle with more severely affected adolescents.

### Ch 1: An Introduction to DBT and Skills Training

**Core concepts covered:**

**Five problem areas and corresponding skills modules:**
| Problem Area | Skills Module |
|---|---|
| Reduced awareness / confusion about self | Core Mindfulness |
| Emotional dysregulation | Emotion Regulation |
| Impulsivity | Distress Tolerance |
| Interpersonal problems | Interpersonal Effectiveness |
| Adolescent–family challenges | Walking the Middle Path |

**DBT Treatment Stages:**

| Stage | Focus |
|---|---|
| Pretreatment | Orientation, assessment, commitment to treatment |
| Stage 1 | Attaining basic capacities — safety, behavioral control |
| Stage 2 | Reducing Post-Traumatic Stress; exposure work for trauma |
| Stage 3 | Increasing self-respect and individual goals |
| Stage 4 | Achieving capacity for sustained joy |

**Stage 1 Target Hierarchy (individual therapy):**
1. Decrease life-threatening behaviors (suicidality, self-harm)
2. Decrease therapy-interfering behaviors
3. Decrease quality-of-life-interfering behaviors
4. Increase behavioral skills

**Stage 1 Target Hierarchy (skills training):**
1. Decrease behaviors likely to destroy therapy
2. Increase skills acquisition, strengthening, and generalization

**Dialectical Dilemmas — Standard DBT (Linehan):**
- Emotional vulnerability vs. self-invalidation
- Active passivity vs. apparent competence
- Unrelenting crisis vs. inhibited experiencing

**Dialectical Dilemmas — Adolescent-Specific (Rathus & Miller):**
- Excessive leniency vs. authoritarian control
- Normalizing pathological behaviors vs. pathologizing normative behaviors
- Forcing autonomy vs. fostering dependence

Each dilemma has secondary treatment targets: one aimed at decreasing the maladaptive pole, one at increasing the adaptive response.

**Research base:** Multiple quasi-experimental studies and open trials support DBT with adolescents across a range of presentations (multiproblem suicidal youth, BPD, bipolar disorder, eating disorders, externalizing disorders). Three RCTs are cited: Cooney et al. (New Zealand), Mehlum et al. (Norway — 16-week DBT vs. enhanced usual care; significant reduction in self-harm and suicidality), and an ongoing large RCT. The Mehlum study used multifamily format with Walking the Middle Path content.

### Ch 2: Structure of DBT Skills Training

**Module cycle (24 weeks total):**
- 2 weeks: Orientation + Mindfulness (repeated before each module)
- 4 weeks each: Distress Tolerance, Walking the Middle Path, Emotion Regulation, Interpersonal Effectiveness
- Alternative: 1 week Orientation/Mindfulness + 5 weeks each for other modules

**Rolling admissions vs. closed groups:** Open groups recommended — new families enter at the start of each Orientation/Mindfulness block.

**Session format (2 hours):**
- First half (50–55 min): Mindfulness practice + homework review
- Break (10 min): Snack, informal connection
- Second half (50–60 min): New skills teaching + homework assignment + wind-down (3–5 min nonjudgmental observation)

**Group size:** 3–5 families optimal (9–15 members), using the rule of 3–5 minutes per person for homework review.

**Graduation ceremony:** Teens receive a diploma; senior members give farewell words; gifts linked to skillful interests.

**Graduate group:** Optional continuation phase focused on skill maintenance and peer-led problem solving.

**Setting variations:**
- **Inpatient**: Shorter sessions, focus on orientation, validation, and family skills; suicidal risk management central
- **Residential/forensic**: Longer-term, invite family when possible; caution against contagion of NSSI discussions
- **School**: Sessions halved in length, yearlong format; avoid skipping homework review
- **Medical settings**: DBT adapted for chronic illness treatment non-compliance (Hashim et al.)

### Ch 3: Managing Skills Training Group Sessions

**Commitment strategies (Linehan):**
- Pros and cons of committing to treatment
- Devil's advocate (argue against commitment to elicit the client's own counterarguments)
- Foot-in-the-door / door-in-the-face techniques
- Connect present commitment to prior commitments
- Highlight freedom to choose and absence of alternatives
- Cheerleading
- Shaping (reinforce successive approximations toward commitment)

**Skills training subtargets:**
1. **Skills acquisition** — didactics, role plays, experiential exercises
2. **Skills strengthening** — behavioral rehearsal with corrective feedback, homework review
3. **Skills generalization and maintenance** — phone coaching, integration into individual therapy

**Managing therapy-interfering behaviors:**
- Ignore minor behaviors (extinction)
- Use mild aversives (looking dismayed, brief behavioral chain analysis)
- Positive reinforcement of prosocial group behaviors
- Environmental interventions

**Handling dialectical dilemmas in group:**
- Active passivity: Shape independent problem solving; block others from rescuing
- Apparent competence: Use mindfulness and emotion regulation to increase awareness
- Inhibited experiencing: Use exposure-based strategies; increase emotion awareness
- Excessive leniency/strictness: Address in group and via primary therapist
- Fostering dependence/forcing autonomy: Coach gradual withdrawal of support; model "holding on while letting go"

**Behavioral chain analysis:** Used for therapy-interfering behaviors (e.g., missed homework, absences). Briefly assess what got in the way and problem-solve for the future.

### Ch 4: The Art of Conducting DBT Skills Training — Balancing Strategies

**Validation levels (Linehan):**

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Unbiased listening and observing — full attentive presence |
| 2 | Reflection — paraphrasing to show understanding |
| 3 | Mind-reading — articulating unspoken thoughts or feelings from nonverbal cues |
| 4 | Validate in terms of past history — behavior makes sense given learning history |
| 5 | Validate in terms of current context — behavior makes sense given current circumstances |
| 6 | Radical genuineness — treat the person as an equal capable of handling direct honest feedback |

**Core DBT treatment strategies (balanced):**
- **Acceptance strategies**: Validation, environmental intervention, reciprocal communication
- **Change strategies**: Problem solving, skills training, exposure, contingency management, cognitive modification
- **Dialectical strategies**: Devil's advocate, entering the paradox, extending, making lemonade, allowing natural change, wise mind (therapist), dialectical assessment
- **Stylistic strategies**: Reciprocal communication (warm, genuine) vs. irreverent communication (off-balance, humorous, confrontational)

**Balancing validation with problem solving:** Therapists combine validation with pushes toward change. Pure validation without change can reinforce passivity; pure change without validation triggers dropout.

**Irreverent communication examples:**
- Responding to "I can't be assertive" with: "You could have fooled me — you're showing me your assertiveness right now by telling me you can't do this!"
- Becoming suddenly quiet to redirect disruptive group behavior
- Disclosing a coping story with humor and resolution

**Managing dialectical tensions in skills training:**
- Overly energized group → mindfulness, paced breathing, muscle relaxation, quiet writing exercises
- Withdrawn/internalizing group → stories, dyadic exercises, co-leader role plays, games (Jeopardy)

### Ch 5: Orientation to Multifamily Skills Training Group

**Session structure:**
- Introduce group members and skills trainers
- Explain DBT, the five problem areas, the five skill sets
- Biosocial theory (using the bio/social framework and the emotion intensity graph)
- DBT treatment assumptions (8 core assumptions)
- Group guidelines and DBT contract

**DBT Assumptions (8):**
1. People are doing the best they can
2. Everyone wants to improve
3. Everyone needs to do better, try harder, and be more motivated
4. People may not have caused all their problems, yet must solve them anyway
5. The lives of emotionally distressed teens and families are painful as currently lived
6. Teens and families must practice new behaviors across all life situations
7. There is no absolute truth
8. Teens and families cannot fail in DBT

**Three types of invalidation taught to families:**
1. Punishing/trivializing valid emotion (Type 1)
2. Intermittently reinforcing extreme emotion only (Type 2)
3. Overstating the ease of problem solving (Type 3 — "Just say no")

**Biosocial theory teaching metaphor:** The "fire in a fireplace" — once a fire is burning, adding small logs (daily stressors) reignites it quickly for emotionally sensitive individuals.

### Ch 6: Mindfulness Skills

**Module goal:** Increase nonjudgmental awareness of present-moment experience; improve attentional control. Foundational to all other DBT skills — one cannot use any other skill without first noticing one needs it.

**Three States of Mind:**

| State | Description |
|---|---|
| Emotional Mind | Emotion consumes reasoning; action urges dominate; impulsive |
| Reasonable Mind | Logic and planning without emotion; facts-only |
| Wise Mind | Synthesis of emotion and reason; "gut knowing" that integrates both; elevated, intuitive wisdom |

**Accessing Wise Mind:** Observe emotional state + check facts + identify long-term goals + listen to intuition.

**Mindfulness "What" Skills (three):**

| Skill | Description |
|---|---|
| **Observe** | Notice experience without words — external (five senses) and internal (thoughts, feelings, urges). "Teflon mind" — let experiences come and go without holding on or pushing away |
| **Describe** | Put words on what you observe — stick to facts, no interpretations. Replace "she has an attitude" with "she rolled her eyes and raised her voice" |
| **Participate** | Throw yourself fully into the current activity without self-consciousness; "in the zone"; one thing at a time |

**Mindfulness "How" Skills (three):**

| Skill | Description |
|---|---|
| **Non-judgmentally** | Notice but don't evaluate as good/bad; acknowledge harmful/helpful without judging; replace "I'm a stupid idiot" with factual description |
| **One-mindfully (Stay focused)** | Do one thing at a time; when mind wanders, gently return; resist multitasking |
| **Effectively (Do what works)** | Focus on what works; cut the cord between feeling and doing; let go of "shoulds" and vengeance |

**Key teaching points:**
- Mindfulness gives you choices before acting on urges (catching emotion at 10 rather than 100)
- Mindfulness reduces suffering and increases capacity for pleasure
- Mindfulness improves focus, academic performance, and compassion
- Mindfulness reduces physical stress and pain (Kabat-Zinn 1990)

**Mindfulness exercises for adolescents (sample):**
- What's Different About Me (observational pairs)
- Snap-Crackle-Pop (one-mindful attention game)
- Mindfully eating a raisin/candy
- Egg balancing
- Wise Mind Charades
- Walking meditation
- Focusing on the breath
- Observing emotions
- Blowing bubbles (one-mindfully)

### Ch 7: Distress Tolerance Skills

**Module purpose:** Tolerate painful situations and emotional pain when problems cannot be solved immediately. Two types: (1) Crisis Survival Skills and (2) Reality Acceptance Skills.

**Why tolerate distress:**
- Life involves unavoidable pain
- Avoiding pain often creates worse problems
- Short-term coping strategies (drugs, self-harm, avoidance) work briefly but make things worse long-term
- TIPP skills work as fast as dysfunctional behaviors, without the negative consequences

---

#### Crisis Survival Skills

**1. Distract with Wise Mind ACCEPTS**

| Letter | Skill | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Activities | Do something absorbing — games, exercise, hobbies |
| **C** | Contributing | Do something for someone else — volunteering, helping |
| **C** | Comparisons | Compare yourself to others who are coping less well; compare to your own past hardships |
| **E** | Emotions | Create different emotions — watch comedy, listen to upbeat music |
| **P** | Pushing away | Temporarily push the problem away ("put it in a box"); use mental imagery to set it aside |
| **T** | Thoughts | Replace distressing thoughts — count, describe objects, recite lyrics |
| **S** | Sensations | Intense sensory input — snap a rubber band, hold ice, listen to loud music, spicy food |

Key teaching point: Skills must be done **mindfully** to work — half-hearted distracting does not reduce distress. Use multiple skills in sequence if needed.

**2. Self-Soothe with Six Senses**

Soothe each of the senses (plus movement):
- **Vision**: Look at beautiful or calming images, nature, art
- **Hearing**: Soothing music, nature sounds
- **Touch**: Warm bath, soft fabrics, massage
- **Smell**: Scented candles, essential oils, baking
- **Taste**: Herbal tea, chocolate, a favorite food (mindfully)
- **Movement**: Stretching, yoga, dance, walking

**3. IMPROVE the Moment**

| Letter | Skill | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | Imagery | Visualize a safe, calming, or confidence-boosting scene |
| **M** | Meaning | Find purpose or a silver lining in the pain (not mandatory; not trivializing) |
| **P** | Prayer | Meditation, prayer, connection to something larger |
| **R** | Relaxation | Progressive muscle relaxation, paced breathing, hot bath |
| **O** | One thing in the moment | Focus full attention on only this moment; reduce past/future rumination |
| **V** | Vacation | Brief escape from the stressor — Starbucks, a walk, unplug for an hour |
| **E** | Encouragement | Positive self-talk — "I can get through this," "This feeling will pass" |

**4. Pros and Cons**

Weigh the advantages and disadvantages of:
- Acting impulsively (on the urge) — short-term relief, long-term damage
- Not acting impulsively (using skills) — short-term effort, long-term gain

Key technique: Label each pro/con as **short-term (st)** or **long-term (lt)**. The pattern typically shows: pros of impulsive action are mostly short-term; pros of skillful action are mostly long-term. Build this list in advance as part of treatment planning and review it in moments of crisis.

**5. TIPP Skills (Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation)**

Used when in "red zone" — so distressed that other skills are inaccessible. TIPP activates the **parasympathetic nervous system (PNS)** to rapidly reduce extreme arousal.

| Letter | Skill | Mechanism | Cautions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **T** | Temperature — cold water on face | Activates dive reflex → reduces heart rate rapidly; effect lasts 5–20 minutes | Not for cardiac conditions, anorexia/bulimia, cold allergy; requires medical clearance |
| **I** | Intense aerobic exercise | Activates SNS; after stopping, PNS rebounds with 20-minute calming effect | — |
| **P** | Paced breathing | Slow breathing (5–6 cycles/min); exhale longer than inhale; activates PNS | — |
| **P** | Progressive muscle relaxation | Systematic tension-and-release of muscle groups; requires daily practice for 1 month for full benefit; can be abbreviated after overlearning | Watch for relaxation-induced panic |

**SNS/PNS physiology teaching point:** When in crisis, the SNS activates (fight-or-flight: increased heart rate, blood pressure, dilated pupils, slowed digestion). TIPP skills activate PNS (rest-and-digest: decreased heart rate and blood pressure, constricted pupils, improved digestion), buying 5–20 minutes of calm in which other skills can be accessed.

#### Reality Acceptance Skills

**Radical Acceptance**
- Accepting reality as it is, not as it should be
- Acceptance ≠ approval
- Pain vs. suffering: Pain is unavoidable; suffering = pain + non-acceptance
- Radical acceptance converts unbearable suffering into bearable pain

**Turning the Mind**
- Acceptance is a choice — repeated, often moment by moment
- When you catch yourself fighting reality, notice it and turn your mind back toward acceptance
- The turning is itself a skill — not a single decision but an ongoing practice

**Willingness vs. Willfulness**
- **Willingness**: Doing what the situation calls for; being effective; open to what is
- **Willfulness**: Refusing to engage with reality; not doing what is needed; clinging to what "should" be

**Half-Smile**
- Relax facial muscles (forehead to jaw); gently upturn corners of mouth
- Acts as a behavioral cue for acceptance — changes emotional experience through body posture
- Distinct from a fake, forced smile — subtle, barely visible
- Used as mindfulness exercise at start of Session 4

**Ways to Practice Accepting Reality:**
- Observe that you are questioning reality
- Remind yourself that reality is what it is
- Consider causes of the present event (everything has a cause)
- Practice opposite action to acceptance-fighting behaviors
- Cope ahead for future rejections of reality
- Practice mindfully with the half-smile and willing hands posture

### Ch 8: Walking the Middle Path Skills

**Module purpose:** Reduce family conflict and polarized thinking through dialectics, validation, and behavior change principles. Developed specifically by Rathus and Miller for adolescents and families. Not included in standard adult DBT.

**Three components:**
1. **Dialectics** — reducing black-and-white thinking; finding middle path
2. **Validation** — acceptance of self and others
3. **Behavior change** — reinforcement, shaping, extinction, punishment

---

#### Session 1: Dialectics

**Core dialectical principles:**
1. There is always more than one way to see a situation
2. Each person has unique qualities; people influence each other
3. Everything and every person has strengths and limitations
4. Change is constant

**How-to guide for dialectical thinking and acting:**
1. Move from "either/or" to "both–and" thinking
2. Look at all sides; find the kernel of truth in each
3. No one has the absolute truth
4. Use "I feel…" statements instead of "You are…"
5. Accept that different opinions can be valid without agreeing
6. Check your assumptions
7. Don't expect others to know what you're thinking

**Thinking mistakes (cognitive distortions) taught:**
- All-or-none thinking
- Jumping to conclusions
- Labeling
- "Shoulds"

**Adolescent–family dialectical dilemmas:**

| Dilemma | Too loose pole | Too strict pole | Middle path |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Excessive leniency vs. authoritarian control** | No rules, no monitoring, no consequences | Rigid rules, excessive monitoring, extreme consequences | Authoritative parenting — firm limits with warmth and flexibility; earn back privacy through improved behavior |
| **Normalizing pathological behaviors vs. pathologizing normative behaviors** | "Everyone their age does this" — minimizing self-harm, substance use | Treating normal teen behavior (wanting privacy, phone use) as dangerous | Recognizing when behavior "crosses the line"; use developmental norms as guide; consider culture |
| **Forcing autonomy vs. fostering dependence** | Cutting support prematurely; expecting self-sufficiency before skills are developed | Helicopter parenting; solving all problems before teen can try | "Holding on while letting go" — guide, coach, support while gradually expanding freedom |

---

#### Session 2: Validation

**Why validate:** Lowers emotional intensity in the person being validated; increases likelihood they stay in the conversation; strengthens the relationship; creates positive cycles.

**What to validate:** Feelings, thoughts, and behaviors (though not always the behavior — can validate the emotion without validating the harmful action).

**Validate the valid, not the invalid:** "I understand you're furious" (validate emotion) ≠ "It was okay to punch the wall" (don't validate harmful behavior).

**How to validate others (Handout 9):**
1. Actively listen — eye contact, stay focused
2. Reflect — paraphrase what was said
3. Mind-read — articulate unspoken feelings/thoughts ("I can see you're in pain right now")
4. Validate based on past history — "Given what you've been through, of course you feel this way"
5. Validate based on current context — "Of course you're nervous — anyone in your situation would be" (Level 5 — this is the heart of DBT validation)
6. Radical genuineness — treat the person as an equal; honest, direct, warm

**How to validate oneself (Handout 10):**
- Notice self-invalidation ("I shouldn't feel this way")
- Replace with nonjudgmental description of experience
- Acknowledge what makes sense about your response given context
- Practice self-compassion

**Common validation errors:**
- Complimenting instead of validating ("I'm sure you'll do fine!" — dismissive)
- Focusing on the problem instead of the emotion
- Validating a perceived overreaction by minimizing ("You're getting this angry over a hairbrush?")
- Redirecting to own experience ("I've had days like that too! Last month my boss…")
- Teaching logical thinking instead of validating ("Well, you should have left earlier")

#### Sessions 3–4: Behavior Change

**Positive reinforcement:** Add a rewarding consequence to increase a desired behavior.
- Must be immediate, motivating, and not given for the wrong behavior
- Examples: praise, privileges, time together, money, affection
- Satiation: Too much of a reinforcer makes it lose effect

**Negative reinforcement:** Remove something unpleasant to increase a behavior (relief). Not the same as punishment. Example: Teen cleaning room → parent stops nagging → teen more likely to clean room again.

**Shaping:** Reinforce successive approximations toward a goal. Used when the target behavior is complex, takes time, or involves multiple steps. Key principle: Don't expect full behavior immediately — reinforce each small step.

**Extinction:** Withhold the reinforcer that has been maintaining an unwanted behavior. Key rules:
1. Identify a replacement behavior and reinforce it
2. Expect and ride out the **behavioral burst** — the initial escalation of the unwanted behavior before it fades

**Punishment:** A consequence that decreases a behavior by adding something negative or removing something positive.
- **Overuse risks**: Demoralizes, teaches hiding rather than stopping, doesn't motivate, doesn't teach new behavior, may lead to self-punishment
- **Effective use**: Pair with reinforcement of desired behavior; communicate clear rules; have a punishment menu ready; apply immediately; use measured consequences; allow natural consequences

**Self-reinforcement:** Reward yourself for steps toward goals; essential for motivation and mastery.

### Ch 9: Emotion Regulation Skills

**Module purpose:** Address the biological vulnerability to emotion dysregulation. Skills reduce vulnerability, create positive emotions, prevent negative emotions, and lower intensity of emotions that have already fired.

**Why bother:** Intense emotions → impulsive/problem behaviors; learning to regulate reduces the problematic behavior chain.

**Goals of emotion regulation training:**
1. Understand emotions experienced
2. Reduce emotional vulnerability to Emotion Mind
3. Decrease frequency of unwanted emotions
4. Decrease emotional suffering

---

#### Model of Emotions (A→B→C→D chain)

Components of an emotion:
- **Vulnerability factors** (tiredness, hunger, illness) → increase sensitivity to prompting events
- **Prompting event** (triggering stimulus) →
- **Interpretation/Thoughts** (how we make sense of the event) →
- **Internal responses** (brain changes, body sensations, urges — internal) + **External responses** (facial expression, actions — observable) →
- **Emotion name** (the whole cluster)
- **Aftereffects** (secondary emotions, sensitivity to further triggers)

**Clinical implications:** The circuit can be interrupted at any point. Skills map onto each link in the chain (vulnerability → ABC PLEASE; thoughts → Check the Facts; urges → Opposite Action; sensations → distress tolerance; etc.).

**Key point:** "Emotions are not facts." Fear does not prove threat. Sadness does not prove loss is permanent.

---

#### ABC PLEASE Overview

**A — Accumulating Positive Experiences**

*Short-term:*
- Schedule pleasant activities daily (treat them like doctor's appointments)
- Be mindful of positive experiences — participate fully, resist rumination
- Unmindful of worries during positive activities
- Handout 10 (Pleasant Activities List) + Handout 11 (Parent–Teen Shared Pleasant Activities List — original to this manual)

*Long-term:*
- Identify Wise Mind values and priorities (Handout 13)
- Set long-term goals consistent with values
- Take small, graduated steps toward goals
- Pay attention to relationships as sources of positive emotion
- Avoid avoiding; avoid giving up

**B — Building Mastery**
- Do at least one thing daily that builds competence and sense of control
- Plan for success, not failure — match challenge to current capability
- Increase difficulty gradually (ladder metaphor)
- Mastery ≠ fun, but it builds positive mood and reduces emptiness

**C — Cope Ahead**
Steps:
1. Describe the upcoming situation specifically; name the emotions likely to interfere
2. Decide which skills to use; write the plan
3. Imagine the situation vividly (first-person, not observer)
4. Rehearse coping effectively in your mind — actions, words, tone

**PLEASE Skills (Reduce Emotional Vulnerability)**

| Letter | Skill | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **PL** | Treat PhysicaL illness | Untreated illness increases irritability, reduces coping capacity |
| **E** | balance Eating | Balanced meals, no skipping breakfast; avoid excessive caffeine, processed food |
| **A** | avoid mood-Altering drugs and alcohol | Alcohol is a depressant; marijuana worsens anxiety, motivation, and memory; associated with NSSI in adolescents (Velting & Miller, 1999) |
| **S** | balance Sleep | Teenagers need 8+ hours; sleep deprivation is a major vulnerability factor; sleep hygiene handout available (Handout 16b) |
| **E** | get Exercise | Best natural antidepressant; mood enhancement within 5 minutes of moderate activity; reduces anxiety and stress |

---

#### Changing Emotions

**The Wave Skill — Mindfulness of Current Emotion**
- Emotions come in waves — they do not last forever if you let them
- Don't try to get rid of the emotion; don't hold on to it
- Experience the sensation fully; observe how long it takes to pass
- You are not your emotion; you don't have to act on it
- "Invite it home for dinner" rather than fighting it
- Practice loving/accepting the emotion
- Radically accept the emotion

**Check the Facts and Problem Solving**
- When an emotion is fuel for distorted thoughts, check the facts first
- Is the problem as bad as it seems? Is the interpretation accurate?
- If the problem remains, problem-solve systematically: brainstorm solutions, attempt one, evaluate, try another

**Opposite Action to Change Emotions**

When emotion does not fit the facts, or when acting on the urge would not be effective, act opposite to the emotional action urge — **all the way** (posture, voice, thoughts, actions).

| Emotion | Action Urge | Opposite Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Fear** | Avoid, escape | Approach, confront — repeatedly; do what feared (exposure) |
| **Anger** | Attack, confront | Avoid temporarily; imagine understanding/empathy for the other person; approach with kindness |
| **Sadness/depression** | Withdraw, stay in bed, be passive | Become active; engage with others; resume pleasurable activities |
| **Shame (unjustified)** | Hide, avoid, lower head | Go public; stand tall; maintain eye contact; act innocent and proud |
| **Shame (justified)** | Hide, beg, punish self | Face the music; accept responsibility; apologize; repair; prevent recurrence |
| **Guilt (justified)** | Beg forgiveness, punish self | Apologize once (don't beg); make amends; commit to change |
| **Guilt (unjustified)** | Over-apologize | Don't apologize; stand tall; let go of guilt |
| **Jealousy** | Demand attention, check, control | Don't act on jealousy; engage in other activities; strengthen other relationships |

**How to do opposite action (8 steps):**
1. Identify the emotion (observe and describe)
2. Identify the action urge
3. Ask: Does the emotion fit the facts?
4. Ask: Would acting on the urge be effective?
5. Ask: Do I want to change the emotion?
6. Figure out the opposite action
7. Do the opposite action — all the way
8. Continue until the emotion comes down

### Ch 10: Interpersonal Effectiveness Skills

**Module purpose:** Build and maintain positive relationships; get needs met effectively; preserve self-respect. Directly complements other modules (emotion regulation → fewer outbursts; distress tolerance → weather conflict; mindfulness → attend to relationships).

**Three goals of interpersonal effectiveness:**
1. **Maintain relationships** → GIVE skills
2. **Get what you want / say no effectively** → DEAR MAN skills
3. **Keep self-respect** → FAST skills

The goal is to balance all three goals simultaneously — like juggling three balls.

**Factors that interfere with interpersonal effectiveness:**
1. Lack of skill
2. Worry thoughts
3. Emotions
4. Can't decide
5. Environment (other person is unskillful)

---

#### GIVE Skills — Maintaining Relationships

| Letter | Skill | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **G** | (be) Gentle | No attacks, no threats, no sarcasm or put-downs; kind tone of voice; manage anger before speaking |
| **I** | (act) Interested | Listen — put phone down; make eye contact; don't multitask; show you care |
| **V** | Validate | Use validation levels 1–6; find the kernel of truth; communicate understanding |
| **E** | (use an) Easy manner | Lighten up; smile; use humor; be politically savvy; pick the right moment |

---

#### DEAR MAN Skills — Getting What You Want / Saying No

| Letter | Skill | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **D** | Describe | State the current facts of the situation objectively; no interpretations, no judgments |
| **E** | Express | Express feelings and opinions about the situation using "I feel…" statements |
| **A** | Assert | Clearly ask for what you want or say no — don't hint, don't assume they know |
| **R** | Reinforce | Tell the person what they will get if they give you what you want; explain the benefit to them |
| **M** | (be) Mindful | Stay focused on your goal; broken-record technique — return to request repeatedly without being derailed |
| **A** | Appear confident | Voice tone, eye contact, posture, physical appearance — project confidence even if you don't feel it |
| **N** | Negotiate | Offer to give to get; ask what would help the other person grant your request; joint problem solving |

**Broken-record technique:** When distracted, invalidated, or stonewalled — calmly and repeatedly return to the request or "no" without escalating. Don't be pulled into debates or side arguments.

---

#### FAST Skills — Maintaining Self-Respect

| Letter | Skill | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **F** | (be) Fair | Fair to yourself and to the other person; honor your needs and theirs |
| **A** | (no) Apologies | Don't over-apologize; no apologizing for existing, for having needs, for your values |
| **S** | Stick to values | Do what is right for you according to your Wise Mind values; don't sell out under pressure |
| **T** | (be) Truthful | Don't lie, exaggerate, or act helpless ("learned helplessness") |

---

#### Factors to Consider (Deciding Intensity of Request or "No")

- **Priorities**: What matters most — relationship, getting what you want, or self-respect?
- **Capability**: Can they actually give you what you're asking for?
- **Timeliness**: Is this the right moment to ask?
- **Relationship**: Has the relationship "earned" this level of request? Is there give and take?
- **Long-term goals**: Will asking/refusing help or hurt your long-term goals?
- **Self-respect**: Will you feel good about how you handled this?

---

#### THINK Skills (Optional — not in standard DBT)

Developed by Rathus and Miller based on Crick and Dodge's (1994) social information-processing model. Used when someone assumes the worst about another person's intentions (common in emotionally dysregulated adolescents).

| Letter | Skill |
|---|---|
| **T** | (Think) — Consider other possible explanations for the person's behavior |
| **H** | Have empathy — imagine the person's perspective, feelings, pressures |
| **I** | Interpretations — check whether your negative interpretation is the only possibility |
| **N** | Notice — notice how this person has shown caring or tried to help at other times |
| **K** | Kindness — approach with gentleness given the more charitable understanding |

**Note:** THINK skills have not been used in clinical trials.

## DBT Skills by Module

### Core Mindfulness

**Three States of Mind:**
- Emotional Mind — emotion-dominated, impulsive
- Reasonable Mind — logic-only, no emotion
- Wise Mind — synthesized, intuitive knowing

**What Skills:**
- **Observe**: Notice without words; both external (five senses) and internal (thoughts, feelings, urges); Teflon mind; popcorn mind; don't push away or hold on
- **Describe**: Put words on observed facts; no interpretations or judgments; "just the facts"
- **Participate**: Throw yourself fully into the present activity; "in the zone"; no self-consciousness; one thing at a time

**How Skills:**
- **Non-judgmentally**: No evaluating as good/bad; stick to observable facts; replace judgmental language with description
- **One-mindfully (Stay Focused)**: One thing at a time; when mind wanders, gently redirect
- **Effectively (Do What Works)**: Focus on what's effective, not on being right; let go of "shoulds," vengeance, and point-proving

**How to practice mindfulness:** Choose a focus; set intention; when mind wanders, nonjudgmentally notice and return attention; 2–5 minutes; do daily in many contexts.

---

### Distress Tolerance

**Crisis Survival Skills:**
- **Distract: Wise Mind ACCEPTS** — Activities, Contributing, Comparisons, Emotions, Pushing away, Thoughts, Sensations
- **Self-Soothe with Six Senses** — Vision, Hearing, Touch, Smell, Taste, Movement
- **IMPROVE the Moment** — Imagery, Meaning, Prayer, Relaxation, One thing, Vacation, Encouragement
- **Pros and Cons** — Compare acting skillfully vs. impulsively; short-term vs. long-term
- **TIPP Skills** — Temperature (cold water), Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation

**Reality Acceptance Skills:**
- **Radical Acceptance** — Accept reality as it is; acceptance ≠ approval; pain × non-acceptance = suffering
- **Turning the Mind** — Choose acceptance repeatedly, moment by moment
- **Willingness** — Do what the situation requires; be effective
- **Half-Smile** — Relax face; gently upturn mouth corners; facial feedback cues acceptance

---

### Emotion Regulation

**Understanding Emotions:**
- Observe and describe emotions (using Handout 3 — Short List of Emotions)
- Model of emotions: vulnerability factors → prompting event → thoughts → internal/external responses → emotion name → aftereffects
- Functions of emotions: information, communication, motivation, action-readiness

**ABC PLEASE — Reducing Vulnerability:**
- **A — Accumulate Positives** (short-term: pleasant activities; long-term: values-based goals)
- **B — Build Mastery** (daily competence-building; graduated difficulty)
- **C — Cope Ahead** (imaginal rehearsal of future stressors; four steps)
- **PL — Treat PhysicaL illness**
- **E — balance Eating**
- **A — Avoid mood-Altering drugs**
- **S — balance Sleep**
- **E — Exercise**

**Changing Emotions:**
- **The Wave Skill** — Mindfulness of current emotion; ride the wave; don't fight or hold on
- **Check the Facts** — Verify whether emotion fits the facts; problem-solve if it does
- **Opposite Action** — Act opposite to urge, all the way, until emotion decreases

### Interpersonal Effectiveness

**Goals:** Maintain relationships (GIVE) + Get what you want / say no (DEAR MAN) + Maintain self-respect (FAST)

**GIVE:** Gentle, Interested, Validate, Easy manner
**DEAR MAN:** Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce, Mindful, Appear confident, Negotiate
**FAST:** Fair, no Apologies, Stick to values, Truthful
**THINK (optional):** Think / Have empathy / Interpretations / Notice / Kindness

**Supporting skills:**
- Clarify priorities before any interaction (Handout 1)
- Identify factors that interfere (lack of skill, worry thoughts, emotions, indecision, environment)
- Challenge worry thoughts with Wise Mind self-statements
- STOP skill (informal): Stop, Take a step back, Observe, Proceed mindfully

---

### Walking the Middle Path (Adolescent-Specific)

**Dialectics:**
- Both–and thinking; no absolute truth; find kernels of truth in each position
- Recognize and address thinking mistakes (all-or-none, jumping to conclusions, labeling, shoulds)

**Three dialectical dilemmas:**
1. Excessive leniency vs. authoritarian control → middle path: authoritative parenting with earned privileges
2. Normalizing pathological behaviors vs. pathologizing normative behaviors → middle path: developmental norms with cultural awareness
3. Fostering dependence vs. forcing autonomy → middle path: "holding on while letting go"

**Validation (6 levels):** Listening → Reflecting → Mind-reading → History-based → Context-based → Radical genuineness

**Behavior change:**
- Positive reinforcement (add reward to increase behavior)
- Negative reinforcement (remove aversive to increase behavior)
- Shaping (reinforce successive approximations)
- Extinction (withhold reinforcer; ride out behavioral burst; reinforce replacement behavior)
- Punishment (use sparingly; pair with reinforcement; natural consequences preferred)

## Clinical Techniques Catalogue

### Chain Analysis (Behavioral Analysis)
A detailed examination of the sequence of events, thoughts, feelings, bodily sensations, and behaviors that led to a problem behavior.
- **When used:** After every life-threatening behavior, therapy-interfering behavior, or target behavior
- **Steps:** (1) Identify the problem behavior precisely; (2) Identify the vulnerability factors operating beforehand; (3) Identify the prompting event; (4) Map each link in the chain (thoughts, feelings, body sensations, actions); (5) Identify consequences; (6) Generate solutions for each link
- **Purpose:** Increases awareness, reduces shame through nonjudgmental analysis, identifies points of intervention

### Solution Analysis
Follows chain analysis. Generates specific strategies to intervene at each link in the problem behavior chain. Solutions may include skills from any module, environmental changes, or individual therapy work.

### Diary Card
Weekly self-monitoring form tracking daily emotions (rated), urges to engage in target behaviors, skills use, and individualized target behaviors. Reviewed at the start of each individual therapy session. Drives the treatment hierarchy — highest-priority target behaviors on the card are addressed first.

### Phone Coaching
Brief (typically under 10 minutes) between-session contact with the therapist or skills trainer to provide real-time skills coaching. In adolescent DBT, available to both teens and parents. Purpose: Generalize skills from therapy room to daily life. Not for crisis management per se — if the client has already engaged in self-harm, the coaching call may be restricted per treatment agreements.

### Behavioral Rehearsal
In-session practice of skills (especially interpersonal skills — DEAR MAN, GIVE, FAST) with corrective feedback and opportunity to redo. Done in dyads, triads, or with co-leaders. Feedback is specific, behavioral, and surrounded by positive reinforcement.

### Dialectical Strategies
- **Entering the paradox**: Highlight contradictions to create movement
- **Devil's advocate**: Argue the other side to elicit the client's own counterarguments
- **Extending**: Take a client's position to an absurd extreme
- **Making lemonade**: Find the silver lining or opportunity in a painful situation
- **Allowing natural change**: Let the treatment evolve and change naturally over time
- **Wise mind (therapist)**: Ground interventions in wisdom rather than technique alone
- **Dialectical assessment**: Assess for what is valid and what needs to change simultaneously

### Validation Levels 1–6

| Level | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Unbiased listening, full attention |
| 2 | Accurate reflection / paraphrase |
| 3 | Mind-reading — articulating unspoken experience from nonverbal cues |
| 4 | Validate based on past learning history |
| 5 | Validate based on current context (this is the heart of DBT validation) |
| 6 | Radical genuineness — treat as capable equal; honest and direct |

### Commitment Strategies
(Used in pretreatment and throughout treatment to obtain and renew commitment)
1. Pros and cons of committing vs. not
2. Devil's advocate (argue against — elicits client's own reasons for committing)
3. Foot-in-the-door / door-in-the-face
4. Connect to prior commitments
5. Highlight freedom of choice and absence of alternatives
6. Cheerleading (generate hope)
7. Shaping (reinforce successive approximations toward commitment)

## Adolescent-Specific Adaptations

| Feature | Standard DBT | Adolescent DBT |
|---|---|---|
| Number of modules | 4 | 5 (adds Walking the Middle Path) |
| Skills group format | Adults only | Multifamily (teens + parents/caregivers together) |
| Session length | 2.5 hours | 2 hours |
| Phone coaching | Client only | Client AND parents |
| Family involvement | Minimal | Central — family sessions, parenting sessions, multifamily group |
| Treatment duration | 1 year | 6 months (24 weeks), often repeated |
| Dialectical dilemmas | 3 (Linehan) | 6 (3 Linehan + 3 adolescent-specific) |
| Handout language | Adult level | Simplified; graphics; varied fonts |
| Teaching examples | Adult contexts | School, social media, peers, curfews, family |
| Graduation ceremony | Varies | Diploma, farewell speeches, token gift |
| THINK skills | Not included | Optional addition (based on Crick & Dodge, 1994) |
| Parent–Teen shared activities | Not included | Emotion Regulation Handout 11 — original to this manual |
| Self-validation | Secondary target | Formally taught (Walking the Middle Path Handout 10) |
| Food/sleep handouts | Not included | Optional ER Handouts 16a and 16b |

**Walking the Middle Path rationale:** Parents and teens often polarize — one goes to extremes of leniency or strictness, pathologizing or minimizing, fostering dependence or forcing independence. The module teaches both parties to find the dialectical middle path, reducing the invalidating environment directly rather than only helping the teen cope with it.

**Confidentiality in multifamily format:** Particularly complex. Group leaders know more about clients than they have disclosed in group, and teens may be reluctant to speak openly in front of parents. Group rules require that no family names or information leave the group.

**Graduate group:** Optional continuation phase. Reduces leader role; increases peer-led problem solving and coaching. Goals: skill maintenance, generalization, peer support, and quality-of-life enhancement.

## Exam-Ready Extracts

> **EXAM PEARL:** The biosocial model holds that chronic emotional dysregulation results from the **transaction** of biological vulnerability (high sensitivity + high reactivity + slow return to baseline) with a **pervasively invalidating environment**. This transaction is bidirectional — the teen also invalidates the environment.

> **EXAM PEARL:** DBT has **five treatment modules** in the adolescent version (vs. four in adult): Core Mindfulness, Distress Tolerance, Emotion Regulation, Interpersonal Effectiveness, and **Walking the Middle Path** (adolescent-specific). The 24-week cycle is completed in the same time as standard DBT but devotes less time to each module.

> **EXAM PEARL:** The **Stage 1 target hierarchy** in individual DBT is: (1) life-threatening behaviors → (2) therapy-interfering behaviors → (3) quality-of-life-interfering behaviors → (4) increase behavioral skills. In skills training, the hierarchy is: (1) destroy-therapy behaviors → (2) skills acquisition.

> **EXAM PEARL:** **Validation Level 5** — validating in terms of the current context (behavior makes sense given the present situation) — is described as "the heart of DBT." Level 6 — radical genuineness — treats the client as a capable equal.

> **EXAM PEARL:** **TIPP** skills work by activating the **parasympathetic nervous system**. Cold water activates the dive reflex. Intense exercise is followed by a PNS rebound. Paced breathing (exhale longer than inhale, ~5–6 cycles/minute) directly stimulates the PNS. PMR creates a relaxation response. Effects last 5–20 minutes.

> **EXAM PEARL:** **Opposite action** must be done **all the way** — posture, voice, thoughts, and actions all oriented opposite to the emotion urge. Half-hearted opposite action (e.g., getting out of bed to lie on the couch) does not qualify.

> **EXAM PEARL:** **Extinction** requires two additional components to work: (1) identify and **reinforce a replacement behavior**, and (2) **ride out the behavioral burst** — the initial escalation of the unwanted behavior before it extinguishes.

> **EXAM PEARL:** The **three adolescent-specific dialectical dilemmas** are: excessive leniency vs. authoritarian control; normalizing pathological behaviors vs. pathologizing normative behaviors; fostering dependence vs. forcing autonomy. The corresponding middle paths are: authoritative parenting; developmental norm-awareness; "holding on while letting go."

> **EXAM PEARL:** **Phone coaching** in adolescent DBT extends to both teens and parents. It is for real-time skills application, not crisis management. If self-harm has already occurred, coaching calls may be restricted per treatment contract.

> **EXAM PEARL:** **DEAR MAN** = Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce, Mindful (broken record), Appear confident, Negotiate. **GIVE** = Gentle, Interested, Validate, Easy manner. **FAST** = Fair, no Apologies, Stick to values, Truthful. **ACCEPTS** = Activities, Contributing, Comparisons, Emotions, Pushing away, Thoughts, Sensations. **IMPROVE** = Imagery, Meaning, Prayer, Relaxation, One thing, Vacation, Encouragement. **TIPP** = Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation. **PLEASE** = treat PhysicaL illness, balance Eating, Avoid mood-altering drugs, balance Sleep, Exercise. **ABC PLEASE** = Accumulate positives + Build mastery + Cope ahead + PLEASE.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** A parent who gives in when a teenager escalates to extreme emotional displays is providing **intermittent reinforcement** for the extreme display (Type 2 invalidation). This shapes escalation — the teen learns that ordinary communication is ineffective but extremes are rewarded. DBT addresses this through extinction + reinforcement of calm expression, and through parent education on validation.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** When a client says "I was too upset to use my skills," the clinician normalizes: DBT was developed specifically for severe, chronic dysregulation — being very upset is *exactly* when to try skills. Multiple skills in sequence are often needed. No single skill works every time; building a toolbox matters.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** **Behavioral chain analysis** is the engine of individual DBT. Without understanding the specific chain of events leading to a problem behavior, generic skills practice is insufficient. Chain analysis identifies idiosyncratic vulnerability factors, triggers, and links that are specific to the individual — enabling targeted solution analysis.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** The **half-smile** is not about fake positivity — it is a specific behavioral cue for radical acceptance. The subtle upturning of the corners of the mouth, with fully relaxed facial muscles, activates facial feedback and shifts emotional tone. It is contraindicated when done as a full, forced smile (which increases distress).

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR:** In multifamily skills group, the **biosocial theory** is the therapeutic frame for both generations. It reduces parental blame and teen self-blame simultaneously. Parents hear "you didn't create this" while also hearing "the environment matters." Teens hear "you're wired differently" while also hearing "you can learn to manage it."
