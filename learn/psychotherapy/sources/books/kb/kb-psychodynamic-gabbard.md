## Core Framework

### What LTPP Is

Long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy is defined as "a therapy that involves careful attention to the therapist-patient interaction, with thoughtfully timed interpretation of transference and resistance embedded in a sophisticated appreciation of the therapist's contribution to the two-person field" (Gunderson and Gabbard 1999). For this text, **long-term = duration greater than 24 sessions or 6 months**. Two categories exist: (1) time-limited (predetermined session count, e.g. 40-52 sessions), and (2) open-ended (naturalistic termination).

### How It Differs From Other Therapies

- Focus on **unconscious processes** rather than conscious cognitions or behaviours
- The **therapeutic relationship itself** is a primary tool, not merely a vehicle for delivering techniques
- **Transference and countertransference** are central data sources, not nuisances
- Change occurs through both **insight** (interpretation) and **relationship** (new object experience) working synergistically
- Treatment is **non-directive** in principle (though directive interventions are used when needed)
- Goals include structural personality change, not only symptom reduction

### Theoretical Foundations (Four Models)

| Model | Key Theorists | Motivation | Basic Units | Psychopathology |
|-------|--------------|------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Ego Psychology | Freud, A. Freud, Hartmann | Drive gratification | Ego, Id, Superego | Conflict / compromise formation |
| Object Relations | Klein, Fairbairn, Winnicott, Kernberg | Object seeking | Self-other representations linked by affect | Maladaptive relationship patterns from externalised internal objects |
| Self Psychology | Kohut | Self-cohesion / self-esteem | Self-selfobject | Fragmentation of self / narcissistic vulnerability |
| Attachment Theory | Bowlby, Ainsworth, Fonagy | Physical sense of safety | Internal working models | Insecure attachment / failure of mentalization |

### Freudian Models of Mind

- **Topographic model:** Conscious / Preconscious / Unconscious (historical, partially superseded)
- **Structural model (1923):** Id (entirely unconscious drives), Ego (conscious executive + unconscious defences), Superego (predominantly unconscious moral internalisation, subdivided into ego ideal and moral conscience)
- **Signal anxiety:** Conflict between id/ego/superego produces signal anxiety, triggering defences, leading to compromise formations (symptoms)

### Memory Systems and the Unconscious

| Type of Knowledge | Mode of Expression | Example |
|-------------------|--------------------|---------|
| Declarative (facts) | Explicit (conscious) | Remembering teacher's name |
| Declarative (facts) | Implicit (unconscious) | Song triggering unknown tears |
| Procedural (skills) | Implicit (unconscious) | Automatic relational patterns, defence mechanisms |
| Procedural (skills) | Explicit (conscious) | Suppression (deliberate) |

The "how-to" of relatedness is encoded in **implicit procedural memory** from earliest attachment relationships. This is the primary target of therapeutic change.

### Seven Basic Principles (Table 1-1)

1. Much of mental life is unconscious
2. Childhood experiences in concert with genetic factors shape the adult
3. The patient's transference to the therapist is a primary source of understanding
4. The therapist's countertransference provides valuable understanding about what the patient induces in others
5. The patient's resistance to the therapy process is a major focus
6. Symptoms and behaviours serve multiple functions and are determined by complex, often unconscious forces
7. A psychodynamic therapist assists the patient in achieving a sense of authenticity and uniqueness

## Chapter Digest

### Ch 1: Key Concepts (pp. 1-20)

Gabbard opens with the conceptual DNA of LTPP. The field evolved from Freud's cathartic abreaction to a modern integrative model synthesising ego psychology, object relations, self psychology, and attachment theory. The defining epistemological move of late-20th-century psychoanalysis is the shift from one-person (intrapsychic) to two-person (relational/intersubjective) psychology.

**Unconscious mental functioning** is the bedrock. Gabbard links this to neuroscience: implicit procedural memory stores relational patterns operating outside awareness. **Transference** is defined both classically (displacement of past feelings) and constructivistically (co-created relational field). **Countertransference** is repositioned from contaminant to primary data source. The totalist view holds that the therapist's emotional response reveals the patient's internal world, especially via projective identification.

The chapter introduces **psychic determinism** (behaviours are overdetermined by unconscious forces) and the patient's **unique subjectivity** (Winnicott's true self vs. false self). The evidence base is briefly reviewed: therapeutic alliance accounts for 21% of outcome variance (Krupnick et al. 1996), more than any specific technique.

### Ch 2: Assessment, Indications, and Formulation (pp. 21-40)

Assessment requires psychodynamic interviewing rather than standard medical interviewing. The clinician evaluates: (1) whether clinical symptoms will respond to LTPP, and (2) whether the patient has psychological characteristics suited to it.

**Four components of personality** (Gabbard 2001): biologically based temperament, internal representations of self-other linked by affect, characteristic defence mechanisms, and related cognitive style.

**Reflective function and mentalization** (Fonagy): the developmental acquisition permitting response to others' beliefs, feelings, and intentions. Secure attachment promotes mentalization; insecure attachment (especially with trauma) impairs it. Children before age 3 operate in psychic equivalence mode (no distinction between perception and reality); between 3-6, pretend mode integrates with psychic equivalence if attachment is secure.

**Level of personality organisation** (neurotic vs. borderline): neurotic patients have integrated identity, high-level defences, intact reality testing, conflict-based pathology, and intact reflective function. Borderline patients have identity diffusion, primitive defences, ego weaknesses, significant deficits alongside conflicts, and impaired reflective function.

**Suitability for exploratory therapy:** strong motivation, significant suffering, good frustration tolerance, psychological-mindedness, capacity for analogy/metaphor.

**Indications:** Cluster C personality disorders, narcissistic/borderline/histrionic PD (with supportive elements), generalised anxiety, treatment-resistant panic/social phobia/PTSD, anorexia nervosa, dysthymia/depression with characterological substrate.

**Contraindications:** OCD symptoms directly (use BT + SSRI), active substance abuse, antisocial PD without guilt/remorse.

**Psychodynamic formulation** has three components (Sperry et al. 1992): (1) brief description of clinical picture + stressors, (2) biopsychosocial explanatory hypotheses, (3) prediction about treatment course and prognosis.

### Ch 3: The Nuts and Bolts of Psychotherapy: Getting Started (pp. 41-58)

The chapter covers practical infrastructure: establishing rapport, the therapeutic alliance, the frame, fees, gifts, confidentiality, and session management.

**Therapeutic alliance** is the critical factor in outcome across all therapies. Krupnick et al. (1996) found it accounted for 21% of outcome variance in the NIMH TDCRP across all four arms (IPT, CBT, medication + CM, placebo + CM).

**The therapeutic frame** consists of: location, duration, fee, absence of physical contact (handshake excepted), limited self-disclosure, confidentiality, and absence of dual relationships. The frame creates a safe "play space" (cf. Winnicott's holding environment). **Boundary crossings** (benign, isolated, minor, discussable) must be distinguished from **boundary violations** (exploitative, repetitive, egregious, not discussable).

**The Tiffany's approach:** for ambivalent patients, the therapist is better served by not being too eager, recognising the patient's reservations, and suggesting they think about it. Paradoxically, this often increases motivation.

**Seating arrangement:** 45-degree angle recommended rather than face-to-face, reducing obligatory eye contact.

**Rule of thumb:** "When in doubt, be human."

**Fees:** Winnicott noted both love and hate are inherent in the therapeutic relationship. The fee is the ever-present reminder that this is not friendship, family, or romance. Beginning therapists must not avoid collecting fees; non-collection conveys the work is worthless.

### Ch 4: Therapeutic Interventions: What Does the Therapist Say and Do? (pp. 59-76)

**Neutrality, anonymity, and abstinence** are principles of questionable value in their extreme forms. Contemporary view: the therapist exercises **restraint** while acknowledging that personal subjectivity inevitably enters. Anonymity is impossible (office decor, facial expressions, timing of comments all disclose). Abstinence means avoiding excessive gratification of transference wishes, not total deprivation.

**The expressive-supportive continuum of interventions:**

| Most Expressive | | | | Most Supportive |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Interpretation | Observation | Confrontation | Clarification | Encouragement to elaborate |
| | | | Empathic validation | Psychoeducation, advice, praise |

**Interpretation:** making conscious what was previously unconscious, or pointing out unrecognised connections. Delivered tentatively ("I wonder if..."). Unconscious wishes, fantasies, beliefs, defences are all targets. Transference interpretation connects the relationship with the therapist to past relationships and extratransference relationships.

**Key principle:** Interpret transference when it becomes a resistance. If positive transference facilitates work, don't fix what isn't broken. Formulate the interpretation and think about it four times before verbalising.

**Transference interpretation = high-risk, high-gain** with borderline patients (Gabbard et al. 1994). Requires a holding environment of empathic validation first.

**Gender constellation matters:** real characteristics of the therapist trigger specific neural networks of representations. Four dyads (M-F, F-M, M-M, F-F) produce different transference patterns. Gender and sexual orientation become fluid as therapy deepens.

### Ch 5: Goals and Therapeutic Action (pp. 77-98)

No single path of therapeutic change applies to all patients. The either/or polarisation of insight vs. relationship has given way to recognition that both operate synergistically.

**Goals of LTPP (multiple, theory-dependent):**
- Resolution of conflict (ego psychology)
- Search for truth / authenticity (Winnicott: true self vs. false self)
- Improved capacity to seek appropriate selfobjects (self psychology)
- Improved relationships through understanding internal object relations
- Generation of meaning within the therapeutic dialogue
- Improved reflective functioning / mentalization

**Blatt's distinction** (Menninger Psychotherapy Research Project reanalysis):

| | Introjective patients | Anaclitic patients |
|---|---|---|
| Motivation | Self-development primary | Relationships primary |
| Defences | Intellectualisation, reaction formation, rationalisation | Denial, disavowal, displacement, repression |
| Mode of change | Insight through interpretation | Therapeutic relationship itself |

**Neuroscience of change:** Change involves (1) weakening links between nodes of maladaptive associational networks, and (2) creating/strengthening new adaptive associative linkages. Old networks are not obliterated; they are relatively deactivated while new ones gain activation. Both conscious and unconscious networks need alteration.

**Three classes of intervention for change:**
1. **Fostering insight:** free association, interpretation, observation from outside perspective
2. **Therapeutic relationship:** experiencing a different relationship, internalisation of therapist's functions and attitudes, implicit relational knowing (Lyons-Ruth), identification of recurrent transference-countertransference themes
3. **Secondary strategies:** suggestion, confrontation of dysfunctional beliefs, addressing conscious problem-solving, exposure (phobic patients must face feared situations), judicious self-disclosure, affirmation, facilitative techniques

### Ch 6: Working With Resistance (pp. 99-116)

"Resistance is the daily bread and butter work of the dynamic therapist." Patients are ambivalent about change. Their internal equilibrium, however painful, is threatened by therapy. Resistance manifests as: lateness, silence, superficial talk, forgetting to pay, missing sessions, lack of continuity between sessions.

**Key reframe:** resistance is not an obstacle to be removed but a revelation of internal object relationships. The therapist enters more deeply into the resistance rather than clearing it away.

**Acting out vs. acting in:** Acting out = behaviour outside sessions (displacement of transference onto other figures). Acting in = nonverbal behaviour within sessions with conscious/unconscious meaning (three features: somatic musculature, intentionality, directly observable). Both are informative, not pejorative.

**Kohut's view:** resistances are activities undertaken "in the service of psychological survival" — we should empathise with and respect the patient's need for defences.

**Resistance to awareness of transference:** some narcissistically organised patients deny the therapist's significance. The apparent absence of transference IS the transference.

**Exit lines:** comments at the door carry heightened transference. They represent a compromise between saying and not saying. The therapist must bring these up at the next session because the patient will "forget."

**Characterological resistances:** embedded in lifelong defences and object relations. Track the emotional state; abrupt topic shifts signal defensive flight from affect. Obsessive-compulsive patients control sessions through overinclusiveness and isolation of affect.

**Flight into health:** premature cure claims. Rule of thumb: the first time a patient brings up termination, it probably serves a resistance function.

### Ch 7: Use of Dreams and Fantasies in Dynamic Psychotherapy (pp. 117-130)

**Dreams:** Freud's "royal road to the unconscious" remains valid. Neuroscience findings cannot disprove psychodynamic value of dreams (Reiser 2001).

**Dream mechanisms:** condensation (multiple meanings in one image), displacement (intensity diverted to less threatening figure), symbolic representation (concrete image for complex feelings; synecdoche = part for whole), secondary revision (ego organises primitive elements into coherent narrative).

**Day residue:** event from the day appears in disguised form. Always look for it collaboratively.

**Technique:** always ask "What comes to mind?" before interpreting. Resist being a soothsayer. The dreamer's associations are essential.

**Fantasy:** ubiquitous, adaptive. Daydreams/reveries are conscious subsets. Unconscious fantasies emerge through therapeutic work. **The golden fantasy** (Smith 1977): wish for all needs met in a relationship hallowed by perfection. This is a powerful resistance — patients cling to it, feeling that losing it means existential despair.

**Core conflictual relationship theme** (Luborsky 1984): a wish, a fantasised response from another, and a response from the self. Recurrent across relationships.

**Erotic fantasy:** the "sex print" (Person 1980) is idiosyncratic, often at odds with the person's usual style. Sexual fantasies are windows into object relations, conflicts, and traumas. Handle with tact; therapists maintain a matter-of-fact attitude.

**Suicide fantasies:** always explore what happens after. Revenge fantasies, reunion fantasies, perfectionistic self-expectations — laying these bare makes alternatives visible.

### Ch 8: Identifying and Working With Countertransference (pp. 131-152)

**Countertransference is ubiquitous and should not be regarded pejoratively.** The prevailing view: CT feelings are a joint creation with contributions from both patient and therapist.

**Projective identification (3 steps):**
1. Patient unconsciously projects a self or object representation into the therapist
2. Patient exerts interpersonal pressure ("nudging") that transforms the therapist into an approximation of the projected representation
3. (In therapy) therapist contains, tolerates, and processes the projection; modified contents are reintrojected by the patient

**Concordant CT** (Racker 1968): therapist identifies with patient's projected self (closely related to empathy).
**Complementary CT:** therapist identifies with patient's projected object (e.g. abuser).

**Countertransference enactment** (American ego psychology): "enactments occur when an attempt to actualize the transference fantasy elicits a countertransference reaction" (Chused 1991). Same process as projective identification, different theoretical lineage.

**Key marker:** "I'm not myself" — abnormally angry, unusually forgiving, atypically bored, excessively voyeuristic.

**Management strategies:**
1. **Tolerate it** — Winnicott: patient must destroy the therapist, and the therapist must survive. Durability is mutative.
2. **Use it to inform interpretation** — what is being externalised reveals the patient's internal world. But timing matters: "Strike while the iron is cold" (Pine 1986) — postpone interpretation until both parties can enter analytic space.
3. **Judicious self-disclosure** — sharing the dilemma rather than direct feelings is often safest. Never disclose sexual feelings.

**Varieties:** rescue fantasies (pervasive, often unrecognised), boredom/sleepiness (explore what the patient gets out of inducing this; "to put to sleep" = "to kill" in veterinary usage), erotic CT (often manifests as rescue fantasies; if unmanageable, refer), incapacitating CT (therapist feels unable to think; delay responding, use self-disclosure about the dilemma).

### Ch 9: Working Through and Termination (pp. 153-171)

**Working through** = analysing resistances "again and again, patiently, through a seemingly endless series of repetitions, permutations, combinations, and variations" (Shafer 1983). The patient develops a sense of being the agent/author of their own life.

**Triangle of insight** (Menninger 1958): transference relationship / current extratransference relationships / past relationships. The therapist identifies recurrent patterns across all three.

**Neuroscience frame:** old neural networks involving maladaptive self-other representations are gradually weakened; new networks involving different relatedness are strengthened. This takes time.

**First-person to third-person shift** (Goldberg 1999): therapists empathically validate the patient's "I" experience while bringing their outside perspective. "The unconscious is experienced not as a first-person phenomenon but as something alien and apart."

**Promoting mentalization in borderline patients:** ask patient to elaborate fantasies about therapist's mind; identify and focus on patient's mental states; help patient see that affect colours perception.

**Negative therapeutic reaction:** patient gets worse with accurate, helpful interpretations. Reasons: envy of therapist's insight, revenge against parental figures, need to defeat therapist's expectations. Some patients change only after termination to deprive the therapist of gratification.

**Impasse management:** investigate whether the patient's transference and therapist's CT have recreated the most tenaciously defended internal object relationship. Monitor furor therapeuticus.

**Varieties of termination:**
1. Mutual agreement based on goal achievement
2. Preplanned (time-limited design)
3. Forced (therapist graduates, patient relocates, payer discontinues)
4. Unilateral by patient (feeling no value)
5. Unilateral by therapist (non-compliance, non-payment, unmanageable CT)
6. Failed attempt leading to "therapeutic lifer" status
7. End-setting as a therapeutic strategy

**Termination work:** symptoms often return (protest against loss). Memories of prior abandonments surface. "The door is always open" for mutual terminations. Boundaries may become more permeable — therapists must be vigilant. Fewer than 20% of patients in community mental health centres have a mutually negotiated termination (Beck et al. 1987).

### Ch 10: Use of Supervision (pp. 173-186)

Therapy occurs in a dyad but is learned in a triad (patient-therapist-supervisor). Trainees internalise the supervisor as a guiding representation. Process notes are preferred over video/audio (preserving the natural habitat of therapy). Supervisees should report general themes + struggles, then illustrate with specific session material.

**Supervisory alliance** parallels therapeutic alliance. Supervisors must create safety for disclosure. Two axioms: (1) If you can't share it with a supervisor, you may be descending the slippery slope. (2) What you most want to avoid sharing is probably the most important thing to discuss.

**Parallel process** (Doehrman 1976): the supervisee enacts with the supervisor what the patient enacts with the supervisee. When the supervisor experiences feelings resembling the therapist's CT, this is diagnostically informative.

**Boundaries in supervision:** the supervisor does NOT interpret the childhood origins of the supervisee's CT. Focus on what the patient is inducing (broad CT) rather than the therapist's personal history.

## Master Concept Index

### Defence Mechanisms (Vaillant Hierarchy: Primitive → Neurotic → Mature)

**Primitive defences:**
| Defence | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Splitting | Compartmentalising self/other into all-good and all-bad; bland denial of contradictions |
| Projective identification | Projecting self/object representation into another + exerting interpersonal pressure to make them conform |
| Projection | Perceiving unacceptable inner impulses as outside the self (target not changed, unlike PI) |
| Denial | Avoiding awareness of external reality by disregarding sensory data |
| Dissociation | Disrupting identity, memory, consciousness, or perception to retain illusion of control |
| Idealisation | Attributing perfect qualities to others to avoid anxiety or negative feelings |
| Acting out | Enacting unconscious wish/fantasy impulsively to avoid painful affect |
| Somatisation | Converting emotional pain into physical symptoms |
| Regression | Returning to earlier developmental phase to avoid current tensions |
| Schizoid fantasy | Retreating into private internal world to avoid interpersonal anxiety |

**Higher-level (neurotic) defences:**
| Defence | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Introjection | Internalising aspects of a significant person to deal with loss or gain illusion of control |
| Identification | Internalising qualities of another by becoming like them (experienced as part of self) |
| Displacement | Shifting feelings from one idea/object to a resembling one |
| Intellectualisation | Excessive abstract ideation to avoid difficult feelings |
| Isolation of affect | Separating idea from its associated affect state |
| Rationalisation | Justifying unacceptable attitudes/beliefs to make them tolerable |
| Sexualisation | Endowing object/behaviour with sexual significance to ward off anxiety or transform negative into exciting |
| Reaction formation | Transforming unacceptable wish into its opposite |
| Repression | Expelling unacceptable ideas from consciousness (differs from denial: internal vs. external) |
| Undoing | Negating aggressive/sexual/shameful implications by elaborating, clarifying, or doing the opposite |

**Mature defences:**
| Defence | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Humour | Finding comic elements in difficult situations; allows distance and objectivity |
| Suppression | Consciously deciding not to attend to a feeling or impulse (differs from repression: conscious) |
| Asceticism | Eliminating pleasurable aspects due to internal conflicts; can serve spiritual goals |
| Altruism | Committing to others' needs over one's own; can serve narcissistic problems or great achievements |
| Anticipation | Delaying gratification by planning for future accomplishments |
| Sublimation | Transforming socially objectionable aims into socially acceptable ones |

### Transference Types and Concepts

- **Classical (Freud):** "stereotype plate" — libidinal desires transferred onto analyst
- **Object relations / Kleinian:** projective identification — patient nudges therapist into embodying an internal object
- **Self psychological (Kohut/Stolorow):** bidimensional — repetitive aspect + reparative aspect (seeking new healing experience)
- **Constructivist (Hoffman):** therapist's actual behaviour always influences patient's experience; transference = mixture of real characteristics + past figures
- **Selfobject transference:** therapist as completion of patient's self (mirroring, idealising, twinship)
- **Erotic transference:** sexual/romantic feelings toward therapist; often conceals aggression underneath
- **Erotised transference:** more fixed, demanding, resistant to interpretation; patient insists on gratification
- **Negative transference:** hostility, anger, contempt; interpret when it becomes a resistance
- **Transference resistance:** opposition to therapy based on fantasies about how therapist views patient

### Levels of Psychodynamic Therapy (Supportive → Expressive)

| Feature | Supportive end | Expressive end |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Frequency | Once weekly or less | Two to three times weekly |
| Focus | Ego strengthening, psychoeducation, reality testing | Transference, unconscious conflict, interpretation |
| Defences | Bolstered, not challenged | Explored, interpreted |
| Patient organisation | Borderline level, ego weaknesses | Neurotic level, good ego strengths |
| Interventions | Advice, praise, empathic validation, psychoeducation | Interpretation, confrontation, observation |
| Goal | Improved functioning, stabilisation | Structural personality change, insight |

### Level of Personality Organisation (Kernberg)

| Domain | Neurotic Level | Borderline Level |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Superego | Well-integrated but punitive | Minimal integration; guilt fluctuates |
| Defences | High-level (repression, reaction formation, intellectualisation) | Primitive (splitting, PI, idealisation, devaluation) |
| Identity | Stable; ambivalently regarded whole objects; triangular conflict | Diffuse; partial objects split into all-good/all-bad |
| Ego | Good impulse control, intact judgment, consistent reality testing | Impulsivity, impaired judgment, brief lapses in reality testing |
| Pathology | Conflict-based | Significant deficits alongside conflicts |
| Reflective function | Intact | Impaired |

## Clinical Techniques Catalogue

### 1. Interpretation
Making patients aware of things outside their awareness; explaining motives and meanings. Deliver tentatively ("I wonder if..."). Targets: wishes, fantasies, beliefs, defences, conflicts, transference, relational patterns. Formulate silently four times before speaking. Postpone if not close to patient's awareness.

### 2. Observation
Stops short of explanation. Notes behaviour, sequence, affect flash, or pattern. "You teared up when I asked about your sister." Leaves meaning for patient to discover.

### 3. Confrontation
Draws attention to something being avoided. Can be gentle and empathic, not only aggressive. Includes limit-setting with borderline patients. Example: "I noticed you haven't paid your bill for 3 months."

### 4. Clarification
Brings clarity to vague, diffuse, or disconnected material. Checks therapist's understanding. Summarises and repackages. "If I understand correctly..."

### 5. Empathic Validation
Immersion in patient's internal state. "I can appreciate why you would feel horrible." Especially important early in therapy for alliance. **Affirmative interventions** (Killingmo 1995): remove doubt about experience of reality, re-establish identity. Essential for trauma survivors whose feelings were invalidated.

### 6. Encouragement to Elaborate
"Can you tell me more about that?" Also used when patient falls silent: "I'm curious about what made you stop."

### 7. Free Association
Patient says whatever comes to mind. Reveals defences in action, maps implicit associational networks. More characteristic of psychoanalysis than psychotherapy, but adapted for LTPP.

### 8. Transference Analysis
Monitor transference fluctuations and multiple transferences (father, mother, sibling, child). Interpret when it becomes resistance. Use extratransference interpretations when patient finds in-session work too uncomfortable. Triangle of insight: transference / current relationships / past relationships.

### 9. Countertransference Management
Tolerate, use to inform interpretation, or employ judicious self-disclosure. Process between sessions. Containment = going from thinking the patient's thoughts to thinking one's own. Dual consciousness: enacting something while observing it happening.

### 10. Working With Resistance
Enter more deeply into the resistance rather than clearing it away. Invite curiosity. Silence has multiple meanings — sit with it if gentle enquiry fails. Track emotional states for defensive shifts. One must grow comfortable with resistance.

### 11. Dream Work
Ask "What comes to mind?" before interpreting. Look for day residue. Identify condensation, displacement, symbolic representation, secondary revision. Dreams can themselves serve as resistance (flooding sessions with dream material).

### 12. Working With Fantasy
Explore expectations and disappointments to access fantasy. Golden fantasy = wish for perfect caretaking. Identify core conflictual relationship theme: wish + fantasised response + self response. Maintain tact with erotic material. Examine suicide fantasies (revenge, reunion, perfectionistic).

### 13. Promoting Mentalization
Ask patient to elaborate fantasies about therapist's mind. Identify and focus on patient's mental states. Help patient see affect colours perception. "What do you think I might have been feeling while waiting for you?"

### 14. Managing Impasse
Investigate whether transference-CT has recreated the most tenaciously defended internal object relationship. Monitor furor therapeuticus. Negative therapeutic reaction: patient worsens with accurate help (envy, revenge, need to defeat). Some patients change only after termination.

## Disorder-Specific Applications

### Depression
- Dysthymia and major depressive disorder often require LTPP combined with antidepressant medication
- Blatt's introjective vs. anaclitic distinction shapes intervention strategy
- Depressive patients often ward off positive self-representations; exposure model useful
- Conscious self-berating and rumination addressed alongside unconscious networks
- Distinguish introspection (curious, future-oriented) from rumination (regretful, past-oriented)

### Personality Disorders
- **Cluster C** (avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive, self-defeating, hysterical): best evidence for LTPP; neurotically organised; respond to exploratory approach
- **Cluster B** (borderline, narcissistic, histrionic): require extended LTPP with supportive elements; supportive interventions address self-structure deficits and impaired mentalization
- **Borderline PD:** Bateman and Fonagy (1999) RCT — mentalization-based treatment in partial hospital showed significant improvement in depression, social functioning, hospitalisation, self-harm at 18 months, with continued improvement at 18-month follow-up
- **Antisocial PD:** generally unresponsive unless comorbid MDD or capacity for remorse
- Transference interpretation is high-risk, high-gain with borderline patients
- Splitting, projective identification, idealisation/devaluation are the clinical hallmarks

### Anxiety Disorders
- Generalised anxiety disorder: LTPP helps patients grasp reasons for anxiety and tolerate it
- Panic disorder, social phobia, PTSD: when brief treatments fail, LTPP explores resistances to change and dynamic origins
- Exposure component is implicit in LTPP (phobic patients must face feared situations for progress)
- Signal anxiety concept is directly relevant to understanding symptom formation

### Other Conditions
- **Eating disorders:** Anorexia nervosa may need extended LTPP (Dare 2001) as part of multimodal treatment
- **Schizophrenia:** Not a primary indication for LTPP; primarily needs pharmacotherapy, supportive therapy, cognitive remediation. However, psychodynamic understanding helps with treatment resistance and CT difficulties
- **Bipolar disorder:** Treatable with LTPP when euthymic, always in conjunction with medication

## Exam-Ready Extracts

> **EXAM PEARL 1:** The therapeutic alliance accounts for ~21% of outcome variance — greater than any specific technique — across all therapy modalities including pharmacotherapy (Krupnick et al. 1996, NIMH TDCRP).

> **EXAM PEARL 2:** Long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy is defined as therapy lasting >24 sessions or >6 months (Gabbard's operational definition).

> **EXAM PEARL 3:** Contraindications for LTPP: OCD symptoms directly (use BT + SSRI), active substance abuse, antisocial PD without remorse. These are NOT suitable for exploratory psychodynamic work as primary treatment.

> **EXAM PEARL 4:** Projective identification has three steps: (1) unconscious projection of self/object representation, (2) interpersonal pressure to make target identify with projection, (3) in therapy — therapist contains, processes, and returns modified contents for reintrojection.

> **EXAM PEARL 5:** Neurotic vs. borderline personality organisation determines the balance of expressive vs. supportive technique. Neurotic = exploratory emphasis. Borderline = supportive elements needed alongside insight.

> **EXAM PEARL 6:** Boundary crossings (benign, isolated, discussable, no harm) are distinguished from boundary violations (exploitative, repetitive, not discussable, harmful). Crossings can be therapeutically useful; violations always damage.

> **EXAM PEARL 7:** Transference interpretation is high-risk, high-gain with borderline patients (Gabbard et al. 1994). Must be preceded by empathic validation to create a holding environment. Monitor alliance fluctuations in response.

> **EXAM PEARL 8:** The negative therapeutic reaction: patient deteriorates in response to accurate, helpful interpretations. Causes include envy, revenge fantasies against parental figures, need to defeat the therapist. Some patients improve only after termination.

> **EXAM PEARL 9:** Blatt's introjective patients respond to insight/interpretation; anaclitic patients respond to the therapeutic relationship itself. Different patients use different aspects of treatment.

> **EXAM PEARL 10:** "Strike while the iron is cold" (Pine 1986): postpone interpretation originating in countertransference until the patient can enter analytic (potential) space and recognise aspects of themselves in the therapist.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 1:** When a patient falls silent, resist the urge to fill the gap. Sit quietly, then invite curiosity: "I noticed you stopped right after mentioning your mother. Any thoughts about that?" Enter the resistance rather than attacking it.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 2:** Exit lines (comments at the door) are among the most important communications of the hour. The patient is ambivalent — delivering a parting shot is a compromise between saying and not saying. Always bring it up next session.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 3:** The bored or sleepy therapist should not self-flagellate or tank up on caffeine. Ask: "What is this particular patient on this particular day getting out of inducing this state?" Make the boring patient a fascinating subject of study.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 4:** When a patient asks "Are you angry with me?" and the therapist's face is red, jaw clenched, and veins bulging — acknowledge it. "I think you're picking up on something. I'd like to explore that with you since I'd rather help you than get irritated." Denial creates two problems: an angry therapist and a dishonest one.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 5:** For ambivalent patients, use the "Tiffany's approach" — don't hard-sell therapy. Say: "I can tell you have real reservations. You may want to think about it awhile. It's a long and at times difficult process." This paradoxically increases motivation.

> **CLINICAL ANCHOR 6:** Never disclose sexual countertransference. If directly asked, share the dilemma instead: "If I say no, you may feel devastated. If I say yes, you may feel this isn't safe. So I think it's best not to answer."
