## 1. Who We're Talking To

Three people. Every sentence should work for at least one of them.

**The Stuck Twentysomething** (Wilfred's side)
20s-30s. Probably has ADHD, mood issues, or substance concerns they haven't named yet. Smart, scrolls at 2 AM, Googles "why can't I focus." Needs to feel seen before they'll trust you.

**The Overwhelmed Woman** (Niharika's side)
Carrying everything for everyone. Relationships, children, family. Quietly unravelling. Needs permission to put something down.

**The Community Seeker**
Wants growth, not just symptom relief. Interested in workshops, group work, meaning-making. Looking for a practice that feels like a place, not a transaction.

## 2. The Voice

**Warm-clinical hybrid.** The voice of a brilliant clinician who also happens to be a real human being.

- Lead with warmth (hero sections, first impressions, CTAs)
- Anchor with clinical credibility (credentials, evidence, medication information)
- Sprinkle with poetic moments (occasional headline, a well-placed metaphor)
- Never sacrifice clarity for any register

### What we sound like
- A therapist who speaks plainly
- A friend who happens to have a medical degree
- Someone who's been on the other side and came back sharper

### What we don't sound like
- A hospital website
- A wellness influencer
- A textbook
- A corporate brochure

## 3. The Architecture

Every piece of Weave content — page, post, email, caption — follows this sequence:

### Mirror → Validate → Normalize → Reframe → Invite → Affirm

1. **Mirror** — Describe their experience so accurately they feel seen. Use somatic, sensory language. "That tightness in your chest when you walk into the room..."
2. **Validate** — Tell them their response makes sense. "Of course you feel that way."
3. **Normalize** — Widen the frame. "Many people experience this."
4. **Reframe** — Shift from deficit to adaptation. "You're not broken — you learned to survive."
5. **Invite** — Offer one tiny next step. Not "commit to therapy." Just "tell us what's on your mind."
6. **Affirm agency** — "You get to decide. Whenever you're ready."

Not every piece hits all six. But the sequence is sacred — never skip validation to get to the invite.

## 4. Reading Level & Structure

| Rule | Target |
|------|--------|
| Reading level | Grade 6 Flesch-Kincaid |
| Sentence length | 15-20 words average |
| Paragraph length | 3-4 sentences max, one idea each |
| Voice | Active, always |
| Person | "You" (default), "We" (for shared experience), "I" (for doctor-specific) |
| CTA per section | One. Clear. Soft. |

### Structural rules
- One idea per sentence
- Short sentences carry emotional truth; longer sentences carry context
- Use headers generously — an ADHD reader scans before they read
- White space is not wasted space — it's breathing room
- Bold key phrases for scanners
- Lists over paragraphs when presenting options

## 6. Words We Never Use

| Never | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| Patient | Person, you, someone |
| Treatment | Care, support, help, tools |
| Disorder (in headlines) | Experience, pattern, challenge |
| Symptoms | Signs, what you're noticing, what's been happening |
| Diagnosis (as a threat) | Understanding, making sense of |
| Mental illness | Mental health, emotional health |
| Session (for resistant audiences) | Conversation, meeting, check-in |
| You need help | You deserve support |
| You should | You might consider, what if |
| Normal / abnormal | Common, makes sense |
| Coping mechanism | Way of getting through, what helped you survive |
| Dysfunction | Difficulty, challenge, stuck place |
| Compliance | Working together, your choice |
| Relapse | Setback, difficult stretch |
| Fix, cure, repair | Support, build, grow, understand |
| Broken, damaged | Adapted, learned to survive |

### Absolute bans (never, in any context)
- Crazy, insane, psycho, pagal
- Clean / dirty (for substance use)
- Addict, substance abuser
- Weakness (near help-seeking)
- "You need to" / "You must" / "You have to"
- Fear-based urgency ("Don't let your life fall apart")
- Outcome promises ("We'll cure your depression")
- Superiority claims ("Best psychiatrist in Mumbai")

## 7. The Golden Zone

Clinical accuracy without clinical language. Here's how:

### Name it without diagnosing it
"When you find yourself replaying conversations for hours, questioning everything you said" > "rumination, a common feature of GAD"

### Teach the mechanism without the jargon
"When something reminds your body of an old danger, it responds as if the danger is here now — even when you're safe" > "trauma-related hyperarousal mediated by amygdala-prefrontal dysfunction"

### Validate before you educate
"It makes sense that you shut down in conflict — your nervous system learned early that shutting down kept you safe"

### Use the body as an entry point
"You might notice your chest gets tight" > "you may experience anxiety"

### Hold complexity without confusion
"This is hard, and you can do hard things" > "Recovery is a long and difficult process requiring sustained commitment"

### Offer a next step that's laughably small
"Notice what happens in your body the next time you feel that way" > "Find a therapist and commit to weekly sessions"

## 8. India-Specific Rules

### Stigma is collective, not individual
In India, the barrier isn't "I'm not crazy" — it's "log kya kahenge." Address the family shame, not just personal shame.

### The family frame
"Taking care of your mental health makes you a better parent / spouse / son / daughter." In collectivist cultures, self-care must be framed as other-care.

### Credentialing matters
Indian audiences trust institutional affiliation. Name your colleges, research, registrations. The market is full of unregulated practitioners — differentiate through credentials.

### Cost transparency reduces stigma
Show your prices. No "call for pricing." Transparency signals this is a practical service, not a luxury indulgence.

### Address medication fears directly
Nobody else does. The top fears: personality change, dependence, marriage prospects, Ayurvedic interactions. Name them, answer them honestly.

### Address confidentiality directly
"Will my parents find out?" is the #1 barrier for 18-25 year olds. Answer it plainly, prominently, early.

### WhatsApp is the channel
Content must be screenshot-friendly, shareable without context. Short, shareable text blocks travel on WhatsApp.

### Spiritual framing is an ally
"Meditation has been part of our culture for thousands of years — therapy is the same idea with modern tools." Work with Indian philosophical frameworks, not against them.

## 9. CTA Rules

### The soft cascade (matching stages of change)
- Pre-contemplation: "Learn more" / "Keep reading"
- Contemplation: "See what to expect" / "Read about how we work"
- Preparation: "Tell us what's on your mind"
- Action: "Book a consultation" / "Message us on WhatsApp"

### CTA phrasing
- First person converts 25% better: "I'm ready to start" > "Book now"
- Permission-slip framing: "You don't need to have it figured out"
- Always offer dual CTA: form + WhatsApp (lower barrier)
- Never use urgency: no "Limited spots!" or "Don't wait!"

## 13. NMC Compliance Checklist

Before publishing any content, verify:

- [ ] No testimonials or patient stories (even anonymized, NMC prohibits)
- [ ] No outcome promises ("We'll cure your..." / "90% success rate")
- [ ] No superiority claims ("Best psychiatrist" / "Leading practice")
- [ ] No comparative claims against other providers
- [ ] No fear-based marketing
- [ ] No self-promotion through patient photographs
- [ ] Credentials accurately stated
- [ ] Registration numbers displayed where required
- [ ] Crisis helplines included on clinical content pages

---

## 14. The Decision Filter

Run every piece of copy through these 8 questions:

1. Would someone in distress feel safe reading this?
2. Does it validate before it educates?
3. Is the reader positioned as capable, not broken?
4. Can a 15-year-old understand every sentence?
5. Does it offer one clear, small next step?
6. Does it respect the reader's autonomy?
7. Is it free of banned words and clinical jargon in headlines?
8. Does it comply with NMC advertising guidelines?

If any answer is no, rewrite before publishing.
